# **Strategic Redesign and UI/UX Research Report: CI Institute of Nursing CNA Recertification MVP**

## **Executive Summary**

The transition of the CI Institute of Nursing Certified Nursing Assistant (CNA) Recertification Theory and Clinical Support application from a rudimentary task-based interface to a premium, compliance-aware digital learning experience requires a foundational shift in information architecture and visual design. Current iterations of healthcare Learning Management Systems (LMS) often fail by overwhelming users with unstructured task backlogs, resulting in elevated cognitive load and user fatigue, especially for adult learners and shift workers.1 This comprehensive research report establishes an expert-level UI/UX strategy to replace the existing task clutter with a guided, asynchronous learning pathway.  
The target design leverages a premium cinematic aesthetic—utilizing a dark charcoal base, deep burgundy accents, and antique gold highlights—to convey trust, professional prestige, and institutional authority.3 Crucially, the interface must strictly delineate between the Required Online Continuing Education (CE) Theory Pathway and the Optional Clinical Support Pathway. This separation ensures strict compliance with California Department of Public Health (CDPH) regulations, which mandate a maximum of 24 online CE hours per two-year renewal period out of a total 48 required hours.4 Any blending of these pathways risks severe regulatory exposure.  
To resolve the usability and compliance challenges of the current platform, this report outlines the top ten critical UI/UX redesign directives. First, the task backlog must be eradicated and replaced with a "Next Up" progressive disclosure model to reduce decision fatigue for shift-working learners.5 Second, the design must employ distinct visual containers to permanently separate gated CE theory from optional clinical support. Third, the platform must implement a "Gate Center" paradigm, abstracting complex backend compliance logic into a transparent user-facing timeline. Fourth, the execution of the dark mode must utilize off-black backgrounds (e.g., \#121212) rather than pure black to reduce eye strain, paired with accessible gold and burgundy accents.3 Fifth, any application of glassmorphism must be highly restricted, utilizing specific background blurs and semi-transparent overlays to meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2 Level AA standards.7  
Furthermore, the integration of active-time validation UI is paramount; the system must seamlessly incorporate non-intrusive interactive knowledge checks within the Lesson Player to actively validate attendance without disrupting the learning flow.9 For mobile users, a persistent, icon-driven bottom navigation bar must be deployed to optimize one-handed operation during clinical breaks.11 To protect institutional liability, the UI must embed unmissable, standardized visual blocks explicitly forbidding Protected Health Information (PHI) entry in all free-text and upload zones. Finally, compliance evidence must be presented in polished, defensible data rows rather than raw backend spreadsheets, and the standalone MVP must feature stakeholder-facing transitional UI elements that visually bridge the current components to future Moodle core modules.10

## **Current App UX Problem Diagnosis**

The existing application relies on a task-based interface that operates fundamentally as an administrative to-do list. In educational UI/UX, relying on a raw task list to deliver asynchronous theory presents severe pedagogical and usability risks. Cognitive load theory dictates that presenting an exhaustive list of modules, clinical checklists, and administrative gates simultaneously induces high mental strain.1 For healthcare workers experiencing physiological and cognitive fatigue post-shift 2, a dense task interface creates immediate psychological friction, actively deterring course engagement and reducing overall completion rates.  
Beyond cognitive fatigue, the current flat architecture fails to provide essential spatial and sequential mapping. A raw task list lacks the visual hierarchy necessary to communicate the pedagogical structure of the course. Learners cannot distinguish at a glance between a minor five-minute knowledge check and a critical two-hour module. The absence of this hierarchy directly violates Donald Norman’s principles of Visibility and Mapping, which assert that the relationship between interface elements and their underlying importance must be immediately clear to the user.14  
Most critically, the current interface presents severe compliance risks. Standard task lists inadvertently and visually equate required regulatory gates, such as signing a legal affidavit, with purely optional support tools like scenario coaching. The CDPH strictly regulates what constitutes mandatory California renewal credit, limiting online computer training to a maximum of 24 hours per renewal cycle.4 If a CDPH auditor or a user perceives that optional clinical support simulations are required for the certificate, the institution risks regulatory penalties for misrepresenting clinical hour credits. Therefore, the task interface must be entirely dismantled and replaced by a structured page-view system that enforces an impenetrable visual and structural wall between required CE modules and optional support tools.

## **Information Architecture Recommendation**

To resolve the task-list clutter, the application must adopt a structured Hub-and-Spoke architectural model embedded within a sequential learning journey. This architecture abstracts the complexity of the backend database into distinct, digestible page views. The current model forces the user to act as an administrator of their own curriculum, navigating a messy backlog. The new architecture shifts this burden to the system, transforming the user from a task manager into a guided consumer of knowledge.  
The central component of this architecture is the Dashboard, serving as the learner's command center and the primary hub routing the user to their next required action. From the Dashboard, users access the Modules view, which acts as the macro-level course catalog for the asynchronous theory. Drilling down, the Module Detail view serves as the micro-level chapter overview, bridging the gap between the catalog and the Lesson View. The Lesson View represents the execution layer, an immersive environment where theory is delivered, interactions are logged, and active time is validated.  
Parallel to the learning pathway runs the compliance and support architecture. The Gate Center functions as the compliance spoke, an abstracted view of backend requirements—such as identity verification, time minimums, and affidavits—mapped directly against certificate release. The Clinical Support Hub operates as a strictly walled-off sandbox for optional, non-credit practice, entirely decoupled from the certificate readiness logic. Finally, the Audit and Moodle Map views serve as administrative and stakeholder spokes, demonstrating compliance readiness and mapping the MVP to future-state enterprise architecture.

## **Page-by-Page UX Recommendations**

### **Course / Landing View**

The Course Landing page serves as the high-level introduction to the platform and acts as the primary stakeholder-facing entry point. The primary user goal is to understand the platform's value proposition, comprehend the structural difference between required and optional pathways, and initiate the learning experience without friction. The top information hierarchy must flow logically: Hero branding and title, followed immediately by the primary call-to-action ("Start Module 0"), transitioning into an explanation of the Required vs. Optional pathways, and concluding with a Certificate Status preview.  
The recommended layout features a cinematic hero section utilizing a dark charcoal background with deep burgundy overlay gradients and elegant serif typography. Below the fold, the layout must utilize two visually distinct columns or heavy cards. One card strictly defines the "12-Hour Required Online CE Pathway," while the other visually distinct card details the "Optional Clinical Support Pathway." Required components include the hero title, an unmissable "No PHI" foundational warning block, and a module progress preview strip. A common mistake to avoid is overcrowding this landing page with detailed task lists or blurring the line between CE credit and optional support, which could trigger CDPH compliance scrutiny.4 On desktop, this should resemble a high-end SaaS product landing page, while the mobile layout should stack these cards vertically, ensuring the primary CTA remains above the fold.

### **Dashboard View**

The Dashboard is the learner's operational command center. The primary user goal is to answer a singular question in under five seconds: "What exactly do I need to do right now, and how close am I to my certificate?".18 The information hierarchy must prioritize a personalized greeting, followed immediately by a prominent "Next Up" action card. Below this, the interface must display a locked Certificate Banner, the Required Theory CE Progress, and completely segregated Optional Clinical Support Progress.  
The layout should place the primary KPIs and the "Next Up" card in the top-left quadrant, aligning with Nielsen Norman Group eye-tracking research for dashboard consumption.14 The right column should feature a vertical "Certificate Path" timeline, visually indicating locked and unlocked gates in a sequential flow. The status states are critical; the Certificate Preview must be explicitly locked, utilizing a padlock icon and desaturated styling, until all backend criteria are met. For mobile users, the "Next Up" card must be stacked at the very top, immediately below the header. The global progress ring and secondary stats should be housed in a swipeable carousel to preserve vertical screen real estate and prevent scrolling fatigue.

### **Modules View**

The Modules view functions as the macroscopic course catalog for the asynchronous theory pathway. The primary user goal is to understand the full breadth of the 12-hour curriculum and track macro-progress across the required sections. The top information hierarchy flows from overall program progress down to individual module statuses and estimated durations.  
The layout should feature a structured list or grid of "Module Cards." Each card must display the module title (e.g., "M2: Resident Rights, Abuse Prevention, and Boundaries"), an estimated duration, and a linear progress bar. A left-hand mission or brand panel should be utilized to anchor the cinematic aesthetic. Module rows require distinct status badges, strictly adhering to states such as Not Started, In Progress, Needs Review, and Completed. A critical mistake to avoid is making this catalog look like a standard corporate LMS file directory. The UI must mimic premium consumer streaming platforms, utilizing rich typography, ample negative space, and clear progression markers.19 Mobile implementations should utilize full-width cards that span the viewport, ensuring touch targets for opening modules are generous.

### **Module Detail View**

The Module Detail view acts as the bridge between the high-level catalog and the execution of learning. The primary user goal is to review the specific learning objectives of a chosen module and initiate the correct sequential lesson. The hierarchy must present the Module Title and Overview first, followed by Learning Goals, and finally the sequential Lesson List.  
A split-pane view or a wide central column is recommended for the desktop layout. The top section contains an overview card detailing the estimated time commitment. Below, "Lesson Rows" are stacked sequentially. A prominent "Start Module" or "Continue" floating action button should serve as the primary CTA. Lessons must visually unlock sequentially if strict linear progression is mandated by the compliance model, using subdued opacities for locked future lessons. On mobile, the horizontal module selector tabs should be converted into a sticky dropdown menu to conserve space, allowing the user to seamlessly scroll through the lesson rows.

### **Lesson / Player View**

The Lesson View is the core execution environment where asynchronous theory is consumed and active time is rigorously validated. The primary user goal is to consume content, pass integrated knowledge checks, and accrue required time without experiencing technical friction. The hierarchy places the core lesson content in the center, navigation in a left sidebar, and time tracking/status in a right sidebar.  
A persistent three-column layout is ideal for desktop. The central reading pane must restrict line widths to roughly 60-75 characters to ensure maximum typographic readability.20 To prevent the "document dump" effect, pedagogical UI elements must be used extensively. Text should be chunked into "Key Rule" cards, "CNA Practice Example" callouts, and "Common Mistake" warning blocks. Crucially, to meet strict LMS validation protocols akin to FDA 21 CFR Part 11 10, the player must integrate "Scenario Check" interactive cards periodically. These are not graded exams, but active-time validation gates requiring the user to click or drag elements to prove active engagement, effectively neutralizing "click-and-walk-away" behaviors.9 For mobile interfaces, both sidebars must be hidden behind a bottom sheet or hamburger menu, allowing the central educational content to completely dominate the limited viewport.

When designing the architecture for asynchronous lesson delivery—especially for healthcare compliance—the focus must be on reducing cognitive load, maximizing engagement, and proving active participation. Based on current instructional design research and UI/UX patterns, here is a breakdown of how modern LMS platforms deliver asynchronous theory.

### **1\. Core Architecture: Scrolling vs. Slide-Based vs. Split-Screen**

* **Continuous Vertical Scrolling (Block-Based):** Modern asynchronous delivery heavily favors continuous vertical scrolling over traditional "click-next" slides `,` . This scroll-based architecture mimics how users naturally consume content on the web and mobile apps, making it highly intuitive and responsive to any screen size `. Tools like Articulate Rise utilize this block-based approach, which is ideal for breaking content into manageable, readable chunks` , \`\`.  
* **Slide-Based Presentation:** The traditional slide-based approach (similar to PowerPoint) is declining for general theory delivery because its fixed dimensions do not scale well on mobile devices `. Furthermore, static slides can cause a "split-attention effect," forcing learners to divide their focus between static graphics and text, which strains working memory` . Slide-based architecture is now best reserved strictly for highly complex, non-linear simulations or branching games `,` .  
* **Split-Screen Layouts:** For desktop and tablet views, a split-screen UI is a highly effective pattern for complex asynchronous learning `. For instance, you can pin a video or a clinical reading passage on the left side, while the right side houses a scrolling transcript, interactive knowledge checks, or decision points` , \`\`. This allows learners to reference the material while answering questions without losing their place or context.

### **2\. Interactive Components (Carousels, Cards, and Accordions)**

To prevent overwhelming users with walls of text, modern asynchronous lessons use progressive disclosure UI components embedded directly into the scrolling page:

* **Flip Cards:** Excellent for definitions, vocabulary, or revealing the correct outcome of a brief clinical scenario `,` .  
* **Carousels and Sliders:** Useful for displaying a sequence of events, steps in a clinical procedure, or featuring related imagery without taking up excessive vertical screen real estate `,` .  
* **Accordions and Tabs:** Used to chunk complex topics into expandable sections, allowing the learner to control the flow of information \`\`.

Requiring the user to physically click these elements not only paces the learning but also generates interaction data to prove active engagement to the system.

### **3\. Video Duration and Microlearning**

If you are incorporating video, strict adherence to microlearning principles is crucial, especially for fatigued shift workers.

* **The Drop-off Point:** Analytics show that viewer engagement begins to decline sharply after just 2 minutes, with a significant drop occurring between the 6 and 7-minute marks \`\`.  
* **Ideal Length:** For optimal retention, instructional videos should be kept between 3 to 5 minutes for single concepts `. If a topic is more complex, the absolute maximum duration should be 6 to 9 minutes to maximize learning gains while preventing inattention` , \`\`.  
* Any topic requiring more than 10 minutes of video should be split into multiple shorter modules interspersed with interactive knowledge checks \`\`.

### **4\. Technical Architecture for Active-Time Compliance**

Because California CDPH regulations require strict tracking of time, your lesson player architecture cannot simply track "wall-clock" time (e.g., a user leaving a browser tab open and walking away).

* **Heartbeat Signals:** The LMS player should be engineered to send background "heartbeat" signals (e.g., every 30 seconds) to the server to verify that the user is actively scrolling, clicking, or watching a video \`\`.  
* **Idle Timeout Popups:** If the system detects inactivity, the UI should trigger a modal popup (e.g., a 60-second countdown warning) asking the user to confirm they are still present. If they do not click to continue, the system should automatically log them out or pause their time accrual \`\`.

## **Visual Design System Recommendation**

The requested "Dark Premium Cinematic" direction is highly effective for reducing cognitive load and eye strain for tired healthcare workers operating in low-light environments.15 However, dark mode design systems require rigorous mathematical adherence to contrast rules to prevent the interface from becoming an accessibility liability.

### **Color Palette**

The color palette must evoke the prestige of a medical institute while remaining highly functional.

* **Base and Background:** The application must use Charcoal (\#121212) and Deep Surface tones (\#1E1E1E). Pure black (\#000000) must be strictly avoided, as it causes severe OLED smearing and creates halation effects for users with astigmatism when paired with light text.6  
* **Primary Accents:** Deep Burgundy and Oxblood tones (\#7A1026, \#5A0F1D) serve as the primary brand anchors, used for structural headers and critical warning blocks.3  
* **Secondary Accents:** Antique Gold (\#D2A84C, \#B7892B) is utilized for progress rings, active states, and primary Call-To-Action buttons, creating a sense of premium prestige and drawing the user's eye to required tasks.3  
* **Typography and Foreground:** Warm White (\#F7EEDC) or light grey (\#E0E0E0) must be used for primary reading text to soften the contrast slightly compared to pure white. Muted Taupe/Grey (\#B0B0B0) is reserved for secondary text and metadata.24

### **Typography Hierarchy**

The typographic system relies on a dual-font approach to balance institutional elegance with modern legibility. Elegant Serif typefaces (such as *Playfair Display*, *Merriweather*, or *Lora*) should be used sparingly for display purposes, page titles, and module headers to convey institutional authority. Conversely, the body interface must utilize a modern, highly legible Sans-Serif typeface (such as *Inter*, *Roboto*, or *Public Sans*). This sans-serif font is deployed for all body copy, UI labels, and data tables to ensure maximum readability at small sizes and on mobile screens.20

### **Glassmorphism Implementation Rules**

Subtle glassmorphism is a core aesthetic request, but it poses severe accessibility risks if applied indiscriminately over complex backgrounds.25 To implement glassmorphism safely, designers must follow strict parameter constraints. The background blur radius must be limited to between 10px and 30px to prevent the interface from looking muddy. Furthermore, an opacity shield is mandatory; designers must always include a semi-transparent solid background tint (e.g., rgba(255,255,255, 0.05)) to act as a dampening layer against background visual noise. A 1px semi-transparent border (e.g., rgba(255,255,255, 0.15)) is non-negotiable to clearly define the edge of the glass card for users with low vision.7 Most importantly, any text placed on a glassmorphic card must be tested dynamically to ensure a 4.5:1 contrast ratio against the lightest possible background pixel that could bleed through the blur.7

### **Spatial and Structural Rules**

Card styles should feature subtle 8px rounded corners to soften the harshness of the dark interface. Shadow rules in dark mode require careful handling; traditional drop shadows disappear against charcoal backgrounds. Instead, elevation must be depicted through subtle shifts in surface lightness (making the elevated card slightly lighter grey) or by using a soft, low-opacity colored glow.27 Button hierarchy must be strictly maintained: primary CTAs are filled Antique Gold, secondary CTAs feature gold outlines, and tertiary actions are simple text links with underlines. Data tables must utilize horizontal borders only, with generous padding (e.g., 16px) to prevent the claustrophobic density typical of legacy administrative software.

## **Accessibility Audit and Recommendations**

As a healthcare education platform intended to eventually map to Moodle—a platform that boasts formal WCAG 2.2 AA compliance accreditation 8—the standalone MVP must embed digital accessibility into its core design tokens from inception. Failure to meet these standards not only alienates users but poses compliance risks for institutional adoption.

1. **Dark Mode Contrast Risks:** The most common failure in dark mode UI is utilizing medium-grey text on dark-grey backgrounds. All body text must mathematically pass the WCAG minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 against its immediate background.23 Large text (defined as 18pt or 14pt bold) requires a minimum 3:1 ratio.20 Even disabled states (e.g., locked buttons) must be carefully tuned to remain visible without confusing the user about their interactivity.  
2. **Focus States and Keyboard Navigation:** The application must be entirely navigable via a keyboard. The default browser focus ring is often invisible on dark backgrounds. Therefore, the design must specify a highly visible, custom focus state—such as an Antique Gold outline (2px solid \#D2A84C with a 2px offset)—to clearly indicate the focused element.6  
3. **Tap Target Recommendations:** The mobile experience must adhere strictly to minimum touch target sizes. All interactive elements, including links, buttons, and navigation icons, must possess a minimum touch area of 44x44 CSS pixels. This accommodates shift workers operating the app quickly, potentially with one hand, or with limited dexterity due to fatigue.11  
4. **Status-Not-By-Color Rules:** Information cannot be conveyed using color alone.16 If a module is marked "Complete" using a green badge, it must also include the word "Complete" or a checkmark icon. This ensures that users with red-green color blindness—the most common form of color vision deficiency—can successfully parse interface states.28  
5. **Link Identifiers:** In-line text links within the Lesson Player cannot rely solely on a color shift (e.g., turning text gold). They must include a persistent underline or a distinct background hover state to differentiate them from surrounding body text.29  
6. **Screen Reader Labeling Guidance:** The visual hierarchy must perfectly match the underlying Document Object Model (DOM) hierarchy. The Page Title must be programmed as an \<h1\>, Module Titles as \<h2\>, and sub-sections as \<h3\>. Designers must not skip heading levels simply for visual sizing, as this breaks screen reader navigation.16

## **Compliance-Safe UX Guidance**

The interface must visually enforce strict regulatory guardrails. Failure to do so jeopardizes the legal validity of the generated CE certificates and places the operating institution at risk of CDPH auditing failure.  
**1\. Required vs. Optional Separation:** Under California CDPH regulations, CNAs must complete 48 total hours of CE, but a maximum of only 24 hours may be completed via an online computer training program.4 The MVP delivers a 12-hour required online theory pathway, which safely fits within this limit. However, the UI must *never* visually blend this 12-hour required pathway with the optional clinical support hub. If a user assumes the optional simulations count toward their California clinical hours, the platform is out of compliance.4 The UX solution is to keep progress bars strictly isolated. The "Overall Course Progress" dashboard ring must *only* calculate data from the Required CE pathway. The Clinical Support Hub must feature a persistent "Non-Credit" and "Optional" badge on every screen.  
**2\. Active-Time Requirement Visualization:** LMS compliance for FDA-regulated or state-regulated healthcare industries (akin to 21 CFR Part 11 protocols) requires proving that the user was actively engaged with the material, not merely leaving a browser tab open in the background.10 The UX solution involves integrating "Time Elapsed" versus "Estimated Time" indicators directly into the Lesson Player. The system must use interactive UI components—such as click-to-reveal cards or scenario branching checks—that log interaction timestamps to the database, thereby validating active human presence.9  
**3\. Affidavit and Final Exam Gating:**  
Regulatory bodies require a final assessment and a legally binding attestation of completion. The final exam and the final statement/affidavit must be explicitly locked until all prerequisite modules and active-time minimums are achieved. In the Gate Center, the UI must show the Final Exam row with a locked padlock icon and explicit helper text: *"Unlocks upon verified completion of Modules 1-8 and active-time validation."*  
**4\. No PHI (Protected Health Information) Protocol:**  
Because the Optional Clinical Support Hub includes documentation practice and case study notes, there is a severe risk of users accidentally pasting real patient data into the system, violating HIPAA. The UX solution mandates that every free-text textarea and file upload zone must have a hardcoded, highly visible alert block: *"STOP: Do not enter Protected Health Information (PHI), real patient names, or facility data. Use simulated case data only."*  
**5\. Audit Packet Readiness:** The system must be capable of generating defensible reports for state auditors. The Audit View must not look like an unformatted database dump. It must clearly display approval metadata, timestamped identity verification, and cleanly formatted tables proving that the user met all gates prior to the certificate being generated.10

## **Desktop and Mobile Prototype Recommendations**

### **Desktop Prototype Structure**

The desktop prototype should emulate a high-end, native software application rather than a traditional scrolling webpage. The Global Header spans the top, featuring the CI Institute Logo on the left, contextual breadcrumbs in the center to aid deep navigation, and a notification bell with the user avatar on the right.  
In the critical Lesson View, a left-hand sidebar should be introduced to track the table of contents and lesson status, allowing users to jump seamlessly between completed sections. The main content area must be constrained to a maximum width of roughly 1200px. This prevents the UI from stretching uncomfortably on ultra-wide clinical monitors, maintaining optimal reading line lengths. Content should be centered using a strict grid system, with ample negative space (margins) utilized to elevate the premium, uncluttered feel.

### **Mobile Prototype Structure (Priority for Shift Workers)**

The mobile layout is arguably more critical, as shift workers frequently rely on their smartphones during brief breaks.11 The complex desktop header must be abandoned in favor of a persistent Bottom Navigation Bar. This bar should feature large, easily tap-able icons for Home (Dashboard), Modules, Certificate, and Hub (Clinical).  
On the mobile Dashboard layout, single-column scrolling is mandatory. The "Next Up" card must consume the very top of the viewport immediately under the greeting, featuring a large, unmissable Antique Gold CTA button. This guarantees the user knows exactly what to do without scrolling. In the mobile Lesson View, the left sidebar from the desktop version must be converted into a swipeable bottom sheet or a hidden hamburger menu. The textual content utilizes dynamic typography sizing to ensure effortless legibility on small screens without requiring the user to pinch and zoom.

| **Moodle Core LMS** | Deep, highly structured compliance tracking and rigid hierarchy.22 | Map the MVP’s custom modules directly to Moodle’s mod\_lesson and mod\_quiz backend structures to guarantee future integration ease.12 | Absolutely avoid Moodle's notoriously dated, blocky default UI and overwhelming nested menus.32 |
| **Epic Systems (EHR)** | High-density data presentation tailored specifically to clinicians.33 | Emulate their clear typographic hierarchy and specific iconography styles designed for healthcare contexts. | Avoid the severe cognitive overload and cluttered screen real estate inherent in legacy EHR systems. |

## **UI/UX Risk Register**

A successful redesign must preemptively mitigate design risks that could lead to compliance failures or user abandonment.

| Risk Category | Identified Design Risk | Strategic Mitigation Plan |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| **Compliance** | Optional Clinical Support appears required, leading users to believe they are earning CA renewal clinical hours, causing CDPH auditing failure.4 | Implement explicit, standardized "Non-Credit" and "Optional" badging on every clinical hub card. Separate all progress tracking entirely from the main CE dashboard. |
| **Accessibility** | Dark mode readability failure due to insufficient contrast between charcoal backgrounds and grey text.15 | Strict enforcement of WCAG 4.5:1 minimum contrast ratios.23 Use Warm White (\#F7EEDC) for all primary reading text. |
| **Performance** | Glassmorphism effects degrade rendering performance and cause scrolling lag on older mobile devices.7 | Restrict CSS background blur to a maximum of 15px. Use CSS @supports queries to provide flat, semi-transparent fallbacks for unsupported browsers. |
| **Data Presentation** | The Audit page resembles a confusing, raw backend spreadsheet, overwhelming non-technical reviewers.34 | Utilize polished, well-spaced data tables with generous padding, clear status pills, and human-readable timestamps. |
| **User Experience** | "Active-Time" validation frustrates users if they are logged out or timed out without sufficient warning.10 | Implement clear, visible session timers and gentle toast notifications prompting the user to confirm they are still active before automatically timing out. |
| **Cognitive Load** | Dashboard overcrowding occurs if too many reminders and quick links are surfaced simultaneously.1 | Rely on the "Next Up" progressive disclosure model. Hide secondary metrics inside swipeable carousels. |
| **Visual Design** | Overuse of Antique Gold and Burgundy creates a visually garish or overwhelming interface.3 | Use charcoal/black as the 80% base. Reserve Burgundy for structural accents and Gold strictly for interactive elements and primary CTAs. |
| **Liability** | The critical PHI warning is buried below the fold or ignored by users moving quickly. | The PHI warning block must be pinned directly above the text input field, utilizing high-contrast oxblood, and cannot be dismissed by the user. |
| **Navigation** | Mobile navigation overload occurs if the desktop header is simply shrunk down. | Transition to a strict 4-to-5 icon bottom navigation bar for all mobile viewports. |
| **Progression** | Certificate status confuses learners, leaving them unsure why their document won't generate. | The Gate Center must explicitly list every single required boolean check in plain English with a corresponding Complete/Pending badge. |

## **Prioritized Redesign Roadmap**

To execute this strategy efficiently without overwhelming the development pipeline, the redesign must be phased.  
**P0: Must Fix Before Stakeholder Demo**  
The immediate priority is repairing the Information Architecture and core flows. The design team must replace the flat task lists with the structured Dashboard, Modules, and Lesson Views. Establishing the impenetrable visual bifurcation between the required CE Theory and the Optional Clinical Hub is mandatory at this stage. Finally, the Dark Premium design tokens (colors, typography) must be applied to these core screens to secure stakeholder buy-in.  
**P1: Must Fix Before Learner Pilot** Before actual users interact with the system, all compliance and accessibility safeguards must be operational. The Gate Center must be designed to visualize the Certificate Status locks accurately. The active-time UI checks (interactive scenario cards) must be embedded within the Lesson Player. The PHI warning blocks must be deployed across the clinical hub. Concurrently, a full WCAG AA contrast audit must be conducted to ensure the dark mode text is fully legible.23  
**P2: Post-MVP Polish**  
Once the core loop is functional and compliant, attention shifts to administrative and stakeholder views. The Audit data tables must be refined, and export functionalities visualized. The Moodle Map stakeholder view should be built out to demonstrate long-term scalability. The subtle glassmorphic effects and elevation shadows can be fine-tuned during this phase to enhance the premium feel.  
**P3: Future Elite Experience**  
Long-term enhancements include adding advanced micro-interactions, complex scenario-based branching simulations in the Clinical Hub, and potentially exploring localized offline-mode support for the mobile application to assist learners with poor hospital Wi-Fi connectivity.

## **Designer Handoff Checklist**

To execute this research strategy, the Senior Product Designer should utilize the following checklist to produce the high-fidelity Figma designs:

* \[ \] **Establish Design Tokens:** Define core hex codes for Base (Charcoal \#121212), Accents (Burgundy \#7A1026, Gold \#D2A84C), and Text (Warm White \#F7EEDC). Ensure all specific foreground/background pairings pass WCAG AA 4.5:1 checks via a contrast plugin.  
* \[ \] **Define Typography Scale:** Establish h1 through h6 utilizing the chosen Serif display font, and create robust body text styles utilizing the chosen Sans-Serif font. Set line-heights to a minimum of 1.5 for readability.  
* \[ \] **Build Component Library:** Construct the core interactive components: the App Shell, Mobile Bottom Navigation, Module Cards, Lesson Rows, and Gate Requirement Rows. Ensure hover, active, and disabled states are designed for each.  
* \[ \] **Standardize Glassmorphism Spec:** Create a standardized effect style in Figma (e.g., Background Blur 15px, Fill White 10%, Stroke White 15% inside) and apply it consistently to progress cards and dropdowns.7  
* \[ \] **Create Status Badge System:** Build auto-layout badges combining a distinct shape/icon and text for all states (Complete, Pending, Blocked, Needs Review, Optional/Non-Credit).  
* \[ \] **Design Desktop Views (10):** Produce high-fidelity mockups for Course Landing, Dashboard, Modules, Module Detail, Lesson Player, Certificate Status, Gate Center, Clinical Hub, Audit, and Moodle Map.  
* \[ \] **Design Mobile Views (Priority):** Produce corresponding high-fidelity mobile layouts for the Dashboard, Modules, Lesson View, Gate Center, and Clinical Hub, ensuring 44x44px touch targets.  
* \[ \] **Implement Compliance Components:** Standardize and place the "Certificate Locked" banner and the "No PHI" warning block precisely as dictated by the UX rules.  
* \[ \] **Link Interactive Prototype:** Connect the "Next Up" card on the Dashboard to the Lesson Player, and link the Lesson Player's completion state back to an updated, unlocked Dashboard view to demonstrate the core user loop to stakeholders.

By adhering strictly to this comprehensive UI/UX research report, the CI Institute MVP will successfully transcend the limitations of a generic task tracker. It will deliver a highly premium, empathetic, and cognitively optimized learning journey for exhausted healthcare workers, while simultaneously maintaining the rigorous compliance architecture demanded by CDPH regulations and enterprise auditing standards.
