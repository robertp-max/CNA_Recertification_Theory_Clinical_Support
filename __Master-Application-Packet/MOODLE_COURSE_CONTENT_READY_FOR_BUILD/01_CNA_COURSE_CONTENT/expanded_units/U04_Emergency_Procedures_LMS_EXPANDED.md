moodle_description: Explains how CNAs recognize possible emergency distress using airway, breathing, circulation, neurologic, bleeding, pain, and behavior cues.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after U04 overview page is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify the difference between routine observations and distress signs that require immediate help.
content_body: |
  Emergency recognition begins with the idea that a change can be more important than a number. Some emergencies are obvious, such as a resident collapsing, choking silently, bleeding heavily, or having severe shortness of breath. Others are quieter. A resident who is usually alert may become suddenly difficult to awaken. A resident who usually speaks clearly may develop slurred speech or a new facial droop. A resident who usually walks to the bathroom may become pale, sweaty, weak, dizzy, or unable to stand. A resident at a meal may stop speaking, clutch the throat, or turn bluish. These are not moments for routine documentation first. They are moments to call for help according to facility procedure.

  Think in broad categories. Airway and breathing distress may include inability to speak, ineffective coughing, noisy breathing, gasping, severe shortness of breath, blue or gray color around lips or nail beds, unusually fast or slow respirations, or a resident saying, "I cannot breathe." Circulation distress may include chest pressure, pain radiating to the arm, jaw, back, or shoulder, fainting, severe weakness, pale or clammy skin, uncontrolled bleeding, or signs of shock. Neurologic distress may include sudden confusion, seizure-like activity, new one-sided weakness, severe headache, new trouble speaking, or loss of consciousness. Other urgent findings include a fall with injury concern, suspected fracture, burns, poisoning concern, severe allergic response, or sudden intense pain.

  CNAs do not diagnose heart attack, stroke, respiratory failure, seizure disorder, or shock. The CNA identifies what is seen, heard, measured, or stated and immediately alerts the nurse or emergency response chain. Use direct words. "Resident is unable to speak and is clutching the throat" is clearer than "something is wrong." "Resident has new left-sided weakness and slurred speech" is clearer than "resident seems off." Good emergency communication gives licensed staff a chance to respond quickly.

  The emergency mindset is calm urgency. Calm does not mean slow. It means you keep your voice steady, stay with the resident when safe, call for help, reduce hazards around the resident, protect privacy, and follow the role you have been assigned. If several residents are nearby, your actions should avoid panic while still making the seriousness clear to staff. If you are unsure whether a finding is urgent, report it. Delay can be more dangerous than asking for help.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Immediate Reporting, Team Response, and Scope Boundaries

activity_id: U04_BOOK_SEGMENT_02
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 2 — Activate Help Before Routine Documentation
moodle_description: Reviews immediate reporting, emergency communication, resident safety steps, and CNA scope boundaries during a facility emergency.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and practice forming a brief emergency report that includes location, observation, resident statement, and action requested.
content_body: |
  In an emergency, the order of actions matters. Routine charting is not the first step when a resident may be in danger. The CNA should activate help immediately by using the approved facility method: calling the nurse, using the call system, pulling an alarm if policy directs, pressing an emergency button, sending a nearby staff member for help, or using the facility code process. The exact method is local, but the principle is consistent: do not wait quietly to see whether a serious sign disappears.

  A useful emergency report is short, direct, and factual. Include who needs help, where the resident is, what you observed, what the resident said if able to speak, what changed from the usual pattern, and what has already been done within policy. Example: "Resident D is in the dining room, cannot speak, is clutching the throat, and cannot cough effectively. Emergency response is needed now." Another example: "Resident is in room area, new slurred speech and right arm weakness started during morning care; nurse needed now." Avoid long explanations, blame, or guesses about diagnosis. The nurse and emergency team need the key facts first.

  Scope boundaries protect residents and learners. The CNA should not independently interpret medical orders, decide that an emergency code is unnecessary, tell a family member that a resident is or is not having a specific diagnosis, or perform an emergency procedure that is not within current training and authorization. If trained and authorized, follow facility procedure. If not trained or not sure, activate help, stay with the resident as safe, keep the area clear, bring requested supplies if directed, and continue communicating facts. Online CE can reinforce recognition and judgment, but hands-on emergency skills must be taught, practiced, and validated through approved channels.

  Team response also requires awareness of other residents. A medical emergency in a dining room, hallway, shower room, or activity area may create crowding, fear, or trip hazards. CNAs may be asked to move other residents away, clear equipment, guide responding staff to the location, protect privacy, or continue monitoring residents not directly involved. Follow directions from licensed staff and the facility emergency plan. Do not post, photograph, text, or discuss the event outside approved reporting pathways.

  After the immediate danger is addressed, documentation and reporting continue. Chart only what policy allows and what you personally observed or did. Record times, objective signs, resident statements, notifications, and assigned actions. If an incident or event form is required, use the current facility process rather than copying forms into the online course.

moodle_description: Describes choking signs, airway danger cues, unsafe delays, and online CE boundaries for emergency-response skills.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and focus on recognition, immediate escalation, and what this online CE does not certify.
content_body: |
  Choking is one of the most time-sensitive emergencies a CNA may witness during meals, snacks, oral care, or medication pass support. A resident with a mild airway problem may be coughing forcefully and able to speak. A resident with a severe airway obstruction may be unable to speak, unable to cough effectively, unable to breathe, clutching the throat, panicked, turning blue or gray, or collapsing. Silence during a meal can be a warning sign. The CNA should not assume the resident is being dramatic, embarrassed, or difficult.

  Immediate escalation is essential. Call for the nurse and activate the facility emergency response according to policy. If you are currently trained and authorized in the facility's choking-response procedure, follow that procedure. If you are not trained or are uncertain, get help immediately, stay with the resident as safe, and follow the directions of licensed or emergency-response staff. Do not wait until the end of the meal, offer a drink to "wash it down," place fingers blindly in the mouth, slap or move the resident in a way not allowed by current training, or leave the resident alone to find paperwork.

  Choking recognition also includes prevention awareness, although prevention does not eliminate emergencies. CNAs support safe eating by checking the tray against the diet order, positioning the resident as directed, encouraging small bites and slow pace when assigned, observing for pocketing or fatigue, and reporting coughing, wet voice, drooling, difficulty swallowing, or repeated throat clearing. If a resident has dysphagia precautions, thickened liquids, supervision requirements, dentures, adaptive utensils, or a modified diet, the CNA follows the current care plan and asks before making changes. The CNA never changes the diet order independently.

  After a choking event or suspected airway emergency, reporting remains factual. Useful details include what the resident was doing, what was eaten if known without guessing, whether the resident could speak or cough, color change, actions used to activate help, who responded, and the resident's condition after the event as observed. Do not diagnose aspiration, pneumonia, or injury. Do not write dramatic opinions such as "almost died" or "staff ignored it." State what you saw, what you did within role, and who was notified.

  This lesson is deliberately cognitive. It helps you recognize danger and choose safe communication. It does not teach the body mechanics of abdominal thrusts, CPR, rescue breathing, suctioning, or any hands-on emergency procedure. Those skills require approved live instruction, practice, and validation outside this online CE unit.

learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify which emergency-code details must come from your current facility policy rather than from this online CE unit.
content_body: |
  Emergency codes are shared signals that help a facility respond quickly. Code colors, names, overhead announcements, badge cards, phone numbers, alarm systems, and role assignments vary by organization. One facility may use a color for a medical emergency, another may use plain language, and another may use a different code set. Because of that variation, online CE can teach the expectation to know and follow local codes, but it cannot provide a universal code list for every workplace.

  Common code categories include medical emergency, fire or smoke, missing resident, severe weather, utility failure, security threat, evacuation, hazardous spill, and disaster response. A CNA should know how to report an emergency, where alarms and exits are located, how to keep hallways clear, how to move residents only as directed, where to assemble if assigned, and how to protect residents who are frightened, confused, oxygen-dependent, hard of hearing, or unable to follow directions independently. If a fire alarm or evacuation instruction occurs, the CNA follows the facility plan and the direction of the charge nurse or emergency leader. Do not improvise routes or move residents in unsafe ways outside the plan.

  Emergency codes require privacy discipline. A code announcement may draw attention, but residents still deserve dignity. Keep voices calm. Do not reveal unnecessary details to visitors, other residents, or bystanders. Do not use personal phones to photograph, record, or text about the event. Do not enter real code events into online assignments. In Moodle, use only fictional Resident D or generic examples.

  Advance directives and DNR orders can feel confusing during emergencies. The CNA should not independently interpret legal or medical documents or decide whether an emergency response should be started or withheld. Follow facility procedure, notify licensed staff immediately, and carry out assigned CNA actions. If a family member asks what will happen, use scope-safe language such as, "I am getting the nurse now," or "The nurse can answer that question." Do not promise that a specific treatment will or will not occur.

  Post-event documentation and debriefing help the facility improve safety. The CNA may be asked to provide a factual statement, complete assigned charting, restock supplies, help calm residents, or attend a staff debrief. Keep documentation objective: time, location, resident statement, observed signs, emergency response activated, who was notified, and assigned actions completed. Avoid blame, speculation, copied forms, and private information not required for your role.

Use only this fictional scenario; do not add real workplace details.

  **Decision Point 1 — Recognize and escalate:** What should the CNA do first?

  A. Activate help immediately according to facility emergency procedure, alert the nurse or emergency response chain, stay with Resident D as safe, and follow only current training and authorization.  
  B. Wait quietly for another minute because the resident may be embarrassed and might cough later.  
  C. Offer water or more food to push the item down.  
  D. Leave the resident alone and look for an incident form before calling anyone.

  **Correct feedback for A:** This response recognizes severe airway danger and prioritizes immediate help. It keeps the CNA inside scope by tying any hands-on response to current training, authorization, and facility procedure.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B, C, or D:** Waiting, offering food or drink, or leaving the resident alone can delay emergency response and increase risk. Paperwork comes after help is activated and the resident is safe.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Rewrite the first sentence you would say to nearby staff using location, observed signs, and the words "help now."

  **Decision Point 2 — Manage the area while help is coming:** Which action best supports the team response within CNA scope?

  A. Announce private details loudly so everyone knows what is happening.  
  B. Clear nearby hazards, ask an available staff member or visitor to get the nurse according to policy if help is not already activated, keep other residents safe, and follow directions from the emergency leader.  
  C. Move Resident D to another room without direction so the dining room can continue meal service.  
  D. Tell the visitor that no emergency exists because you do not want to create panic.

  **Correct feedback for B:** This action supports resident safety, crowd control, privacy, and the facility response chain without exceeding the CNA role.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A, C, or D:** Sharing private details, moving the resident without direction, or minimizing an apparent airway emergency can interfere with safe response.  
  **Remediation prompt:** List two area-safety actions that do not require diagnosis or hands-on skill validation.

  **Decision Point 3 — Report and document after the event:** After licensed staff take over and the immediate response is complete, which report is most appropriate?

  A. "Resident D almost died because people were not paying attention."  
  B. "Resident D probably aspirated and will need a hospital transfer."  
  C. "Resident D was eating dinner, suddenly stopped speaking, grasped the throat, could not cough effectively, emergency response was activated, and the nurse responded."  
  D. "I will write the real resident name and dining room details in my Moodle assignment so the instructor understands."
