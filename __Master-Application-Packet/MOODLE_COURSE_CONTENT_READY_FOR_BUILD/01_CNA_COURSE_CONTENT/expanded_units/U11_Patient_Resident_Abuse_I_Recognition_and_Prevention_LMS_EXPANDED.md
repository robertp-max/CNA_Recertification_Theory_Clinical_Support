content_body:

Abuse prevention begins before a crisis. It begins when every resident is treated as a person with rights, preferences, privacy, and a voice. CNAs are often close to residents during bathing, dressing, meals, toileting, transfers, and quiet moments when a resident may feel safe enough to speak. Because of that close contact, CNAs may notice early clues: a new bruise, a fearful reaction, a change in hygiene, a missing personal item, a humiliating comment, or a resident statement that something is wrong.

By the end of this unit, you should be able to define common abuse-related terms, identify physical, verbal, psychological, sexual, financial, and neglect-related concerns, recognize patterns that need prompt attention, and explain how resident rights reduce risk. You will also practice the boundary between recognition and investigation. A CNA does not decide whether abuse legally occurred. A CNA does not pressure a resident for details, confront a suspected person, collect evidence independently, or promise secrecy. A CNA does protect the resident as directed, stay calm, report promptly through the required path, and record factual observations without accusation.

This course is online CE only. It does not provide clinical time, simulation time, or hands-on validation. All examples are fictional. If a prompt asks you to write a report, use only the fictional resident in the prompt and avoid real workplace details.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 1 — Terminology and Resident Rights

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 1  
lane: CNA online continuing education  
unit_id: U11

estimated_time_minutes: 18  
learner_visible_title: Segment 1 — Words That Protect Residents  
moodle_description: Book page defining key Module 17 abuse-prevention terms and connecting them to resident rights.  
completion_tracking: Require view; include in Book completion.  
restrict_access: Available after U11 overview completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Read the terms and think about how each term changes daily CNA choices.  
content_body:

Clear language helps protect residents. Abuse means harm, threatened harm, or treatment that violates the resident's safety, dignity, rights, or freedom from mistreatment. Neglect means failing to provide needed care, assistance, supervision, or protection. Neglect may be intentional, such as refusing to help a resident who needs toileting, or unintentional, such as leaving a resident without needed fluids because the care plan was not followed. Both forms matter because the resident can still be harmed.

Physical abuse involves force or rough treatment, such as hitting, pushing, handling a limb harshly, using a restraint-like hold outside the care plan, or moving a resident in a way that causes fear or injury. Verbal abuse uses words or tone to threaten, shame, insult, or intimidate. Psychological or emotional abuse includes humiliation, isolation, manipulation, retaliation, or repeated behavior that makes the resident feel powerless. Sexual abuse includes any non-consensual sexual contact, exposure, coercion, or sexualized conduct toward a resident. Financial abuse involves theft, pressure, misuse of money or property, or forcing a resident to give gifts, passwords, access, or signatures. Involuntary seclusion means separating or confining a resident against the resident's will or care-plan direction for staff convenience or control.

Resident rights are not separate from abuse prevention; they are part of prevention. Knocking before entering, covering the resident during care, explaining what you are doing, asking preferences when possible, keeping call lights reachable, and respecting personal property all communicate that the resident matters. Rights also include privacy, confidentiality, freedom from retaliation, participation in care decisions, and the ability to voice grievances. When daily care protects rights, it becomes harder for disrespectful patterns to become normal.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Abuse Types, Neglect, and Common Risk Patterns

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 2  
lane: CNA online continuing education  
unit_id: U11  
activity_id: U11_BOOK_SEGMENT_2  
estimated_time_minutes: 18  
learner_visible_title: Segment 2 — Types of Abuse and Neglect Patterns  
moodle_description: Book page comparing abuse categories, neglect examples, and patterns CNAs may observe during routine care.  
completion_tracking: Require view; include in Book completion.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 1 completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Study the examples as possible warning signs, not as proof or diagnosis.  
content_body:

A single sign does not prove abuse, but signs and patterns should never be ignored. Physical concerns may include unexplained bruises, welts, burns, skin tears, restraint marks, repeated injuries, pain when touched, or an explanation that does not match what is observed. Emotional concerns may include sudden withdrawal, flinching, fear around a particular person, crying after care, sleep changes, refusal of help from certain staff, or a resident saying, “Please do not leave me with that person.” Financial concerns may appear as missing belongings, repeated requests for money, unusual gifts to staff, unexplained lack of personal items, or a resident appearing worried about a card, wallet, password, or account.

Neglect patterns may be less dramatic but equally serious. Examples include frequent unanswered call lights, soiled clothing left unchanged, poor oral care, dehydration risk, weight loss, missed turning and repositioning, unsafe clutter, lack of needed assistive devices, delayed toileting help, or failure to report pain or skin concerns. A resident with cognitive changes may not be able to explain what happened. A resident who depends on staff for mobility, eating, hygiene, communication, or medication access is especially vulnerable when care is rushed or ignored.

Some patterns involve the environment or team culture. Staff may speak about residents as tasks instead of people. A coworker may joke about “punishing” a resident by making them wait. A rushed aide may use a harsh grip and call it “just getting the job done.” These moments are prevention opportunities. You can slow down, ask for help, follow the care plan, protect privacy, and report concerns. Prevention is not only stopping obvious harm; it is refusing to normalize disrespect.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Recognition Without Investigation

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3  
lane: CNA online continuing education  
unit_id: U11  
activity_id: U11_BOOK_SEGMENT_3  
estimated_time_minutes: 18  
learner_visible_title: Segment 3 — What CNAs Recognize, Report, and Do Not Investigate  
moodle_description: Book page distinguishing CNA recognition and safety support from investigation, accusation, or interrogation.  
completion_tracking: Require view; include in Book completion.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 2 completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Focus on what you can do immediately within scope and what you must avoid.  
content_body:

Recognition is the ability to notice a concern and respond safely. Investigation is the process of determining what happened, who is responsible, whether a law or policy was violated, and what formal action follows. That investigation role belongs to authorized people and agencies, not to the CNA. This boundary protects residents because it reduces gossip, retaliation, lost information, and pressure on the resident. It also protects the CNA by keeping the response factual and within scope.

When a resident makes a concerning statement, listen calmly. You may say, “I am sorry this happened,” or “I am going to get help.” Do not promise secrecy, because concerns about abuse or neglect must be reported through required channels. Do not ask repeated leading questions such as, “Was it Sam who did this?” or “Did this happen because you made someone angry?” Leading questions can confuse the resident, contaminate later review, or make the resident feel blamed. Instead, remember the resident's exact words as closely as possible, note what you observed, and notify the licensed nurse or supervisor immediately according to policy.

Objective language is essential. “Resident stated, ‘Please do not leave me with that person,’ and pulled arm away when caregiver entered” is stronger and safer than “Caregiver abused the resident.” The first statement reports facts and the resident's words. The second statement reaches a conclusion the CNA is not authorized to make. If immediate safety is a concern, stay with the resident if safe, call for help, and follow directions from licensed staff. If the suspected person is nearby, do not confront them. Confrontation can increase risk and interfere with required response.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Prevention Through Boundaries, Team Habits, and Self-Control

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 4  
lane: CNA online continuing education  
unit_id: U11  
activity_id: U11_BOOK_SEGMENT_4  
estimated_time_minutes: 16  
learner_visible_title: Segment 4 — Professional Boundaries and Prevention Habits  
moodle_description: Book page on daily prevention behaviors, professional boundaries, and stress-response habits that protect residents.  
completion_tracking: Require view; include in Book completion.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 3 completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Identify two prevention habits you can use during stressful care moments.  
content_body:

Professional boundaries are safety tools. A CNA should not borrow from a resident, lend money, accept inappropriate gifts, keep a resident's valuables for convenience, share personal contact information in ways prohibited by policy, post about residents online, or discuss resident details with people who do not need to know. Boundaries are also emotional. Residents should not be threatened, teased, mocked, punished, isolated, or made responsible for staff frustration. A resident who needs repeated help is not “being difficult”; the resident is communicating a need, fear, symptom, habit, or limitation that the care team must address respectfully.

Prevention includes managing your own stress before it becomes unsafe behavior. If you feel rushed, angry, overwhelmed, or tempted to use forceful words or handling, pause in a safe way and get help. Use call systems, teamwork, supervisor support, and care-plan guidance. Ask for a second staff member when a transfer, behavior, or care task is beyond what you can safely manage alone. Report broken equipment, unsafe room setup, low supplies, or repeated staffing barriers through the proper process. These are not excuses for neglect, but they are conditions that need attention before residents are harmed.

Respectful micro-habits matter: greet the resident by preferred name, explain before touching, offer choices within the care plan, keep the resident covered, place belongings within reach, answer call lights promptly, and speak as if family, surveyors, and the resident's own advocate were listening. Abuse prevention is not a slogan. It is repeated, visible behavior that says the resident's dignity is not optional.

completion_tracking: Require completion of all decision points and either score of at least 80 percent or successful safe branch completion.

learner_instructions: Complete each decision point. If you choose an unsafe option, read the feedback and complete the remediation prompt before retrying.  
content_body:

Scenario setup: Resident K is usually talkative during evening care. Tonight Resident K is quiet and keeps the blanket pulled high. When a particular caregiver walks past the doorway, Resident K flinches, lowers their voice, and says, “Please do not leave me with that person.” During care you observe a new purple bruise on the upper arm. Resident K does not give more details. The call light is within reach, the roommate is asleep, and the nurse is at the nurses' station.

Decision Point 1 — Classify what you noticed.  
Choice A: “The resident statement, flinching, and new bruise are possible warning signs that need prompt reporting.” Correct feedback: This is recognition, not a conclusion. You noticed facts and a resident statement that require action.  
Choice B: “The bruise proves the named caregiver abused the resident.” Unsafe-choice feedback: This jumps to a conclusion. CNAs report facts and concerns; authorized staff or agencies investigate.  
Choice C: “The resident did not provide enough detail, so no action is needed.” Unsafe-choice feedback: Lack of detail does not make the concern safe to ignore. Fearful behavior and a direct request not to be left alone require prompt help.

Decision Point 2 — Choose the immediate safety response.  
Choice A: “Confront the caregiver in the hallway and demand an explanation.” Unsafe-choice feedback: Confrontation can increase risk, alert the suspected person, and interfere with required response.  
Choice B: “Stay calm with the resident, keep the resident safe as directed, and notify the licensed nurse or supervisor immediately using objective facts.” Correct feedback: This protects the resident, preserves information, and stays within CNA scope.  
Choice C: “Post a warning in a staff group chat so everyone knows who might be dangerous.” Unsafe-choice feedback: This violates confidentiality, spreads unverified accusations, and is not an approved reporting path.

Decision Point 3 — Select objective report language.  
Choice A: “Resident K is being abused by the evening caregiver.” Unsafe-choice feedback: This is an accusation and does not separate facts from conclusions.  
Choice B: “Resident K was dramatic and refused normal care.” Unsafe-choice feedback: This blames the resident and omits the concerning statement and bruise.  
Choice C: “At 1905 Resident K stated, ‘Please do not leave me with that person,’ flinched when the caregiver passed the doorway, and a new purple bruise was observed on the upper arm. Nurse notified.” Correct feedback: This uses time, exact words, observable behavior, body location, and notification without diagnosing or accusing.

Remediation prompt: If any unsafe choice is selected, write two fictional sentences: one fact you would report and one action you would avoid because it belongs outside the CNA role. Do not include real names, dates, facility details, or identifiers.

Completion condition: The activity is complete when the learner finishes all three decision points, reviews feedback, submits any required remediation, and reaches the safe branch or a minimum score of 80 percent.

Moodle evidence for this activity: H5P attempt data or Moodle Lesson branch logs, score or completion status, feedback viewed, remediation submission if configured, timestamp, and unit activity completion record.
