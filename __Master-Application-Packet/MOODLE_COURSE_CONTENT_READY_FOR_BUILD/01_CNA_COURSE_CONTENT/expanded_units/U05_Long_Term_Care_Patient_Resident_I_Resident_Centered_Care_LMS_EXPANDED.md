content_body: |
  Long-term care is not only a place where tasks are completed. It is a living environment for residents who have histories, preferences, routines, losses, strengths, and rights. A resident-centered CNA asks, "What matters to this person today?" while still following the care plan and facility policy. That question can affect how you offer a bath, how you position a resident for meals, how you respond to a quiet mood, and how quickly you report a new skin concern.

  In U05, you will practice separating five types of information: resident preference, physical observation, psychosocial observation, reportable skin or mobility concern, and diagnosis or label that is outside CNA documentation. You will also review how aging may affect body systems without assuming that every new change is "just old age." The resident's baseline matters. If a resident usually walks to activities, eats most breakfast, greets peers, and has intact skin, then several days of staying in bed, eating little, moving slowly, and developing tailbone redness deserve prompt attention.

  Learning objectives for this unit are to: describe resident-centered care as support for physical, psychological, social, recreational, and spiritual needs; identify human needs that may appear during routine CNA care; summarize common aging changes while recognizing reportable changes; connect body systems to CNA observations; recognize complications of immobility; identify community and facility resources that support quality of life; and use respectful person-first language.

  Your role is active but scope-limited. You do not diagnose depression, infection, dementia, malnutrition, or pressure injury. You do notice, assist, report, and document facts. You support privacy, offer choices when possible, use the care plan, and ask the nurse when a change, refusal, safety concern, or unmet need appears.

moodle_description: Explains resident-centered care as daily support for physical, safety, belonging, esteem, recreational, and spiritual needs.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after U05 overview page is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify examples of human needs that a CNA may notice during ordinary care.
content_body: |
  Resident-centered care means the resident is treated as a person first, not as a diagnosis, room assignment, task list, or schedule problem. CNAs often provide the most frequent contact in long-term care, so small choices can protect dignity. Asking whether the resident prefers the blue sweater or the gray sweater, closing the privacy curtain, explaining before touch, waiting for a response, and inviting the resident to wash the face independently are not extra favors. They are part of respectful care.

  Human needs show up in ordinary moments. Physical needs include food, fluid, rest, warmth, hygiene, toileting, mobility, pain reporting, skin protection, and safe positioning. Safety needs include call light access, footwear, clear pathways, proper device use, privacy, and help before fatigue leads to a fall. Psychosocial needs include feeling seen, heard, safe, and connected. Social and recreational needs may involve activities, music, gardening, reading, games, religious services, resident council, phone calls, or quiet companionship. Spiritual needs may involve prayer, chaplain visits, cultural practices, meaningful objects, or time with family. Esteem needs include being allowed to do what the resident can still do, being addressed by the preferred name, and not being talked over.

  Resident-centered care does not mean the CNA promises everything requested. The care plan, safety rules, infection prevention, diet orders, and nurse direction still apply. The skill is to respond respectfully within those limits. If a resident refuses a shower, a resident-centered response is not to shame or force. It is to ask what is bothering the resident, offer choices allowed by the care plan, protect privacy, report repeated refusal or a change from baseline, and document facts according to policy. If a resident asks for a food item that is not allowed on the current diet, the CNA should not ignore the order. The CNA can acknowledge the preference, check with the nurse, and help the team find safe alternatives.

  Notice how language changes care. "Resident chose to rest after breakfast and declined group activity" is different from "Resident was lazy." "Resident states, 'I feel sad today'" is different from diagnosing depression. Words can either preserve dignity or create labels that follow a resident through the chart. In this unit, practice using person-first, observation-based language.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Aging Changes, Baseline, and Body Systems

estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 2 — Aging Changes Are Not Automatic Explanations
moodle_description: Reviews body organization, major body systems, common aging changes, and why CNAs compare new findings to baseline.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and look for the difference between expected age-related change and reportable change from baseline.
content_body: |
  Module 13 asks the CNA to understand the resident as a whole person and to connect observations to basic body-system knowledge. The body is organized from cells to tissues, organs, systems, and the whole person. A change in one system can affect another. Pain can reduce mobility. Reduced mobility can affect skin, lungs, appetite, mood, bowel function, and sleep. Dehydration can affect blood pressure, cognition, urinary output, skin, and fall risk. This is why a CNA's everyday observations matter.

  Aging can bring gradual changes. Skin may become thinner, drier, and more fragile. Vision and hearing may decline. Muscle mass and balance may decrease. Joints may become stiffer. The heart and blood vessels may respond more slowly to position changes. Lungs may have less reserve. Digestion may slow. Bladder urgency or nighttime urination may increase. Taste, smell, thirst, sleep pattern, and temperature regulation may change. Some residents have memory changes or slower processing. These changes can influence how care is offered, but they should not be used to dismiss a new concern.

  Baseline means the resident's usual condition or pattern. For one resident, walking slowly with a walker may be baseline. For another resident, suddenly needing two people for a transfer may be a major change. A resident who always prefers quiet time after lunch is different from a resident who suddenly withdraws from favorite activities for several days. A resident who has chronic dry skin still needs reporting if a new open area, persistent redness, drainage, warmth, swelling, pain, or odor appears. The CNA does not decide the medical cause, but the CNA helps the nurse recognize what changed.

  Body-system observations within CNA scope include skin color, temperature, moisture, bruising, redness, tears, pressure areas, swelling, and pain statements for the integumentary system; movement, strength, posture, stiffness, contracture risk, gait, and transfer ability for the musculoskeletal system; alertness, speech, mood, behavior, confusion, dizziness, and sleep pattern for the nervous system; breathing effort, cough, shortness of breath, and activity tolerance for the respiratory system; swelling, skin color, fatigue, and dizziness for circulation; appetite, swallowing concerns, nausea, vomiting, bowel pattern, and abdominal discomfort statements for digestion; urinary frequency, pain statements, odor, color, and output changes for the urinary system.

  Report patterns as well as single urgent findings. "Ate 25 percent breakfast three days in a row" is more helpful than "doesn't eat." "Needed two rest breaks walking to the bathroom today; usually walks without stopping" is more helpful than "getting old." Good CNA observation protects residents because it gives licensed staff timely, factual information.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Immobility Complications and CNA Prevention Support

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3

unit_id: U05
activity_id: U05_BOOK_SEGMENT_03
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 3 — Why Staying in Bed Can Become a Whole-Body Problem
moodle_description: Describes immobility complications and CNA actions that support prevention within the care plan.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify which immobility risks require CNA observation, care-plan support, and reporting.
content_body: |
  Immobility means reduced movement. It may happen because of illness, pain, weakness, fear of falling, depression, cognitive change, restraint use, equipment problems, or a resident's belief that staying still is safer. Even a short period of decreased movement can affect multiple body systems. The CNA cannot prescribe exercises or change mobility status independently, but the CNA can follow the care plan, encourage safe movement as assigned, reposition as directed, protect skin, provide needed items within reach, and report changes early.

  Skin is often the first visible warning area. Pressure over bony areas such as the tailbone, heels, hips, elbows, ankles, shoulders, and back of the head can reduce circulation and contribute to pressure injury. Friction and shear can occur when a resident slides down in bed or is pulled across linens. Moisture from perspiration, urine, stool, or wound drainage increases risk. CNAs should observe during bathing, dressing, toileting, turning, and linen changes. New redness, purple or dark discoloration, warmth, swelling, blisters, open areas, drainage, odor, pain, or a resident statement that an area burns or hurts should be reported promptly. Do not massage reddened bony areas unless specifically directed by approved policy; report them.

  Muscles and joints also change with inactivity. Weakness can increase fall risk. Joints may become stiff. Contractures can develop when muscles shorten and joints lose movement. If range-of-motion activity is assigned, the CNA performs only what has been taught, delegated, and care-plan approved. Pain, resistance, new limitation, swelling, or a device that does not fit should be reported rather than forced.

  Lungs, circulation, digestion, and elimination are affected too. Lying still may contribute to shallow breathing, mucus retention, pneumonia risk, dizziness with position changes, edema, blood-clot concerns, constipation, urinary stasis, incontinence episodes, appetite decline, and sleep disturbance. Psychosocial effects can include boredom, loneliness, loss of confidence, grief, withdrawal, and reduced motivation. A resident who no longer attends activities may be physically tired, embarrassed by incontinence, afraid of falling, in pain, grieving, or experiencing a medical change. The CNA's job is not to pick a diagnosis. The job is to notice the pattern, offer respectful support, and report what is seen, heard, measured, and stated.

  Prevention support is practical: answer call lights, keep the call light and water within allowed reach, use correct positioning devices, encourage the resident to participate in ADLs, assist with walking or transfer only as assigned, keep skin clean and dry, change wet linens promptly, float heels only as directed, turn and reposition per care plan, report poor intake and fluid concerns, and document care accurately. These actions may look routine, but they protect comfort, dignity, and health.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Community Resources, Preferences, and Reporting Pathways

estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 4 — Quality of Life Is Part of Safe Long-Term Care
moodle_description: Connects resident preferences, community resources, interdisciplinary support, and scope-safe reporting/documentation.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 3 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and consider how a CNA helps connect a resident's unmet need to the right team member or resource.
content_body: |
  Resident-centered care includes quality of life. Long-term care residents may need medical support, but they also need relationships, routines, meaningful activity, privacy, comfort, cultural respect, and a voice in decisions. Community and facility resources can help meet those needs. Examples include activity staff, social services, therapy, dietary services, restorative nursing, spiritual care, family or chosen support persons, resident council, volunteer programs, transportation resources, language services, ombudsman information, local aging services, and approved recreational or cultural programs. The CNA usually does not arrange these resources independently, but the CNA may be the first person to hear the unmet need.

  A resident may say, "I miss going outside," "I cannot hear the music group anymore," "I don't want to eat alone," "I wish someone from my faith community could visit," or "I used to garden every morning." These statements are not small talk only. They can guide care-plan discussions, activity planning, social service follow-up, and emotional support. The CNA should listen, acknowledge, and report through the correct channel. If the resident's request conflicts with safety or care-plan limits, the CNA can still protect dignity by explaining that the nurse or appropriate team member will be asked.

  Person-first reporting uses both preference and observation. For example: "Resident states, 'I miss the garden group.' Resident stayed in bed for the third morning and declined breakfast group. Ate 25 percent. New reddened area over tailbone observed during morning care. Nurse notified." This report avoids labels, includes a direct quote, compares to a pattern, identifies a reportable physical concern, and shows notification. It does not diagnose depression, pressure injury, or infection.

  Documentation should be timely, factual, approved by facility policy, and within CNA scope. Include what you observed, what the resident stated, what care you provided, what was refused, and who was notified. Avoid blame, jokes, assumptions, and labels such as lazy, difficult, manipulative, senile, or attention-seeking. If you are unsure whether something should be reported, ask the nurse. Reporting protects residents; it does not mean the CNA has failed.

  As you move into the scenario, remember the central U05 question: how can you protect the whole resident while staying within CNA role? The answer usually combines respect for preference, baseline comparison, body-system observation, timely reporting, and care-plan support.

learner_visible_title: Scenario — Sorting Preference, Observation, Skin Concern, and Diagnosis
moodle_description: Branching H5P or Moodle Lesson with three decision points that require scope-safe resident-centered judgments.
completion_tracking: Require completion of all three decision points and either a passing interaction score of at least 80 percent or completion of assigned remediation screens.
restrict_access: Available after all four U05 book lesson segments are complete.
gradebook_setting: Practice grade or pass/fail completion; do not count as certificate evidence by itself until the full unit gate is complete.
learner_instructions: |
  Work through the fictional scenario. Choose the safest CNA response at each decision point. If you choose an unsafe option, read the feedback and remediation prompt before continuing.
content_body: |
  **Scenario setup:** Resident E usually attends the morning gardening group, eats more than half of breakfast, jokes with tablemates, and transfers slowly but steadily with the assigned assistance. This week Resident E has stayed in bed most mornings. During morning care today, Resident E says, "I just don't feel up to people." You notice breakfast intake is about 25 percent, movement is slower than the usual pattern, and there is a new reddened area over the tailbone. The care plan includes pressure-injury prevention measures and encouragement to attend preferred activities as tolerated.

  **Decision Point 1 — First resident-centered response:** What should the CNA do first during morning care?

  A. Ask permission before care, offer choices allowed by the care plan, observe the skin concern, compare with the usual pattern, and report the change to the nurse.  
  B. Tell Resident E that activities are required and insist on getting up immediately because staying in bed is a bad habit.  
  C. Decide Resident E is depressed or just aging, skip the report, and let the resident remain in bed without follow-up.

  **Correct feedback for A:** This response respects choice while recognizing reportable change. It includes privacy, baseline comparison, physical and psychosocial observation, care-plan support, and timely reporting.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B or C:** Forcing participation ignores dignity and may increase risk. Diagnosing or dismissing change is outside CNA scope and can delay nursing assessment.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Rewrite the response using four verbs: ask, observe, report, support.

  **Decision Point 2 — Sort the information:** Which sorting choice is most accurate?

  A. "Likes gardening group" is a resident preference; "ate 25 percent" and "moved slower" are observations; "new tailbone redness" is a reportable skin concern; "depressed" is not CNA documentation.  
  B. "Likes gardening group" is a diagnosis; "tailbone redness" is a preference; "ate 25 percent" should be ignored unless the resident complains.  
  C. "Stayed in bed" proves the resident is noncompliant; "slower movement" is normal aging and does not need reporting.

  **Correct feedback for A:** Good sorting separates preference, factual observation, reportable skin concern, and diagnostic wording.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B or C:** Mislabeling information can lead to disrespectful documentation and missed changes from baseline. CNAs report facts; licensed staff evaluate causes.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Place each item into one column: preference, physical observation, psychosocial observation, reportable skin concern, or diagnosis/label to avoid.

  **Decision Point 3 — Best report wording:** Which report best fits CNA scope?

  A. "Resident is lazy, refuses life, and probably has depression and a pressure sore."  
  B. "No report is needed because older residents often slow down and stay in bed."  
  C. "Resident stayed in bed for the third morning, ate about 25 percent of breakfast, moved slower than usual, stated 'I just don't feel up to people,' and has new tailbone redness observed during care; nurse notified."

  **Correct feedback for C:** This report is factual, compares to baseline, includes the resident's words, identifies a reportable skin concern, and records notification without diagnosis.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A or B:** Labels and diagnoses are outside CNA documentation. Dismissing changes as age-related can delay care.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Remove labels, add direct observations, include the resident quote, and state who was notified.

  **Completion condition:** The interaction is complete when the learner answers all three decision points and reviews any remediation screen triggered by an unsafe choice. The preferred configuration is H5P Branching Scenario, H5P Question Set, or Moodle Lesson with completion based on required screens viewed and minimum score. The learner must not enter real resident information.

  **Moodle evidence:** Store interaction attempt, selected choices, score or completion status, remediation viewed status if available, timestamps, and activity completion record.
