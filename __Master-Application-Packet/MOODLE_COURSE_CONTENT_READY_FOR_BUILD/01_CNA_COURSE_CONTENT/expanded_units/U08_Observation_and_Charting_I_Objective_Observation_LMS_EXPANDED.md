content_body:

Observation is one of the most important CNA responsibilities because many resident changes are first noticed during routine care. You may see a resident move differently during transfer, hear a new cough during breakfast, smell urine on clothing, feel unusual warmth when assisting with hygiene, or hear the resident say, “I feel dizzy.” Those details are useful only when they are noticed, reported, and documented clearly.

In this unit, **objective observation** means information that can be seen, heard, smelled, measured, or otherwise directly observed. **Subjective information** is what the resident reports or what another person says. Both types of information can matter. The key is to label them honestly. A note that says “Resident is lazy today” is not objective because it judges motivation. A stronger note says, “Resident declined morning walk twice and stated, ‘My legs feel heavy.’ Nurse notified.”

By the end of U08, you should be able to define observation, identify senses used in observation, sort information as objective or subjective, avoid interpretations and diagnoses, use approved terms and abbreviations safely, and rewrite vague language into factual CNA-level reporting. This online lesson does not replace facility documentation training or policy review. Always follow the care plan, the nurse’s direction, and local approved abbreviation list.

Learning objectives:
- Define observation as an intentional CNA skill.
- Identify sight, hearing, smell, touch within assigned care, measurement, and resident statements as sources of observation data.
- Distinguish objective facts, subjective statements, measurements, and interpretations.
- Use terminology and abbreviations only when they are approved and understood.
- Report changes promptly and document facts without PHI or diagnosis.

moodle_description: Book page segment covering observation terminology, word elements, and scope-safe language.  
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; require sequential Book navigation if configured locally.  
restrict_access: Available after overview page completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Read this segment and notice how specific terms make a CNA report clearer without turning the CNA into a diagnostician.  
content_body:

Terminology is the shared language of the care team. A term does not need to be complicated to be valuable. Words such as “redness,” “swelling,” “refused,” “grimaced,” “unsteady,” “wet cough,” “pale,” “clear,” “cloudy,” “loose stool,” “formed stool,” “shortness of breath,” and “denies pain” help the nurse understand what you observed. When terms are used accurately, the report is faster to understand and easier to act on.

Medical words often include word elements: prefixes at the beginning, roots in the middle, suffixes at the end, and combining vowels that connect parts. CNAs are not expected to diagnose based on word parts, but recognizing common elements can help you understand directions and report correctly. For example, “hyper-” often means high or excessive, “hypo-” often means low or below, “brady-” often means slow, “tachy-” often means fast, “-itis” often relates to inflammation, and “-algia” relates to pain. If you are unsure what a term means in a resident’s care plan, ask the nurse rather than guessing.

Terminology should support facts, not replace them. “Edema” may be an approved term for swelling, but a useful CNA report still describes what was observed: “new swelling noted around both ankles; socks left deep marks; resident states shoes feel tight.” “Cyanosis” may be an approved term for bluish color, but the report should identify what you saw and where: “lips appeared bluish while resident was short of breath.” Clear terms help because they connect your observation to the nurse’s assessment process.

Scope-safe language avoids conclusions you are not assigned to make. Do not write “resident has infection,” “resident is depressed,” “resident is faking,” or “resident has low blood sugar” unless you are quoting what a licensed clinician told you to document according to policy. Instead, record the facts that led you to report: temperature reading if assigned, behavior change, intake amount, resident statement, skin appearance, mobility change, or other direct observation. Good terminology makes your report stronger because it is precise, respectful, and within your role.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Observation Uses Senses and Comparison to Baseline

moodle_description: Book page segment explaining intentional observation, senses used to observe, and comparison to the resident's usual pattern.  
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; require sequential Book navigation if configured locally.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 1 completion.

learner_instructions: Read this segment and think about how ordinary care tasks create observation opportunities.  
content_body:

Observation is active, not accidental. A CNA observes while assisting with bathing, dressing, grooming, toileting, eating, repositioning, transfers, ambulation, activities, and rest. The goal is not to search for problems with suspicion; the goal is to notice changes that may affect comfort, safety, dignity, or health. A resident who is usually cheerful but is now withdrawn, a resident who usually transfers with one-person assist but now cannot bear weight, or a resident who usually eats most of breakfast but now takes only two bites may need prompt reporting.

CNAs use several senses while staying within assigned care. **Sight** helps you notice color, posture, movement, facial expression, skin condition, appetite, fluid intake, spills, hazards, and changes in grooming. **Hearing** helps you notice coughing, wheezing, unusual speech, crying, moaning, repeated calls for help, or statements such as “I feel dizzy” or “My chest hurts.” **Smell** may reveal urine, stool, emesis, body odor, smoke, alcohol-like odor, wound drainage odor, or environmental hazards. **Touch** is used only within assigned care and infection-control practice, such as noticing that skin feels unusually warm during hygiene or that clothing is damp while assisting to change. Measurement adds another source of observation when you are assigned to record intake, output, weight, temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure, pain rating, meal percentage, or ADL assistance.

Good observation compares the current situation with the resident’s baseline, meaning the usual condition or pattern known to the care team. Baseline can include usual appetite, sleep, mood, speech, walking ability, toileting pattern, skin color, level of assistance, pain pattern, participation in activities, or preferred routine. A single observation may be important, but a change from baseline often makes the observation more meaningful. “Resident ate 25 percent” is useful. “Resident usually eats 90 percent but ate 25 percent at breakfast and lunch today” is stronger because it shows a pattern.

Observation also respects privacy. You do not expose a resident unnecessarily to “check everything,” discuss findings in public areas, take photos for practice, or enter real examples into Moodle. You report through the proper care channel and document in the approved record. The resident’s dignity remains part of every observation.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Objective, Subjective, Measurement, and Interpretation

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3  
lane: CNA renewal support — online CE only  
unit_id: U08  
activity_id: U08_BOOK_SEGMENT_03  
estimated_time_minutes: 15  
learner_visible_title: Segment 3: Sort the Information Before You Chart  
moodle_description: Book page segment teaching objective versus subjective information and how to avoid interpretation.  
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; require sequential Book navigation if configured locally.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 2 completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Read this segment and practice separating what you observed from what you think might be happening.  
content_body:

Before reporting or charting, sort the information. **Objective information** is directly observed: “resident gripped the bedrail,” “skin appeared pale,” “respirations sounded wet,” “tray contained most of the meal,” or “urine observed on pants.” **Subjective information** is reported by the resident or another person: “resident stated, ‘I feel dizzy,’” “resident says the left knee hurts,” or “family member reports the resident slept poorly.” **Measurement** is a number, amount, time, or rating collected as assigned: “ate 10 percent,” “pain 7 out of 10,” “voided 250 mL,” or “walked 20 feet with walker and gait belt as care planned.” **Interpretation** is a conclusion, label, or diagnosis: “resident is lazy,” “resident is probably infected,” “resident is confused because of medication,” or “resident is pretending.” Interpretation is the category most likely to create unsafe documentation.

Subjective information is not “bad” or less important. A resident’s statement can be the most important thing you report. The safe charting practice is to identify it as a statement rather than convert it into your conclusion. If the resident says, “I feel dizzy,” write that the resident stated those words. Do not write “resident is hypoglycemic” unless that is part of a licensed assessment or approved documentation process outside CNA judgment. If the resident says, “I am scared to stand,” that statement matters and should be reported respectfully.

Objective charting uses neutral words. Compare these examples. Vague: “Resident acted weird at breakfast.” Stronger: “At breakfast, resident stared at tray for several minutes, answered questions slowly, gripped table when standing, and stated, ‘I feel dizzy.’ Nurse notified.” Vague: “Resident refused care and was difficult.” Stronger: “Resident declined shower twice, pulled blanket to chin, and stated, ‘I do not want a shower today.’ Offered washcloth and privacy; nurse notified per care plan.” Vague: “Resident smelled bad.” Stronger: “Strong urine odor noted on clothing during morning care; pants damp; perineal care offered and nurse notified.”

The strongest reports often include both subjective and objective details: what the resident said, what you saw or heard, what was measured, when it happened, what assistance was provided, and who was notified. Your words should help the licensed nurse understand the situation quickly without guessing what you meant.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Approved Abbreviations and Factual Report Wording

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 4  
lane: CNA renewal support — online CE only  
unit_id: U08  
activity_id: U08_BOOK_SEGMENT_04  
estimated_time_minutes: 15  
learner_visible_title: Segment 4: Use Approved Abbreviations Carefully  
moodle_description: Book page segment on abbreviation safety, approved terms, and concise factual reporting.  
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; require sequential Book navigation if configured locally.  
restrict_access: Available after Segment 3 completion.  
gradebook_setting: None.  
learner_instructions: Read this segment and identify when writing words out is safer than guessing at an abbreviation.  
content_body:

Abbreviations save time only when everyone understands them the same way. In health care, an unclear abbreviation can cause serious mistakes. Some abbreviations are approved in one facility but not in another, and some are prohibited because they can be misread. A CNA should use only the abbreviations approved by the employer, documentation system, care plan, and nurse instruction. If you are unsure, write the words out or ask before using the abbreviation.

Common documentation systems may include approved abbreviations for activities of daily living, intake and output, vital signs, assistance levels, diet textures, mobility devices, or resident responses. Even then, context matters. “Amb” may be understood as ambulation in one system, but if it is not on the approved list, writing “walked” is safer. “SOB” can mean shortness of breath in many settings, but it can also be misunderstood or considered inappropriate if not approved. A note that says “resident SOB” is less clear than “resident stated, ‘I cannot catch my breath’; respirations appeared labored; nurse notified.”

Never invent abbreviations because you are in a hurry. “DZ” for dizzy, “WIRD” for weird, or “NFD” for not feeling different may mean something to the writer but not to the nurse, surveyor, or next shift. Do not use texting shortcuts, emojis, sarcasm, slang, blame, or labels. Do not write in a way that exposes private information beyond the approved record. Documentation is a professional communication tool and a legal record.

A useful CNA report can follow a simple pattern: **who/what changed, what the resident said, what you observed or measured, what you did within the care plan, and who you notified.** For example: “During 0730 transfer, resident stated, ‘I feel dizzy,’ gripped bedrail, and appeared pale. Assisted resident to sit per safety procedure and notified licensed nurse immediately.” This wording separates subjective statement, objective observation, action within role, and notification. It does not diagnose the cause. It gives the nurse enough information to assess and decide next steps.

When charting after reporting, follow facility timing and correction policies. Routine documentation should never replace urgent reporting. If a change could affect resident safety, tell the nurse promptly, then document according to policy. Factual language protects the resident, the team, and your CNA scope.

content_body:

**Scenario setup:** During a fictional morning care situation, Resident H stands from the bed and says, “I feel dizzy.” The resident grips the bedrail, appears pale, and lowers slowly back to the mattress with your standby assistance. The breakfast tray from 0730 shows only a few bites eaten, and the meal tracking field shows 10 percent intake. You are not asked to diagnose the cause. Your task is to decide what is objective, what is subjective, what is a measurement, what is interpretation, and what should be reported.

**Sorting table content for H5P or Lesson build:**
- “Resident stated, ‘I feel dizzy’” = subjective statement.
- “Resident gripped bedrail while standing” = objective observation.
- “Resident appeared pale” = objective observation.
- “Breakfast intake 10 percent” = measurement.
- “Resident probably has low blood sugar” = interpretation/diagnosis; do not chart as CNA conclusion.
- “Resident was acting weird” = vague label; rewrite.
- “Assisted resident to sit and notified licensed nurse” = CNA action and notification.

**Decision point 1 — What is the safest first response?**  
Choice A: Ignore the statement because dizziness is subjective.  
Choice B: Assist the resident to a safe position as trained, observe facts, and notify the licensed nurse promptly.  
Choice C: Chart “probably low blood sugar” before telling anyone.  
Correct feedback for Choice B: Correct. A subjective dizziness statement plus objective pallor, gripping the bedrail, and poor intake should be reported promptly. You stay within CNA scope by assisting for safety, observing, reporting, and documenting facts.  
Unsafe-choice feedback for Choices A and C: Unsafe. Subjective information can be urgent, and diagnosing the cause is outside CNA scope. Delayed reporting or diagnostic charting can place the resident at risk.  
Remediation prompt: Return to the scenario and identify one resident statement, two direct observations, and one measurement before choosing again.

**Decision point 2 — Which note is most factual?**  
Choice A: “Resident was weird and probably needed sugar.”  
Choice B: “Resident stated, ‘I feel dizzy,’ gripped bedrail while standing, appeared pale, ate 10 percent of breakfast, assisted to sit, nurse notified.”  
Choice C: “Resident refused to cooperate and made morning care difficult.”  
Correct feedback for Choice B: Correct. This wording includes the resident’s statement, objective observations, a measurement, CNA action, and notification without blame or diagnosis.  
Unsafe-choice feedback for Choices A and C: Unsafe. These choices use labels, assumptions, or blame and do not communicate the facts needed by licensed staff.  
Remediation prompt: Rewrite the note by starting with what the resident said, then what you saw, then the measured intake, then who was notified.

**Decision point 3 — How should abbreviations be handled?**  
Choice A: Use any abbreviation that saves time, such as “DZ” for dizzy.  
Choice B: Use only facility-approved abbreviations; if unsure, write the words out.  
Choice C: Use texting shortcuts because this is only an online practice note.  
Correct feedback for Choice B: Correct. Abbreviations are safe only when approved and understood. Writing words out is better than guessing.  
Unsafe-choice feedback for Choices A and C: Unsafe. Invented abbreviations and texting shortcuts can be misunderstood and are not professional charting.  
Remediation prompt: Review the abbreviation segment and replace any unapproved abbreviation with plain words.
