## Learner-Facing Module Purpose

M10 is the integrated capstone for the RN case management course. The module asks the learner to combine assessment, care planning, coordination, documentation, patient advocacy, ethical boundaries, utilization communication, quality escalation, and high-risk patient management into one case-management workflow. The purpose is to practice how a registered nurse case manager thinks across competing risks instead of treating each task as isolated. In a complex case, the safest next step may depend on medication access, symptoms, functional limitations, caregiver reliability, payer communication, transition timing, and what has already been documented. The learner will use a fictional, de-identified case to decide what is urgent, what can be coordinated routinely, what must be escalated, and what must be preserved in the record.

By the end of M10, the learner should be ready to complete a final documentation packet and a final knowledge assessment that test application, not recall alone. The capstone emphasizes clear nursing reasoning: identify the relevant risk, connect it to the patient's goals and current condition, choose a coordination action within RN scope, communicate with the right party, document factual results, and evaluate whether the follow-up plan closes the loop. It also reinforces course completion guardrails. Even when a learner passes the module, certificate release remains blocked until provider approval metadata and completion evidence are available and verified.

## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:

- Explain how integrated RN case management connects assessment findings, patient goals, care coordination, documentation, and follow-up.
- Assess competing clinical, functional, medication, psychosocial, payer, and safety risks in a complex transition case.
- Prioritize case management actions when urgent safety concerns, patient preferences, resource barriers, and documentation requirements compete.
- Document a final capstone packet using factual, timely, audit-ready RN case management language.
- Coordinate interprofessional, payer, community, and caregiver communication while maintaining patient-centered nursing accountability.
- Escalate high-risk findings, ethical concerns, unresolved barriers, and quality events through appropriate channels.
- Evaluate final assessment readiness and certificate guardrails using Moodle completion evidence and provider approval metadata requirements.

content_body:
Integrated assessment begins with collecting data from multiple sources and judging how reliable, current, and relevant each source is. In RN case management, assessment is not limited to a symptom checklist. It includes the patient's reported concerns, caregiver observations, medication access, functional ability, home environment, transportation, follow-up appointments, payer requirements, equipment availability, health literacy, and evidence of change in condition. The capstone asks you to consider how these findings interact.

For example, a patient who was recently discharged after heart failure treatment may report mild shortness of breath, missed diuretic doses because the prescription was not picked up, uncertainty about sodium instructions, and no scale at home. Each fact matters, but the integrated assessment looks at the combined risk. Missed medication, limited monitoring, unclear teaching, and new symptoms together suggest a higher-risk transition than any single item would show alone.

The RN case manager should separate known facts from assumptions. A caregiver may say the patient "seems worse," but the documentation should clarify what was observed or reported: increased fatigue, new swelling, confusion about medication timing, or inability to attend a follow-up appointment. A payer portal may show that a service is pending, but that does not mean the patient is safe while waiting. A hospital discharge summary may list instructions, but the RN still evaluates whether the patient understands and can carry them out.

Integrated assessment also requires scope awareness. The RN case manager does not diagnose a new medical condition or independently change a treatment order. The RN does identify concerning findings, provide education within scope, coordinate follow-up, communicate with the provider or team, and escalate according to urgency. When the record shows the assessment source, patient-care relevance, RN action, and follow-up plan, it supports both safe care and audit-ready review.

In the capstone, read every case detail with three questions in mind: What risk does this create for the patient? What RN case management action is appropriate now? What evidence must be documented so the next person can understand the decision?

content_body:
Prioritization in M10 requires more than ranking tasks by convenience or chronology. The RN case manager weighs severity, likelihood of harm, time sensitivity, reversibility, patient goals, available support, and scope of practice. A routine equipment request may become urgent if the lack of equipment prevents safe medication administration or ambulation. A payer authorization issue may become a safety issue if a delayed service leaves the patient without needed monitoring. A documentation gap may become urgent if it hides an unresolved risk from the care team.

A useful capstone approach is to group findings into immediate safety concerns, same-day coordination needs, routine follow-up needs, and documentation or quality review needs. Immediate safety concerns include symptoms suggesting a change in condition, medication errors with potential harm, inability to obtain essential medications, unsafe home circumstances, or signs that the patient cannot follow the discharge plan. Same-day coordination may include provider notification, pharmacy contact, home health scheduling, durable equipment follow-up, transportation support, or caregiver teaching. Routine follow-up may include education reinforcement, resource referrals, and verification that planned services occurred.

Patient goals still matter when risk is high. A patient may decline a recommended service, fear costs, prefer a family-led plan, or want to avoid rehospitalization. RN case management respects autonomy while clarifying risk, offering education, documenting the discussion, and escalating when safety concerns remain unresolved. The learner should avoid framing patient choice as noncompliance without describing what was explained, what the patient understood, what barriers existed, and what follow-up was offered.

Prioritization also includes deciding what not to do. The RN should not promise payer approval, guarantee service availability, ignore unresolved symptoms because paperwork is pending, or delay documentation until every barrier is solved. The safer path is to act on the highest risk within RN scope, communicate through the correct channel, document the facts, and set a follow-up trigger. In the final assessment, the best answer often protects patient safety while preserving scope, autonomy, and documentation integrity.

content_body:
Coordination is the practical bridge between assessment and outcomes. In an integrated case, the RN case manager may need to contact the provider about symptoms, confirm pharmacy access, request clarification of discharge instructions, coordinate home health or community resources, help the patient prepare for an appointment, or communicate clinical facts to a payer. Each contact should have a patient-care purpose and a closed-loop follow-up plan.

Closed-loop communication means the RN does not simply send information and assume the problem is solved. The record should show who was contacted, what information was shared, what response was received or remains pending, what the patient was told, and what follow-up is planned. If a message is left for a provider about a concerning change in condition, the RN should define the follow-up interval and escalation path if no response occurs. If a payer receives clinical information, the RN should communicate facts and avoid guaranteeing an authorization outcome.

Ethical boundaries are especially important in the capstone. The patient may choose a plan that differs from what the team recommends. A family member may request information that the patient has not authorized. A payer requirement may conflict with the patient's preferred timing. A caregiver may pressure the patient to accept or refuse services. The RN case manager supports informed participation by explaining risks and options within scope, protecting confidentiality, avoiding coercion, and escalating concerns when patient safety or rights may be affected.

Advocacy does not mean promising a result. It means making patient needs visible, documenting barriers accurately, communicating relevant clinical facts, and helping the team respond. When resources are limited, the RN should identify alternatives, clarify urgency, and document unresolved barriers. When a situation suggests abuse, neglect, unsafe discharge, or significant deterioration, the RN follows applicable policy and escalation pathways rather than handling the issue as a routine referral.

The capstone expects you to show coordination that is timely, factual, patient-centered, and traceable. If a decision cannot be verified in the record, it is difficult to defend as part of a safe case-management plan.

content_body:
The final documentation packet is the learner's evidence of RN case management reasoning. It should not read like a list of disconnected calls. It should tell a clear story: what was assessed, what risks were identified, what goals and preferences were known, what actions were taken, who was contacted, what education was provided, what remains unresolved, and what follow-up is required. The packet should be factual and concise, but complete enough for another qualified reviewer to understand the case.

A strong packet begins with a brief integrated assessment summary. This summary should include the patient's fictional presenting situation, relevant transition context, clinical concerns, medication or treatment barriers, functional or psychosocial risks, patient goals, and available supports. It should avoid unsupported judgments. Instead of writing that a patient is "difficult," document the observable issue: the patient declined the offered service after education, stated a concern about cost, and agreed to a follow-up call after speaking with family.

The care coordination section should connect each action to a risk or goal. For example, contacting the pharmacy is relevant when medication access affects symptom control or readmission risk. Provider notification is relevant when assessment findings suggest a change in condition or clarification is needed. Payer communication is relevant when clinical facts support service review, but the RN should not state or imply that approval is guaranteed. Community resource referral is relevant when transportation, food access, caregiver support, or equipment barriers affect the plan.

The escalation section should show why a pathway was chosen. Immediate escalation may be needed for symptoms, medication errors, unsafe environment, suspected abuse or neglect, or inability to reach a responsible clinician when urgent risk is present. Quality review may be appropriate for near misses, repeated handoff failures, complaints, or system issues. Routine follow-up may be appropriate when risk is stable and a clear next step is scheduled. Documenting the reason prevents the packet from appearing arbitrary.

The final packet should close with evaluation. State whether the immediate case-management risks were reduced, unresolved, or pending. Identify what evidence supports that evaluation and what follow-up remains. content_body:
Scenario setup: The fictional patient is an older adult discharged from an acute-care stay after worsening chronic heart failure and a fall at home. The patient reports fatigue, mild shortness of breath with activity, uncertainty about new medication instructions, limited transportation, and concern about cost. A family caregiver can help evenings but works during the day. The discharge plan includes provider follow-up, medication changes, home monitoring, and possible home health evaluation. No real patient or facility information is used.

Decision Point 1 - First priority after reviewing the case:

Choice A: Treat transportation as the only issue because the appointment is listed first on the discharge summary.

Choice B: Complete an integrated assessment of symptoms, medication access, understanding of instructions, fall risk, support, and follow-up barriers before setting priorities.

Choice C: Tell the patient that home health and payer coverage will be approved because the discharge summary recommends support.

Correct-path feedback: Choice B is the safest path. The RN case manager starts with integrated assessment because symptoms, medication access, fall risk, support, and follow-up barriers interact. Transportation matters, but it may not be the highest risk if the patient has new symptoms or cannot obtain essential medication.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A narrows the case too early and may miss clinical or medication risks. Choice C oversteps payer communication boundaries and creates an unsupported promise.

Remediation prompt: Re-open the scenario summary and list three findings that could increase short-term safety risk before selecting a coordination action.

Decision Point 2 - Communication and coordination:

Choice A: Contact the provider or designated clinical channel about reported symptoms and medication uncertainty, coordinate pharmacy and follow-up support, and document pending responses.

Choice B: Wait until every payer and transportation question is resolved before notifying the clinical team.

Choice C: Ask the caregiver to decide whether medication changes should be followed until the next appointment.

Correct-path feedback: Choice A supports closed-loop coordination. The RN communicates relevant clinical facts through the appropriate channel, addresses medication access, and documents what is pending. This stays within RN scope and protects the patient while the plan is clarified.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice B delays clinical communication despite unresolved symptoms and medication uncertainty. Choice C shifts a clinical decision to a caregiver and does not create a safe escalation pathway.

Remediation prompt: Identify what information should be included in a provider message and what follow-up time frame should be documented if no response is received.

Decision Point 3 - Documentation, ethics, and escalation:

Choice A: Document only that calls were made because detailed notes can wait until the patient improves.

Choice B: Record the patient's concerns as refusal and close the case-management task.

Choice C: Document assessment findings, patient goals, education provided, contacts made, responses pending, escalation rationale, and next follow-up while respecting the patient's choices and confidentiality.

Correct-path feedback: Choice C creates an audit-ready record and supports ethical practice. It captures the patient's perspective, nursing assessment, education, coordination actions, unresolved barriers, and escalation plan without blaming the patient or exposing confidential information.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A leaves the record incomplete during an active risk period. Choice B may misrepresent patient concerns and prematurely close follow-up. Both choices weaken continuity and reviewability.

Remediation prompt: Draft one factual sentence that distinguishes a patient concern from a refusal, then add one follow-up action that keeps the case open until risk is addressed.

Completion condition: The learner must reach the final feedback screen after all three decision points. If the learner selects an unsafe or incomplete path, the activity should show remediation feedback before allowing completion.
