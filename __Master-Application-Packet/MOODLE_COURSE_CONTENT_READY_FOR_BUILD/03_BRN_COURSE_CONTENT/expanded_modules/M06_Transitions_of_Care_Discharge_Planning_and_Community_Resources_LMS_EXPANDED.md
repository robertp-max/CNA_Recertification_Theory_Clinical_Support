## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of M06, the learner will be able to:

- Explain transition risks that can arise after hospital, skilled nursing, clinic, emergency department, or home health changes.
- Assess discharge instructions, medication changes, equipment needs, home safety concerns, caregiver readiness, and follow-up barriers.
- Prioritize patient-care risks that require action within the first 72 hours after a transition.
- Document transition planning, teaching, unresolved barriers, referrals, and escalation steps using factual RN case-management language.
- Coordinate appointments, records, transportation, supplies, durable medical equipment, home safety supports, and community resources.
- Escalate unresolved medication, symptom, equipment, access, safety, or caregiver barriers through the appropriate clinical or administrative pathway.
- Evaluate whether the transition plan supports continuity, patient goals, RN scope, and audit-ready completion evidence.

content_body:
Transitions of care are periods when a patient's risk can increase quickly because responsibility, information, location, services, medications, and routines are changing at the same time. A patient may leave the hospital with new medications, a revised diet, oxygen, wound supplies, therapy orders, follow-up appointments, and instructions that are clear to the discharge team but confusing at home. Another patient may move from skilled nursing to assisted living, return to a caregiver who is overwhelmed, or receive new home health services without the needed equipment in place. M06 treats these transitions as RN case-management events that require clinical attention, not simple scheduling tasks.

The RN case manager contributes by organizing reliable information, identifying what could harm the patient if unresolved, and coordinating next steps with the patient, caregiver, providers, payers, vendors, community partners, and the care team. The RN does not prescribe treatment, guarantee payer approval, or replace emergency response. The RN does assess risk, clarify instructions, recognize discrepancies, teach warning signs, verify follow-up arrangements, identify social and functional barriers, and escalate concerns when the transition plan is not safe or complete.

This module is organized around the first 72 hours after a transition because many avoidable problems appear early. A medication list may not match what the patient has at home. A follow-up appointment may be scheduled but unreachable because transportation is not arranged. Oxygen may be ordered but not delivered. A caregiver may say the instructions are understood while also describing fatigue, poor teach-back, or fear of managing symptoms. A community resource referral may be entered, yet the patient may not be connected to food, housing, utility, mobility, behavioral health, or caregiver-support services. RN case management closes these gaps by verifying, documenting, following up, and escalating.

content_body:
Transition risk begins when the patient's care plan moves from one setting, service, or responsible team to another. The discharge paperwork may be accurate, but the plan can still fail if the patient cannot obtain medications, cannot understand changes, lacks a caregiver, has no transportation, or does not have the equipment needed for safe care. RN case management looks for the gap between the written plan and the patient's real ability to follow it.

Common transition risks include new or changed medications, high-risk diagnoses, recent infection, wound care, oxygen use, anticoagulant therapy, diabetes management, fall risk, cognitive impairment, language barriers, limited health literacy, food insecurity, unstable housing, caregiver strain, and delayed provider follow-up. A patient with several moderate barriers may be at higher risk than a patient with one obvious problem that is already being managed. The RN case manager therefore considers the whole transition, not only the discharge diagnosis.

High-risk information can come from multiple sources: discharge instructions, medication lists, therapy notes, durable medical equipment orders, social work notes, caregiver reports, home health referrals, appointment summaries, payer authorization notes, and the patient's own description of what is happening at home. When sources conflict, the conflict itself becomes a transition risk. For example, if the discharge summary says an inhaler was changed but the patient reports using the old inhaler because the new one was not available, the RN case manager treats that as a medication-access and education problem that needs prompt follow-up.

Risk identification should lead to a clear action. Some risks call for education and teach-back. Others call for provider clarification, pharmacy contact, equipment vendor follow-up, social service referral, transportation coordination, or urgent clinical escalation. The RN case manager documents what was known, how the risk was prioritized, who was contacted, what response was received, and what remains unresolved. This creates continuity for the next team member and supports audit review without overstating what the RN controlled.

content_body:
Discharge reconciliation in RN case management means comparing the written plan with what the patient actually has, understands, and can do. The RN case manager reviews discharge instructions, current medication use, reported symptoms, follow-up appointments, equipment orders, and pending services. The goal is to surface discrepancies early enough to prevent confusion, delayed treatment, avoidable deterioration, or unnecessary readmission.

Medication changes deserve particular attention because patients may continue stopped medications, miss newly prescribed medications, take duplicate therapies, misunderstand tapering instructions, or be unable to pay for a prescription. The RN case manager does not independently change prescriptions. Instead, the RN identifies the discrepancy, assesses potential risk, teaches within scope using available instructions, contacts the appropriate provider or pharmacy for clarification, and documents the follow-up plan. If the discrepancy creates immediate risk, such as duplicate anticoagulant use, severe symptoms, or inability to obtain an essential medication, escalation should occur promptly through the established clinical pathway.

Appointment coordination is more than listing dates. A safe transition plan confirms the appointment type, date, time, location or telehealth access, transportation, required records, caregiver involvement, and the patient's understanding of why the visit matters. A follow-up appointment in seven days may still be unsafe if the patient has worsening symptoms today, cannot travel, or does not know which provider to call. The RN case manager verifies whether the appointment matches the level of risk and whether additional same-day contact is needed.

Closed-loop coordination means the RN does not assume that sending a message completed the task. The RN confirms receipt when appropriate, notes pending responses, follows up when barriers remain, and records alternate plans if the first option fails. If the primary care office cannot schedule timely follow-up, the RN documents the attempt, communicates the risk, and uses the designated escalation pathway. If the patient lacks transportation, the RN connects the appointment need to transportation resources rather than treating the barrier as unrelated.

content_body:
Equipment and supplies often determine whether a discharge plan is usable. A patient may be clinically ready to leave a facility but unsafe at home if oxygen delivery is delayed, the walker is the wrong size, wound supplies are missing, a glucose meter is not available, or the caregiver does not know how to obtain refills. RN case management verifies these practical elements because they affect treatment, mobility, symptom monitoring, infection prevention, and fall risk.

Durable medical equipment and supply coordination should be tied to the patient's condition and discharge instructions. The RN case manager identifies what was ordered, what was delivered, what the patient is using, what training was provided, and what remains pending. If an item is delayed, the RN assesses the patient-care impact and coordinates with the vendor, provider, payer, home health agency, or responsible department. Documentation should distinguish between "order sent," "delivery confirmed," "patient using correctly," and "barrier unresolved." These are different levels of completion.

Home safety assessment is also part of transition planning. The RN case manager may learn that the patient sleeps upstairs but cannot climb stairs, lacks refrigeration for medication, has poor lighting, has loose rugs, cannot bathe safely, or has no caregiver available overnight. The RN does not need to solve every social or environmental problem personally, but the RN should recognize when the environment undermines the plan. Appropriate actions may include referral to home health, therapy, social work, community agencies, caregiver support, transportation programs, meal services, or emergency resources depending on urgency.

Supplies and home safety issues require escalation when the barrier places the patient at immediate risk or prevents an essential treatment from being carried out. For example, oxygen not delivered for a patient discharged on continuous oxygen is not a routine vendor delay. A missing wound dressing may become urgent if the patient has no safe way to cover a surgical site. The RN case manager documents the risk, contacts the responsible party, identifies interim instructions within scope, and escalates unresolved barriers according to policy.

Read the segment and identify how teaching, caregiver capacity, and community resources support safe transitions.

content_body:
Caregiver teaching is effective only when the patient or caregiver can explain what they will do after the RN leaves the conversation. RN case management uses plain language, checks understanding, and focuses teaching on the actions most likely to protect safety during the transition. Teaching may include medication changes, symptom monitoring, diet or activity instructions, wound care reminders, equipment use, appointment expectations, and who to contact for specific concerns.

Teach-back is more reliable than asking, "Do you understand?" The RN case manager might ask the caregiver to describe when the next medication dose is due, what warning signs should trigger a call, how oxygen equipment will be used, or what transportation plan is in place for the follow-up appointment. If the caregiver cannot explain the plan, that is not a failure of the caregiver; it is information the RN uses to adjust teaching, simplify instructions, involve another support person, or escalate to the care team.

Community resources should be selected because they address a barrier that affects health, continuity, or safety. Examples include meal delivery, transportation, medication assistance, housing support, utility support, caregiver respite, disease-specific education, behavioral health support, translation services, mobility resources, home modifications, and local aging or disability services. The RN case manager connects the resource to the patient's need and verifies the next step when possible. A referral that is entered but never reaches the patient may not reduce risk.

Patient goals remain important during transition planning. A patient may value staying at home, attending a family event, avoiding another hospitalization, managing fatigue, or keeping a caregiver relationship stable. The RN case manager aligns coordination with these goals while maintaining safety and scope. If the patient's goal conflicts with the written plan, the RN documents the concern, educates within scope, communicates with the responsible provider or team, and escalates if refusal or lack of support creates a serious risk.

Read the segment and identify which transition barriers should be documented as pending, resolved, or escalated.

content_body:
The first 72 hours after a transition are a critical window for RN case management because the patient is testing the plan in real life. Medications must be obtained and taken correctly, follow-up must be understood, equipment must be present and usable, symptoms must be monitored, and caregiver support must match the patient's needs. A timely follow-up call, portal message, home health coordination touchpoint, or structured review can reveal whether the discharge plan is functioning.

An effective 72-hour follow-up covers current symptoms, medication access and understanding, equipment and supply status, appointment readiness, home safety, caregiver capacity, community-resource connection, and unresolved questions. The RN case manager listens for mismatches: the patient says the appointment is "next week" but cannot name the provider, the caregiver says supplies are "coming soon" but does not know from whom, or the medication list includes a stopped drug still being taken. Each mismatch is sorted by urgency and assigned to a next action.

Escalation is appropriate when a barrier is beyond routine coordination, creates immediate or worsening patient risk, or remains unresolved after reasonable follow-up. Examples include new or worsening symptoms, medication discrepancies that may cause harm, inability to obtain essential equipment, unsafe home conditions, caregiver absence for a dependent patient, repeated failed contact with a needed provider, or a community barrier that prevents treatment. Escalation may involve a provider, supervisor, emergency pathway, home health agency, payer contact, social work, or other designated resource depending on the policy and urgency.

Transition documentation should tell the story of the risk and response. A complete note identifies the transition source, key discharge instructions, patient or caregiver report, discrepancies found, education provided, teach-back results, referrals made, appointments verified, equipment or supply status, unresolved barriers, escalation steps, and follow-up plan. The note should be factual and specific. It should avoid vague phrases such as "handled discharge" or "patient aware" when the real issue is whether the patient can safely carry out the plan. The record should allow another RN, supervisor, auditor, or instructor to see what was known, what was done, what remains pending, and why the chosen priority was appropriate.

Work through the fictional case. Choose the safest RN case-management action at each decision point. Use the feedback and remediation prompts to revise unsafe or incomplete decisions before completing the final summary.

content_body:
Scenario setup: A fictional older adult has returned home after hospitalization for pneumonia and dehydration. The discharge instructions list a new inhaler, an antibiotic, oxygen at night, a primary care follow-up in seven days, and home health nursing. During a 48-hour transition follow-up, the patient reports fatigue and mild shortness of breath with activity. The caregiver says the antibiotic was picked up, but the patient is still using an old inhaler because the new inhaler was not ready. Oxygen was ordered, yet the vendor has not delivered it. The caregiver also says transportation to the follow-up appointment is uncertain because the usual driver is unavailable.

Decision point 1: Medication and instruction reconciliation.

Choice A: Document that the patient has medications at home and wait for the scheduled provider appointment.

Choice B: Compare the discharge medication list with what the patient is actually taking, clarify the inhaler discrepancy with the pharmacy or provider, teach warning signs within scope, and document the unresolved item.

Choice C: Tell the patient to stop all inhalers until the follow-up appointment.

Correct-path feedback: Choice B is the safest RN case-management action because it verifies the discrepancy, uses closed-loop communication, stays within RN scope, and documents the remaining risk. The RN does not independently change prescriptions, but the RN does act on a medication mismatch that could affect respiratory stability.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A treats a medication discrepancy as routine despite respiratory symptoms and a recent pneumonia discharge. Choice C gives a medication order the RN is not authorized to make in this scenario.

Remediation prompt: Identify the medication discrepancy, who should be contacted for clarification, and what should be documented before moving to the next decision.

Decision point 2: Equipment, oxygen, and home safety.

Choice A: Mark the oxygen order complete because the discharge paperwork includes it.

Choice B: Advise the caregiver to purchase oxygen equipment privately without clarifying the order.

Choice C: Verify whether oxygen delivery is scheduled, assess the patient-care risk of the delay, contact the vendor or responsible coordinator, and escalate if oxygen is needed before delivery can occur.

Correct-path feedback: Choice C is correct because transition planning requires confirmation that ordered equipment is available and usable. A written order is not the same as delivery, patient setup, or safe use.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A closes the loop too early and may hide an unresolved safety barrier. Choice B bypasses the ordered process and may create unsafe or unaffordable instructions.

Remediation prompt: State why missing oxygen equipment can be a transition risk and list one documentation phrase that distinguishes "ordered" from "delivered."

Decision point 3: Follow-up, transportation, and unresolved barriers.

Choice A: Note that the appointment is scheduled and take no further action because transportation is a social issue.

Choice B: Confirm the appointment details, identify transportation options or referral pathways, teach when to seek earlier help for worsening symptoms, and escalate unresolved access barriers based on policy and risk.

Choice C: Cancel the appointment because transportation is uncertain.

Correct-path feedback: Choice B is correct because the appointment only supports continuity if the patient can attend it and knows when earlier help is needed. The RN case manager connects the access barrier to health risk and uses appropriate coordination or escalation.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A separates transportation from patient-care continuity even though missed follow-up may increase risk. Choice C removes a planned clinical follow-up without provider direction.

Remediation prompt: Build a same-day follow-up note that includes appointment status, transportation barrier, patient teaching, escalation threshold, and next contact plan.

Completion condition: The learner reaches the final case summary after responding to all three decision points and reviewing remediation feedback when an unsafe or incomplete choice is selected.
