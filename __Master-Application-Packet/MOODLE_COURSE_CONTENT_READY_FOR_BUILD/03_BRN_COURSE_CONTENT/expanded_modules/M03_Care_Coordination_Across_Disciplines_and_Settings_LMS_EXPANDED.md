Behavioral learner objectives:

- Explain how interdisciplinary coordination supports safe RN case management across home health, clinic, hospital, pharmacy, and community settings.
- Assess communication gaps that can leave referrals, handoffs, medication questions, or patient-resource needs unresolved.
- Prioritize coordination actions according to patient safety risk, time sensitivity, scope, and accountability.

- Escalate delayed responses when the delay creates patient safety risk or prevents timely care.
- Evaluate whether the patient was included in the communication plan and whether the team can verify who owns each next step.

content_body:
RN case management coordination is the work of making care understandable, connected, and accountable across people and settings. A patient may receive instructions from a hospital discharge planner, medication information from a pharmacy, orders from a provider, teaching from a home health nurse, exercises from therapy, resource information from a social worker, and transportation support from a community partner. The patient experiences these pieces as one care journey, even when the organizations use different systems. The RN case manager helps reduce the risk that important information is lost between settings or that a patient is left carrying responsibility for a clinical handoff they cannot safely manage alone.

In M03, coordination begins with identifying what must happen and who is the right partner for each next step. The RN does not simply forward information. The RN interprets assessment findings, recognizes patient-care risk, chooses an appropriate communication pathway, and determines whether the issue is routine, time-sensitive, or urgent. For example, a delayed home health therapy evaluation may be routine if the patient is stable and has other supports, but it becomes more serious if the patient has repeated falls, new weakness, no caregiver, and unclear discharge instructions. The same referral can have a different priority when clinical risk changes.

Closed-loop communication is the main practice model for this module. A closed loop includes a clear message, the correct recipient, confirmation that the message was received, confirmation of the planned action or response, and documentation of any pending item. The RN should avoid assuming that a voicemail, fax, portal message, or referral entry automatically produced follow-up. The loop closes only when the next step is known or when the unresolved delay has been escalated according to patient safety risk and organizational policy.

Patient inclusion is also part of coordination. The patient and authorized caregiver should understand what is being coordinated, what to expect, what warning signs or delays to report, and whom to contact when the plan changes. Inclusion does not mean overwhelming the patient with professional language or asking the patient to solve system barriers alone. It means making the plan usable, respectful, and realistic. When language access, hearing, cognition, transportation, health literacy, caregiver limitations, or financial barriers affect follow-through, the RN should coordinate support and document what was addressed.

Read this segment and identify how the RN case manager decides which team member or partner should receive each coordination request.

content_body:
Care coordination starts with disciplined selection of the right partner. The RN case manager reviews the patient's current risks, goals, orders, functional needs, medication concerns, social barriers, and setting of care, then determines which discipline or service is most likely to resolve the barrier safely. A provider may need to clarify a medication order or new symptom. A pharmacist may need to address access, duplicate therapy, refill timing, or administration questions. A therapist may need to evaluate mobility, safety equipment, transfers, or home exercise barriers. A social worker may need to support transportation, food access, caregiver strain, insurance navigation, or community referrals. A home health clinician may need to communicate changes observed in the home. A community resource may need referral details, eligibility information, or follow-up timing.

The RN case manager should not treat all contacts as equal. The clinical question drives the recipient and urgency. If a patient reports increased shortness of breath after a discharge medication change, the primary concern is patient safety and possible change in condition. The RN coordinates provider communication and escalation according to policy rather than only asking whether the pharmacy filled the prescription. If the patient cannot attend a wound clinic appointment because of transportation, the RN coordinates with the patient, caregiver, clinic, and community resource while also considering whether delayed wound care creates clinical risk. If therapy has not started after a fall-related discharge, the RN determines whether the delay threatens mobility, caregiver safety, or readmission risk.

Good discipline selection also protects scope. RN case managers coordinate, assess, educate within scope, communicate clinical concerns, and document follow-up, but they do not independently diagnose new conditions, change provider orders, approve benefits, guarantee community resource availability, or promise payer decisions. When another discipline owns a decision, the RN should name the issue clearly, send relevant facts, request the needed response, and track the result. This preserves nursing accountability without crossing into another professional role.

Team accountability improves when the RN names the owner of the next step. "Therapy pending" is weaker than "Physical therapy intake contacted for evaluation scheduling; intake confirmed referral received and will call patient by next business day; RN to verify appointment date if patient has not received call." The second statement names recipient, status, expected action, and RN follow-up. It gives the next reviewer enough information to continue coordination without repeating the entire search.

The patient remains part of discipline selection. Ask what the patient understands about the plan, what barriers are most likely to prevent follow-through, and whether the patient has preferences or constraints that should shape coordination. A referral that is clinically appropriate but impossible for the patient to access is not yet a usable plan. Safe coordination means matching the clinical need to the right partner and then checking whether the plan can actually be completed by this patient in this setting.

learner_instructions:
Read this segment and compare a one-way message with a closed-loop coordination process.

content_body:
Closed-loop communication is a safety practice that keeps handoffs and referrals from disappearing into the space between systems. In RN case management, the loop begins when the RN identifies a patient-care issue that requires another person, discipline, or setting to act. The RN sends a clear message to the right recipient, requests a specific response or action, confirms receipt, confirms the next step when possible, and documents the outcome. If the recipient does not respond within the time frame required by the patient's risk, the RN escalates rather than waiting indefinitely.

A one-way message may be appropriate for low-risk information sharing, but it is not enough when the issue affects safety, access to care, medication use, follow-up appointments, durable medical equipment, home health services, or transition instructions. For example, sending a referral to a wound clinic does not prove the wound clinic received the referral, accepted the patient, scheduled the appointment, informed the patient, or communicated special instructions. A closed loop asks: Was the referral received? Is anything missing? Who will contact the patient? When should the patient expect an appointment? What should the RN do if there is no appointment by the expected date?

High-quality handoffs include current status, relevant risk, requested action, urgency, and ownership. The recipient should be able to understand why the message matters without reading an entire chart. A concise RN handoff might include the reason for coordination, most relevant assessment finding, patient barrier, requested action, time frame, and callback or confirmation method. For example: "Patient discharged after fall-related hospitalization, reports two near-falls since return home, no therapy start date, lives alone. Please confirm receipt of PT referral and expected evaluation date today. RN will update patient and reassess fall-prevention needs after confirmation." This message is factual, patient-centered, and tied to a coordination outcome.

Closed-loop communication also requires attention to the communication channel. A portal message may be trackable but may not be fast enough for a time-sensitive risk. A phone call may be faster but requires documentation of who received the call and what was agreed. A fax may transmit documents but may require confirmation if missing paperwork could delay care. Secure messaging may be useful within one organization but may not reach a community partner. The RN chooses the channel based on urgency, reliability, privacy, policy, and the need to preserve evidence.

When a loop cannot be closed, the RN documents the barrier and next step. Unresolved does not mean undocumented. The record should show what was attempted, when it was attempted, who was contacted, what response was received or not received, whether the patient was updated, what safety instructions were reinforced, and when escalation or follow-up will occur. This protects continuity because another RN or reviewer can see both the patient-care issue and the remaining accountability.

content_body:
Different partners need different information. Provider communication should be clinically concise and action oriented. The RN case manager reports relevant patient findings, context, and the requested response without diagnosing or changing orders independently. A provider message about a medication concern might include the medication name or class if allowed by policy, the patient's reported symptom or access problem, relevant discharge context, current safety concern, and the response needed. The RN should document the message, provider response, any order clarification received through appropriate channels, patient education provided within scope, and follow-up plan.

Pharmacy coordination often focuses on access and medication safety barriers. The RN may need to clarify whether a prescription was received, whether there is a refill timing problem, whether a prior authorization or payment issue exists, whether packaging or delivery is available, or whether the patient reports confusion about administration. The pharmacist is a key partner for medication questions, but the RN still evaluates patient risk. If a medication access problem could lead to missed high-risk therapy, symptom worsening, or readmission, the RN tracks the issue until resolved or escalated. Documentation should show the pharmacy contact, barrier, pharmacist response, provider notification if needed, patient update, and next follow-up.

Community-resource communication requires a balance between practical support and clinical judgment. Transportation, food access, home-delivered meals, caregiver support, utility assistance, housing referrals, language access, and disease-specific programs can directly affect whether the plan of care works. The RN case manager should not imply that a resource is guaranteed before eligibility is known. Instead, the RN documents the barrier, the resource contacted, what information was provided, expected response time, and any interim safety plan. The RN also evaluates whether delay in the community service creates a clinical problem that requires another pathway.

Communication with therapy, social work, home health, or clinic staff should clarify the clinical reason for the request and the expected next step. A therapy request may need fall history, equipment barriers, home environment concerns, and discharge instructions. A social work request may need the patient-centered barrier, urgency, and what has already been attempted. A clinic handoff may need current symptoms, appointment status, transportation barriers, and whether the patient understands follow-up instructions. The more precise the message, the less likely the recipient must restart the assessment.

The patient should know what is being coordinated and what to expect next. Patient inclusion might include explaining that the RN contacted the pharmacy and will call back after confirmation, teaching the patient which symptoms require urgent help according to policy, confirming the preferred phone number, arranging language assistance, or asking whether the patient can attend the proposed appointment. Inclusion is not an optional courtesy. It helps prevent missed calls, missed appointments, duplicated messages, and unsafe assumptions.

learner_instructions:
Read this segment and identify when a delayed response becomes a patient-safety escalation issue.

content_body:
Delayed responses are common in care coordination. A referral queue may be backed up, a clinic may request missing documents, a pharmacy may wait for provider clarification, a community agency may need eligibility screening, or a home health partner may not have staffing for an immediate visit. The RN case manager's role is not to control every outside system. The RN's role is to recognize when delay affects patient safety, communicate clearly, update the patient, and escalate according to risk and policy.

Escalation begins with assessment of urgency. Consider what could happen if the delay continues. Is the patient missing medication? Is there a new symptom or change in condition? Is the patient unable to perform essential self-care? Is a wound appointment delayed while signs of infection are present? Is equipment missing after a discharge to home? Is the patient or caregiver unable to explain warning signs? Is the next provider appointment too late for the level of risk? These questions help the RN prioritize action rather than treating all pending items the same.

Escalation pathways vary by organization, but the documentation logic is consistent. The record should show the risk, the attempted contact, the elapsed time or missed response, the escalation recipient, the message sent, the response received, patient notification, and follow-up plan. If the RN contacted a provider after a pharmacy could not obtain clarification, the note should connect the medication issue to patient risk. If the RN elevated a delayed therapy referral to a supervisor or intake manager, the note should explain why delay mattered for this patient, such as fall risk, recent hospitalization, or lack of caregiver support.

Accountability improves when the RN avoids vague ownership. "Will follow up" does not tell the team who is responsible, by when, or for what outcome. A stronger plan states the owner and next check: "RN case manager will verify pharmacy resolution by 1500 today; if no response, RN will notify provider office again and update patient on interim safety instructions." When another partner owns the next step, the RN still tracks whether that step occurred when patient risk requires it.

Team accountability also includes respectful communication. Escalation should not be punitive or vague. The RN should use factual, patient-centered language: what is delayed, why it matters, what action is requested, and by when. Clear escalation can strengthen relationships because partners understand the clinical reason for urgency. It also protects the patient from repeated explanations and fragmented instructions.

Read this segment and focus on what another RN should be able to understand from your communication note.

content_body:
Documentation completes the coordination process because it makes communication visible to the next clinician, reviewer, supervisor, or instructor. A communication note should not merely state that a call was made. It should explain the patient-care issue, recipient, message content, response, pending barrier, patient update, and next action. The record should allow another qualified person to answer these questions: What risk or need prompted communication? Who was contacted? What information was sent or discussed? What did the recipient agree to do or say? What remains unresolved? What is the time frame for follow-up? Was the patient included, and what does the patient understand?

Audit-ready communication is factual and timely. It avoids blame, assumptions, personal opinions, and promises outside the RN role. Instead of writing "clinic ignored referral," the RN can document "No appointment confirmation received from clinic after referral sent on date documented in record; RN called clinic scheduling line, spoke with scheduling representative, confirmed referral not yet processed because insurance information was missing; RN resent required information per policy and informed patient that appointment confirmation remains pending." This note is clearer because it identifies the barrier and action without speculation.

Patient inclusion should appear in the record when it affects follow-through. If the RN tells the patient to expect a pharmacy call, confirms the best callback number, arranges interpreter support, or reviews what symptoms require immediate help, those actions belong in documentation. If the patient cannot participate because of cognition, fatigue, language, hearing, or caregiver dependence, document how the RN adapted communication and who was appropriately included. The goal is not to create extra paperwork. The goal is to show that coordination was usable for the patient.

Communication outcomes may be resolved, pending, escalated, or reassigned. Resolved means the requested action occurred or the next step is confirmed. Pending means a barrier remains and follow-up is scheduled. Escalated means the delay or risk was raised through an appropriate pathway. Reassigned means another team member or discipline accepted ownership for the next action. These outcome words help organize notes, but they must be supported by facts. "Resolved" should name what resolved. "Pending" should name what remains pending and when it will be checked. "Escalated" should name who received the escalation and why.

Good documentation also protects contact-hour learning evidence in Moodle. Your assignment submission and simulation choices should show how you think through coordination. Use fictional examples, connect each message to patient risk, name the intended recipient, state confirmation steps, and document the outcome. If the course later undergoes review, these artifacts demonstrate that the learner practiced RN case management judgment rather than only reading about it.

content_body:
Scenario setup: A fictional adult patient recently returned home after a hospital stay. The patient has a pending home health therapy evaluation, a wound clinic appointment that may need transportation support, and a pharmacy question about a newly prescribed high-risk medication. The patient says, "I keep getting calls from different people, but I am not sure who is supposed to help me first." The caregiver is available only in the evenings. The primary care office has not returned the patient's call. The RN case manager reviews the discharge summary, current referral list, patient report, and available medication information. No real patient or facility information is used in this scenario.

Your purpose is to coordinate without leaving open-ended handoffs. The patient has several needs, but not every need has the same urgency. You must identify the most immediate risk, contact the right partner, close the communication loop, include the patient, and document the outcome. Stay within RN case management scope. Do not diagnose a new condition, change an order, promise an approval, or guarantee a community resource. Use factual communication and track unresolved barriers.

Decision point 1: What should the RN case manager prioritize first?

Choice A: Contact the pharmacy and provider office to clarify the medication concern because missed or incorrect use of a high-risk medication could create immediate safety risk.

Correct-path feedback: This is the safest priority. Medication access and understanding can affect immediate patient safety. The RN should communicate the relevant facts, request clarification through proper channels, update the patient, and document the outcome or unresolved barrier.

Choice B: Tell the patient to wait for the primary care office because all post-discharge questions must be handled at the next visit.

Unsafe-choice feedback: This leaves a time-sensitive medication question unresolved and places responsibility on the patient. Waiting may be unsafe when the issue involves a high-risk medication and unclear instructions.

Choice C: Focus only on transportation for the wound clinic because scheduling is easier to solve than medication clarification.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Transportation may matter, but ease of completion does not determine priority. The RN should address the medication concern first if it creates greater immediate risk, while still tracking the wound clinic barrier.

Remediation prompt: Re-rank the problems by potential harm if no one responds today. Write one sentence explaining why the first contact matters for patient safety.

Decision point 2: Which message best supports closed-loop provider/pharmacy communication?

Choice A: "Patient has medication question. Please advise when possible."

Unsafe-choice feedback: The message is too vague. It does not include risk, context, requested action, or time sensitivity, and it may not prompt a reliable response.

Choice B: "Fictional patient discharged with high-risk medication; patient reports unclear instructions and has not started dose. Pharmacy confirms prescription received but needs provider clarification. Please clarify instructions today or identify alternate plan. RN will update patient after response."

Correct-path feedback: This message gives concise clinical context, names the barrier, requests a clear action and time frame, and states the RN follow-up step.

Choice C: "The pharmacy will fix the issue, and the patient can assume the medication is safe once it is filled."

Unsafe-choice feedback: This overpromises and removes the provider clarification step. The RN should not imply safety or order clarification without an appropriate response.

Remediation prompt: Rewrite the message with current status, risk, recipient, requested action, and confirmation step.

Decision point 3: What should the RN document after the first round of coordination?

Choice A: "Called pharmacy and provider. Will monitor."

Unsafe-choice feedback: This does not show the issue, recipient, response, unresolved barrier, patient update, or next follow-up time.

Choice B: "Medication issue pending. Patient aware."

Unsafe-choice feedback: This is incomplete. It does not show what is pending, who owns the response, whether the loop was closed, or when the RN will act again.

Choice C: "RN contacted pharmacy regarding fictional high-risk medication access and instructions; pharmacy confirmed prescription received but provider clarification is needed. RN sent provider office a concise request for same-day clarification, informed patient not to rely on unclear instructions and to follow existing safety guidance per policy, and scheduled follow-up by end of day. If no response, RN will escalate per organizational pathway."

Correct-path feedback: This documentation shows the coordination issue, recipient response, provider request, patient inclusion, follow-up time, and escalation plan.

Remediation prompt: Add the missing documentation elements: date/time per policy, recipient, message content, response, patient update, unresolved barrier, next follow-up, and escalation trigger.

Completion condition: The learner completes all three decision points, views feedback for each choice made, and submits a final one-paragraph summary naming the priority risk, selected recipient, confirmation method, patient update, and documentation outcome.
