## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, the learner will be able to:

- Explain patient autonomy, informed participation, and patient rights in RN case management.
- Assess consent-aware information sharing needs when family members, caregivers, payers, and interdisciplinary partners request updates.
- Prioritize advocacy actions that protect patient welfare without coercion or role overreach.
- Document refusals, preferences, education, confidentiality limits, and escalation steps using respectful nursing language.
- Coordinate language access, culturally humble communication, and resource navigation when barriers affect participation.
- Escalate ethical concerns, boundary risks, privacy concerns, and conflicts of interest through appropriate supervisory, provider, compliance, or ethics channels.
- Evaluate case management decisions for patient-centeredness, scope alignment, confidentiality, and completion evidence readiness.

content_body:

RN case management often takes place in moments when patients, families, clinicians, agencies, and payers are trying to move care forward under time pressure. Ethical practice requires more than speed. The RN case manager must protect the patient's right to receive information, ask questions, accept help, refuse help, decide who may receive updates, and participate in planning according to capacity, preference, and applicable policy.

Autonomy does not mean the patient is left alone to navigate risk. It means the RN case manager supports the patient's ability to make informed choices. Support may include plain-language teaching, interpreter services, reinforcement of provider instructions, referral to community resources, clarification of what remains pending, and escalation when safety risk is not resolved. Advocacy is strongest when it gives the patient a clearer voice and a safer pathway, not when it substitutes the RN's preference for the patient's decision.

Consent-aware sharing is a core part of M05. Case managers may speak with family members, caregivers, home health staff, clinics, pharmacies, discharge planners, social workers, and payers, but the purpose and scope of communication matter. The RN should share only appropriate information with appropriate parties, follow organizational policy, and respect patient permission when a capable patient limits who may receive updates. When urgent safety concerns exist, the RN follows policy and escalation pathways rather than improvising a privacy exception.

Ethical boundaries also include conflicts of interest, gifts, favoritism, pressure from referral sources, and inaccurate promises about services. A patient should not be steered toward a resource because it is convenient for the agency, financially preferred by someone else, or easier for the team if that recommendation does not fit the patient's needs and available choices. The RN case manager documents clinical facts, patient preferences, teaching, barriers, referrals, and follow-up plans in a neutral and respectful way.

moodle_description: Teaches patient autonomy and informed participation in RN case management.
completion_tracking: View required.

learner_instructions: Read the segment and note how autonomy changes the RN case manager's communication, coordination, and documentation.
content_body:

Patient autonomy is the right of a capable patient to receive meaningful information and make voluntary choices about care. In RN case management, autonomy appears in practical details: whether the patient wants a family member included in planning, whether the patient agrees to home health visits, whether the patient will use a medication organizer, whether the patient accepts transportation help, and whether the patient wants a referral pursued. These decisions may seem operational, but they affect safety, continuity, and the patient's sense of control.

Informed participation is broader than a signed consent form. The patient needs information in a form the patient can understand, a chance to ask questions, and an opportunity to express concerns. The RN case manager supports participation by checking understanding, identifying barriers, arranging language assistance when needed, and explaining the care coordination process without exaggerating what the RN can guarantee. If the patient believes a referral is mandatory when it is optional, the choice is not fully voluntary. If the patient is rushed, shamed, or frightened into accepting services, the case management process has moved away from ethical advocacy.

The RN case manager should distinguish education from persuasion. Education gives relevant facts, expected benefits, limitations, risks of declining, and available follow-up. Persuasion becomes ethically unsafe when it pressures the patient, hides alternatives, minimizes a concern, or uses the RN's authority to override a preference that the patient has the right to hold. A patient may make a decision the RN would not personally choose. The RN's role is to assess safety implications, communicate concerns, involve the right team members, and document the decision and follow-up plan.

Capacity and urgency shape the response. If the patient appears confused, delirious, unable to understand basic information, or at immediate risk of serious harm, the RN case manager does not simply accept or reject the patient's statement without further action. The RN documents observed concerns, not labels or assumptions, and escalates according to policy. If the patient is capable and understands the information, a refusal may still stand even when the team disagrees. Ethical practice protects the patient's rights while ensuring that preventable safety risks are communicated and addressed.

Respectful language matters. Documentation should reflect what the patient said, what information was provided, how understanding was assessed, who was contacted with permission or according to policy, what barriers remain, and what follow-up is planned. Avoid wording that blames the patient, such as "noncompliant" without context. More useful documentation describes the specific behavior and reason when known: "Patient declined home health visit this week, stating concern about cost; RN reviewed available benefit information, offered social work referral, and notified supervisor for follow-up options."

learner_instructions: Read the segment and identify what information may be shared, with whom, and under what authority or permission.
content_body:

RN case managers depend on communication, but communication must be disciplined. A family member may request updates, a clinic may ask about a discharge barrier, a payer may request clinical facts, and a community agency may need eligibility information. Each request requires the RN to consider the purpose of the disclosure, the patient's permission, the minimum information needed, organizational policy, and applicable privacy requirements. The goal is not silence; the goal is appropriate sharing that supports care while protecting the patient.

Consent-aware sharing begins by recognizing the patient as the center of the information circle whenever the patient is capable of directing participation. If the patient says, "Do not tell my son about my medications," the RN does not provide medication details to the son simply because he is persistent or helpful. The RN may still listen to information the son offers, explain general process limits, encourage a patient-family conversation if appropriate, and escalate if safety concerns require guidance. The RN should be transparent with the patient about who is involved and what information is needed for coordination.

Family involvement can be valuable. Caregivers may notice medication problems, transportation barriers, food insecurity, missed appointments, unsafe housing, or changes in cognition. Ethical case management invites helpful participation without treating family preference as a substitute for the patient's voice. A caregiver may strongly prefer skilled services, placement, or a particular discharge plan. The RN case manager should separate the caregiver's concern from the patient's decision, assess risk, provide education, and involve the provider or supervisor when disagreement creates safety concerns.

Privacy risks often occur during routine workflow. A voicemail can reveal more than intended. A portal message can be sent to the wrong recipient. A hallway conversation can be overheard. A payer call can drift into information that is not needed for the request. A family conference can include people the patient did not authorize. The RN case manager prevents these risks by verifying identity, confirming communication preferences, using secure channels, limiting content, and documenting the basis for sharing.

Documentation should show the privacy judgment without copying unnecessary sensitive details. Useful entries include patient permission, the topic discussed, the recipient role, the reason for the communication, information provided, questions or concerns raised, and planned follow-up. When information is withheld because permission is absent or uncertain, document the action respectfully: "Adult daughter requested medication list by phone. Patient has not authorized medication updates to daughter in current record. RN explained that patient permission is needed before details can be shared and offered to discuss authorization options with patient."

learner_instructions: Read the segment and focus on how the RN protects safety while respecting a capable patient's refusal.
content_body:

Refusal of services is not the end of case management. It is a clinical and ethical moment that calls for assessment, education, communication, documentation, and follow-up. A patient may refuse home health, durable medical equipment, medication review, a caregiver meeting, transportation support, a specialty appointment, or a recommended safety intervention. The reason may be cost, fear, prior experience, cultural or spiritual values, privacy concerns, denial, fatigue, transportation difficulty, or lack of trust. The RN case manager should explore the reason respectfully before deciding what coordination steps are needed.

The first task is to clarify what is being refused. A patient who refuses "home care" may refuse strangers in the home but accept one teaching visit. A patient who refuses transportation help may accept appointment scheduling if a family member can drive. A patient who refuses a medication review may be concerned about cost or embarrassment. By identifying the specific concern, the RN can offer realistic alternatives without pressuring the patient. This is advocacy: making sure the patient has a fair opportunity to receive support that fits the patient's goals and circumstances.

Education should be plain, balanced, and linked to the patient's situation. The RN may explain what the service is, why it was recommended, what could happen if the issue remains unresolved, what alternatives exist, and who can answer provider-level questions. The RN should avoid threats, exaggerated outcomes, or statements that imply the patient will lose all care if the patient declines one service. If the refusal creates serious safety concern, the RN escalates to the appropriate provider, supervisor, or emergency pathway according to policy.

Advocacy without coercion also means protecting the patient from pressure by others. Family members may urge the RN to "make him agree." A discharge planner may want a quick acceptance to clear a bed. A payer representative may focus on authorized options rather than patient preference. A resource agency may have limited availability. The RN case manager can acknowledge these pressures while returning to patient-centered planning: What does the patient understand? What does the patient want? What risks need to be addressed? What options are available? Who needs to be notified?

Respectful documentation of refusal includes the offered service, information provided, patient questions, stated reason for refusal when offered, assessment of understanding, safety concerns, alternatives offered, notifications made, and follow-up plan. It should not punish the patient with judgmental wording. The record should support continuity: another RN should be able to see what happened and what still needs attention.

learner_instructions: Read the segment and identify how communication barriers can affect consent, refusal, and safe coordination.
content_body:

Language access is a patient-rights issue because a patient cannot participate meaningfully in planning if the patient cannot understand the information being discussed. RN case managers should use qualified interpreter services according to policy when language barriers affect assessment, teaching, consent-aware planning, refusal, or safety instructions. Relying on a child, neighbor, or untrained family member can create errors, omissions, privacy concerns, and pressure on the patient. Even when a bilingual family member is present, the RN should consider whether the patient has a private and accurate way to communicate.

Language access includes more than spoken interpretation. Written instructions, portal messages, phone calls, teach-back, hearing needs, vision needs, health literacy, and cognitive load all affect understanding. A patient may nod politely without understanding. A patient may read in one language but prefer spoken teaching in another. A patient may understand routine conversation but not medication changes or discharge instructions. The RN case manager should assess communication needs and coordinate appropriate support rather than assuming comprehension from brief agreement.

Cultural humility is the ongoing practice of curiosity, respect, and self-awareness. It does not require the RN to become an expert on every culture or to assume that all members of a group share the same beliefs. It requires the RN to avoid stereotyping and to ask respectful questions about what matters to this patient. Beliefs about family roles, modesty, food, pain, independence, caregiving, spiritual practices, disability, mental health, and outside services may affect case management planning. The RN case manager should invite the patient to describe preferences in the patient's own terms.

Cultural humility also protects against coercion. If the RN assumes that a patient is refusing because of "culture" without asking, the RN may miss cost barriers, fear, prior trauma, transportation issues, or misunderstanding. If the RN dismisses a patient's values as irrational, the patient may withdraw from care. Ethical advocacy looks for options that honor the patient's values while addressing safety. For example, if a patient does not want a certain caregiver in the home, the RN may explore gender preference, scheduling, privacy, or alternative visit formats when available.

Documentation should be specific and respectful. Avoid vague labels such as "non-English speaking" without noting the communication support used. Better documentation might state: "Qualified interpreter used by phone for care coordination call. Patient stated preference for daughter to attend teaching call but requested that financial details not be discussed with daughter. RN reviewed home safety referral purpose, patient questions answered through interpreter, and social work follow-up requested for cost concerns." This kind of note supports continuity, privacy, and patient-centered planning.

content_body:

Ethical boundaries keep RN case management focused on patient welfare, nursing scope, and truthful communication. Boundary concerns may appear as gifts, personal relationships, social media contact, favoritism, pressure to use a preferred vendor, requests to hide information, or promises that a service will be approved. A boundary issue may also arise when the RN becomes so invested in a preferred plan that the patient's voice is minimized. Professional boundaries are not cold or uncaring. They create the conditions for trustworthy, fair, and patient-centered coordination.

Conflicts of interest occur when a secondary interest could influence, or appear to influence, case management judgment. The secondary interest may be financial, personal, organizational, or relational. For example, recommending a resource because a staff member has a personal connection to it, steering a patient away from a needed service because it is inconvenient, or withholding information because a metric looks better without it can undermine patient trust. The RN case manager should disclose and escalate conflicts according to policy, seek supervision, and document patient-care facts neutrally.

Escalation is an ethical tool when the RN cannot safely resolve a concern alone. Escalation may involve the provider, supervisor, social worker, compliance contact, privacy officer, ethics resource, emergency pathway, or interdisciplinary team. The right pathway depends on urgency and risk. A privacy question may need compliance guidance. A refusal with immediate danger may need same-day provider notification. Suspected coercion or abuse may require policy-driven reporting. Resource scarcity may require supervisory review to identify alternatives. Escalation should be timely, factual, and connected to patient safety or rights.

Documentation is the bridge between ethical decision-making and continuity of care. It should be accurate, timely, factual, and respectful. It should include relevant assessment findings, patient statements in quotation marks when useful, education provided, understanding checks, consent or permission status, parties contacted, information shared, barriers, escalation steps, and follow-up tasks. It should avoid sarcasm, blame, speculation, copied text that does not match the encounter, or unnecessary sensitive details. Respectful documentation protects the patient and the RN because it shows the reasoning behind actions.

The RN case manager should also recognize limits. The RN does not provide legal advice, guarantee payer approval, authorize services outside policy, override provider orders, or decide that a patient's values are wrong. The RN can coordinate, educate within scope, communicate concerns, advocate for options, and escalate unresolved risk. When the situation is ethically complex, consultation is a sign of professional practice, not failure.

learner_instructions: Complete the branching scenario. Choose the most appropriate RN case management action at each decision point and read the feedback.
content_body:

Scenario setup: A fictional adult patient is preparing for discharge from a short inpatient stay to home with follow-up care. The patient is alert, speaks directly with the RN case manager, and asks to decide who receives information. The patient's adult child calls the RN case manager separately and says, "Do not tell my parent about the home health plan yet. If you explain it, they will refuse. Just schedule it and I will handle the rest." Later, the patient states, "I do not want strangers in my home, and I do not want my child getting every detail about my medications." The discharge timeline is tight, and home health availability is limited this week.

Decision point 1 - Family pressure and patient participation:

Choice A: Tell the adult child that the patient has the right to participate, then speak with the patient using plain language about the recommended service, expected purpose, available choices, and who may receive updates.

Correct-path feedback: This response protects informed participation and avoids allowing family pressure to replace the patient's voice. It also keeps the RN within case management scope by explaining coordination options and permission needs without promising a specific outcome.

Choice B: Schedule the service without telling the patient because the adult child is trying to prevent refusal.

Unsafe-choice feedback: This bypasses the capable patient's participation and may turn a recommended service into a coerced plan. It also creates documentation and trust risks if the patient later learns that care was arranged without meaningful discussion.

Choice C: End the call and refuse to speak with the adult child about anything because family involvement is always prohibited.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Family involvement is not automatically prohibited. The RN may listen, provide general process information, and involve family according to patient permission and policy. The error is treating privacy as silence rather than appropriate, limited communication.

Remediation prompt for decision point 1: In one or two sentences, explain how the RN can acknowledge family concern while preserving the patient's informed participation.

Decision point 2 - Patient refusal and resource limitations:

Choice A: Document "patient noncompliant" and close the case because the patient refused home health.

Unsafe-choice feedback: This wording is judgmental and incomplete. Refusal requires assessment of the reason, education, alternatives when available, safety evaluation, notification when needed, and follow-up planning.

Choice B: Explore the patient's concern about strangers in the home, explain the purpose and limits of home health, ask whether any alternative support would be acceptable, and escalate same-day if safety risks remain unresolved.

Correct-path feedback: This response respects refusal while continuing patient-centered assessment and advocacy. It recognizes that the patient may refuse one option but accept another safer plan, and it escalates unresolved risk rather than abandoning coordination.

Choice C: Warn the patient that refusal will cause all services to be stopped and that the family will be told everything.

Unsafe-choice feedback: This is coercive and inaccurate. The RN should not threaten loss of care or misuse family disclosure to force agreement. Ethical communication is truthful, proportional, and respectful.

Remediation prompt for decision point 2: Identify one factual education point and one follow-up action the RN should document after a capable patient refuses a recommended service.

Decision point 3 - Confidentiality request and care coordination:

Choice A: Send the adult child the complete medication list because the child is likely to help at home.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Helpfulness does not automatically authorize disclosure of medication details. The RN must consider patient permission, policy, minimum necessary information, and safe alternatives.

Choice B: Avoid all coordination with the clinic, home health agency, and social worker because the patient requested privacy from the adult child.

Unsafe-choice feedback: The patient's limit on family updates does not prevent appropriate care coordination with authorized care partners. The RN should share needed information through proper channels and document the purpose.

Choice C: Confirm the patient's preference, share only appropriate information with authorized care partners for coordination, limit family updates according to patient permission and policy, and document the privacy boundary.

Correct-path feedback: This protects confidentiality while allowing necessary coordination. It also creates a clear record of patient preference, communication limits, and follow-up responsibilities.

Remediation prompt for decision point 3: State how the RN should document the patient's confidentiality preference without adding unnecessary sensitive detail.

Completion condition: The learner must attempt all three decision points. If any unsafe-choice feedback is triggered, the learner must complete the related remediation prompt before the scenario is marked complete.

learner_instructions: Choose the best response for each ethics branch and write a brief rationale for your final path.
content_body:

This active interaction asks you to apply M05 concepts in a short decision path. In each branch, select the response that best protects patient rights, supports informed participation, maintains confidentiality, and uses escalation appropriately.

Branch 1: Family pressure. A caregiver insists the RN should "convince" the patient to accept services and says the patient does not need to know all details. Best response: Acknowledge the caregiver's concern, explain that the patient must be included in planning when capable, and prepare a patient-centered discussion using plain language.

Branch 2: Patient refusal. The patient refuses the first proposed service because of privacy concerns. Best response: Explore the concern, clarify what the service involves, offer available alternatives, assess safety risk, and escalate unresolved risk through the appropriate pathway.

Branch 3: Confidentiality request. The patient allows the caregiver to receive scheduling information but not medication details. Best response: Document the preference, limit family updates accordingly, and coordinate medication-related concerns with authorized care partners according to policy.

Branch 4: Resource limitation. The preferred service is unavailable this week. Best response: Inform the patient honestly, coordinate available alternatives, prioritize safety needs, and escalate if the delay creates significant risk. Do not promise availability or hide the limitation.

Branch 5: Boundary risk. A vendor contact offers the RN a gift for steering referrals. Best response: Decline the gift, follow conflict-of-interest policy, notify the appropriate supervisor or compliance contact, and keep patient resource discussions based on patient need and available options.

After completing the branches, submit a short rationale explaining which patient right was most at risk, what advocacy action protected the patient, and what documentation would support continuity.
