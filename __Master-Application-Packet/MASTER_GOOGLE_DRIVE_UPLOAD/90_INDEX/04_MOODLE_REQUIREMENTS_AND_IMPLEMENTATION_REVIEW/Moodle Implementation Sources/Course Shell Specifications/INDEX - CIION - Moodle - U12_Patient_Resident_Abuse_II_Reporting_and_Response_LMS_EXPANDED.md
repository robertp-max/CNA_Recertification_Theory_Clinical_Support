The CNA role is practical and urgent: listen, observe, assist within the care plan, report through required channels, document within scope, protect privacy, and continue respectful care.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 1 — Mandated-Reporting Responsibility

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 1
lane: CNA/CDPH online CE
unit_id: U12
activity_id: U12_BOOK_SEGMENT_01
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 1: Mandated-Reporting Responsibility
moodle_description: Book page explaining the CNA duty to report suspected abuse, neglect, or exploitation through required channels without investigating independently.
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; include within Book completion.
restrict_access: Available after U12 Overview completion.
gradebook_setting: Not graded.
learner_instructions: Read the segment and note the difference between reporting a concern and proving an allegation.
content_body:
Mandated reporting means that certain workers, including CNAs in long-term care settings, have a legal and professional duty to report suspected abuse, neglect, abandonment, or exploitation through required channels. Reporting is not a favor to the resident and it is not something a CNA may skip because the situation feels uncomfortable. Reporting is part of resident protection.

A CNA does not need proof before reporting. A resident statement, unexplained injury, sudden fear, missing funds, repeated lack of needed supplies, coercive behavior, or a pattern of neglect can be enough to require action. The CNA’s responsibility is to notice, keep the resident as safe as possible within role, report promptly, and document facts. The nurse, supervisor, administrator, ombudsman pathway, adult protective services pathway, law enforcement pathway, licensing pathway, or other required route depends on current law, resident setting, and facility policy. Always follow the current approved instructions for your workplace.

Mandated reporting also means that a request for secrecy cannot be accepted. A resident may say, “Please do not tell anyone,” because they feel ashamed, afraid of retaliation, worried about family conflict, or dependent on the person involved. A respectful response does not argue or dismiss the fear. A safe response is: “I will keep this private from people who do not need to know, but I cannot promise secrecy. I need to tell the nurse or supervisor so you can get help.”

Reporting is different from investigation. CNAs should not interrogate the resident, search belongings, call relatives, record unauthorized audio, confront a suspected person, or decide that the concern is false. Extra questioning can increase distress, contaminate later investigation, or put the resident at more risk. Ask only what is needed for immediate safety and reporting, then follow the required reporting process.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Immediate Response to Disclosure

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 2
lane: CNA/CDPH online CE
unit_id: U12
activity_id: U12_BOOK_SEGMENT_02
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 2: Immediate Response to Disclosure
moodle_description: Book page teaching calm listening, no-secrecy language, immediate safety steps, and scope-safe escalation after a disclosure.
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; include within Book completion.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 completion.
gradebook_setting: Not graded.
learner_instructions: Focus on the first words and first actions that protect the resident without making promises or accusations.
content_body:
The first response to a disclosure can affect whether the resident feels safe enough to accept help. If a resident says someone is taking money, threatening them, touching them, isolating them, withholding care, or hurting them, stop multitasking if safe to do so. Use a calm voice. Move to a private area when possible and consistent with safety. Sit or stand at the resident’s level if appropriate. Do not show shock, anger, disbelief, or impatience.

Use supportive words that do not promise an outcome: “Thank you for telling me,” “I am sorry this is happening,” “You deserve to be safe,” and “I need to report this to the nurse or supervisor.” These statements acknowledge the resident without investigating. Avoid “Are you sure?” “Why did you let that happen?” “I will fix it myself,” or “I won’t tell anyone.” The resident may already feel blame or fear; the CNA should reduce shame, not add to it.

Immediate safety is the next priority. If there is immediate danger, use the emergency response and supervisor-notification process. If the suspected person is present or expected soon, follow facility instructions for privacy and safety rather than confronting the person. If the concern involves financial exploitation, the resident may need help safeguarding belongings, contacting authorized support, or accessing needed hygiene items, but the CNA should not take possession of money, bank cards, passwords, checks, or legal documents unless a specific facility procedure assigns that action.

After the immediate response, report promptly through the chain identified by policy. In many settings, the charge nurse or supervisor is notified first, and external reporting steps are also completed according to current requirements. Do not delay because the shift is busy, because the resident asked for secrecy, or because the suspected person is a relative. Delayed reporting can allow continued harm.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Confidentiality and Reporting Pathways

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3
lane: CNA/CDPH online CE
unit_id: U12
activity_id: U12_BOOK_SEGMENT_03
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 3: Confidentiality and Reporting Pathways
moodle_description: Book page explaining confidentiality, authorized reporting, and pathway principles without copying official forms.
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; include within Book completion.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 completion.
gradebook_setting: Not graded.
learner_instructions: Read how confidentiality and mandated reporting work together instead of competing with each other.
content_body:
Confidentiality protects the resident’s dignity and safety. It means sharing information only with people or agencies that are authorized to receive it for care, protection, reporting, investigation, or oversight. Confidentiality does not mean keeping abuse concerns secret from the nurse, supervisor, administrator, or mandated external reporting pathway. Privacy rules are not a reason to ignore suspected abuse.

A helpful way to think about reporting pathways is “right information, right person, right time.” The right information is factual and limited to the concern: what the resident said, what you observed, what you did, and who was notified. The right person is the licensed nurse, supervisor, administrator, or designated reporting contact required by facility procedure, plus any external agency pathway required by current California rules for the resident’s setting. The right time is prompt enough to meet safety needs and mandated timelines. Because reporting rules can change, this course does not replace current facility instruction.

Confidentiality mistakes can happen quickly. Do not discuss a disclosure in the hallway, dining room, break room, elevator, text thread, or social media post. Do not warn the suspected person. Do not tell another resident or a visitor. Do not include details in a classroom assignment that identify a real person or facility. In Moodle activities, use only the fictional Resident L scenario or another fully fictional example.

Financial exploitation has special privacy concerns because it may involve bank cards, cash, checks, account numbers, passwords, benefits, gifts, property, or family relationships. CNAs should report what the resident says and what is observed, but should not ask for account numbers, photograph cards, handle passwords, or attempt to verify transactions. The goal is to move the concern to authorized protection pathways while preserving the resident’s privacy and dignity.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Objective Documentation and Follow-Through

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 4
lane: CNA/CDPH online CE
unit_id: U12
activity_id: U12_BOOK_SEGMENT_04
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 4: Objective Documentation and Follow-Through
moodle_description: Book page teaching direct quotes, observable facts, notification documentation, follow-through, and certificate gate awareness.
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; include within Book completion.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 3 completion.
gradebook_setting: Not graded.
learner_instructions: Compare objective charting examples with unsafe wording before starting the scenario.
content_body:
Objective documentation records what happened without adding blame, diagnosis, or investigation conclusions. For a disclosure, use the resident’s exact words when possible. Example: Resident stated, “My nephew takes my bank card and yells when I ask for it back.” A related observation may be: Resident stated no money available for preferred hygiene items this month. The entry should also include the date, time, location if required, immediate action taken, and who was notified according to policy.

Unsafe documentation sounds like a conclusion: “Nephew stole from resident,” “family is abusive,” “resident is lying,” or “probably just confused.” These statements may exceed CNA scope and can weaken the record. If the resident appears afraid, tearful, withdrawn, or worried, document the observable behavior: “Resident looked down, spoke quietly, and asked that the information not be shared.” If the resident reports a threat, place the threat in quotation marks. If hygiene items are missing, document what is observed or stated rather than guessing why.

Follow-through matters after the first report. Continue providing assigned care, privacy, and respect. Watch for changes in mood, fear, pain, hygiene, belongings, or interaction with visitors and staff. Report any new concerns promptly. Do not retaliate against the resident for reporting, and do not treat the resident as difficult or dramatic. Abuse response is part of protecting resident rights.

Documentation should also match facility correction rules. If an error is made, correct it according to policy rather than deleting, backdating, or hiding it. If you forgot to include who was notified, follow the approved late-entry or correction process. The record should show that the concern moved to the required pathway.

learner_instructions: Complete the fictional branching case. Choose the safest CNA action at each decision point. If you choose an unsafe option, read the feedback and remediation prompt before trying again.
content_body:
Scenario setup: Resident L is alert after morning care and says quietly, “My nephew takes my bank card and yells when I ask for it back.” Resident L asks, “Please do not tell anyone.” You also know Resident L has said there is no money for preferred hygiene items this month. No real resident information may be entered into this activity.

Decision point 1 — response to the disclosure. Choose one:
A. Listen calmly, thank Resident L for telling you, explain that you cannot promise secrecy, and say you must report to the nurse or supervisor so help can be arranged.
B. Promise not to tell anyone because Resident L seems embarrassed.
C. Tell Resident L that family members often manage money and the situation is probably normal.
D. Ask for bank passwords and details so you can investigate before reporting.
Correct feedback: A is the safest CNA response. It validates the resident, avoids secrecy, and moves the concern toward required reporting.
Unsafe-choice feedback: B delays required reporting. C dismisses a possible exploitation disclosure. D exceeds CNA scope and creates privacy and safety risks.
Remediation prompt: Re-read the no-secrecy language in Lesson Segment 2, then choose the option that listens, protects privacy, and reports.

Decision point 2 — reporting pathway. Choose one:
A. Wait until the end of the shift to see whether Resident L mentions it again.
B. Notify the charge nurse or supervisor promptly and follow the current mandated-reporting pathway identified by facility policy, including required external reporting steps when applicable.
C. Call the nephew directly and demand that the card be returned.
D. Send a group text to coworkers to ask whether they have heard similar stories.
Correct feedback: B is safest because it uses the supervisory chain and current mandated-reporting process without delay.
Unsafe-choice feedback: A delays protection. C may increase risk and is not a CNA investigation step. D violates confidentiality.
Remediation prompt: Review the “right information, right person, right time” rule in Lesson Segment 3.

Decision point 3 — documentation and confidentiality. Choose one:
A. Chart “nephew stole from resident and is abusive” so the record is strong.
B. Tell another resident’s family that money problems are happening so they can watch out.
C. Document Resident L’s exact quote, the hygiene-item statement, your immediate action, and who was notified; share details only through authorized channels.
D. Skip charting because the supervisor already knows.
Correct feedback: C is objective, confidential, and complete within CNA scope.
Unsafe-choice feedback: A states conclusions beyond CNA scope. B discloses information to unauthorized people. D leaves an incomplete record.
Remediation prompt: Re-read Lesson Segment 4 and rewrite the concern as quote + observation + action + notification.

Completion condition: The activity is complete when the learner answers all three decision points, reviews any remediation screens triggered by unsafe choices, and reaches the final confirmation screen stating that required reporting is confidential but not secret.
