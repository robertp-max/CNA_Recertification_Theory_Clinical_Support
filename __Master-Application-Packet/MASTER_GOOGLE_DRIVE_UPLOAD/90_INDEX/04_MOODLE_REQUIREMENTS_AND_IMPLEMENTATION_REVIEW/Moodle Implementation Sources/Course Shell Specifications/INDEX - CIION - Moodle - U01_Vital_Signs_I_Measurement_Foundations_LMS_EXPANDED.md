moodle_description: Explains the purpose of vital signs, baseline comparison, measurement responsibility, and CNA communication boundaries.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after U01 overview page is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify what makes a vital-sign report useful to the licensed nurse.
content_body: |
  Vital signs help the care team see how the body is functioning at a particular time. Temperature, pulse, respirations, blood pressure, oxygen-related observations when assigned by policy, and pain reports can point to comfort, stress, illness, response to activity, response to medication, hydration concerns, or other changes that require nursing review. A single number can matter, but the pattern matters too. A resident whose temperature is usually lower than average may have a meaningful change even when a reading does not look dramatic. A resident who normally walks to breakfast without complaint may need prompt attention when pain suddenly prevents standing.

  Baseline means the resident's usual pattern or condition. CNAs contribute to baseline awareness because they see residents during meals, hygiene, toileting, transfers, rest periods, and conversations. You might notice that a resident is usually talkative but is now quiet, usually eats well but now refuses breakfast, or usually walks with steady assistance but now grimaces and guards a knee. Those observations give context to a temperature or pain score.

  Good vital-sign communication is specific. Instead of saying, "Resident seems sick," report what you measured and observed: "Oral temperature 100.8 F at 0730; resident states, 'I feel cold'; resident wrapped in two blankets; right knee pain reported as 6 out of 10; nurse notified." This wording helps the nurse evaluate next steps. It does not diagnose infection, arthritis, injury, or medication need.

  Measurement responsibility depends on assignment, training, equipment, and facility policy. This online CE unit reviews knowledge and judgment; it does not certify hands-on technique. In practice, CNAs must use facility-approved equipment, follow cleaning and infection-prevention procedures, identify the resident according to policy, choose only the assigned measurement route or site, and ask for help when equipment, readings, or resident condition raise concern. If a reading is unusual, the CNA may be directed to recheck it, but routine rechecking should not delay urgent reporting when the resident appears ill, distressed, confused, in severe pain, or otherwise changed.

  The most reliable CNA habit is to connect four parts: measure accurately as assigned, observe the resident, report promptly, and document factually. Leaving out any one part weakens resident safety. A number without a route may be hard to interpret. A pain report without the resident's words may miss urgency. A chart entry without nurse notification may not protect the resident if action is needed right away. Clear communication turns a routine task into a safety contribution.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Temperature Terms, Routes, and Context

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 2

activity_id: U01_BOOK_SEGMENT_02
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 2 — Temperature Language and Route Matter
moodle_description: Reviews temperature terminology, common routes or methods, route documentation, fever-related observations, and factors that influence readings.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and look for why the route, time, and resident condition must be included with a temperature reading.
content_body: |
  Temperature reflects the body's heat balance. It can rise with infection, inflammation, activity, warm environment, dehydration, or other conditions. It can fall with cold exposure, certain illnesses, or reduced ability to regulate heat. CNAs do not decide the cause of a temperature change, but they do report the reading and related observations.

  Common terms help the team speak the same language. Afebrile means without fever. Febrile means fever is present. Pyrexia is another word for fever. Hyperthermia refers to abnormally high body temperature, and hypothermia refers to abnormally low body temperature. Chills, shivering, sweating, flushed skin, pale or cool skin, warm skin, confusion, weakness, and a resident statement such as "I feel hot" or "I cannot get warm" may be important related observations. Terms should be used carefully. If you are unsure whether a term applies, report the facts and ask the nurse.

  Route or method matters because temperature readings are not all interchangeable. Oral, axillary, tympanic, temporal, and rectal readings may differ. Facility policy determines which routes CNAs may use and under what conditions. Some routes may be inappropriate for a resident because of oxygen use, mouth breathing, recent hot or cold fluids, confusion, injury, infection-control concerns, or other factors. This unit does not teach hands-on technique; it teaches why the reading must be described accurately. "Temperature 100.8 F" is less complete than "Oral temperature 100.8 F at 0730."

  Time matters because temperature can change during the day and in response to activity, medications, meals, environment, and illness. If the nurse asks for a recheck, record the new reading with the new time and route. Do not replace one reading with another or hide an unexpected value. If a device seems faulty, the resident appears changed, or the reading does not match the resident's condition, follow policy and get help.

  Resident comfort and dignity matter during temperature measurement. Explain what you are doing, protect privacy, clean equipment as required, and avoid exposing the resident unnecessarily. If the resident refuses a measurement, report the refusal according to policy. If the resident is uncomfortable, shivering, sweating heavily, newly confused, unusually drowsy, or complaining of pain along with a temperature change, report promptly. Routine charting should not be the only communication when the resident may need nursing assessment.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Pain Observation within CNA Scope

activity_id: U01_BOOK_SEGMENT_03
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 3 — Pain Is Reported, Observed, and Respected
moodle_description: Describes pain as subjective information supported by observation, pain-scale use, nonverbal signs, comfort measures within scope, and reporting expectations.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and notice the difference between reporting pain facts and deciding what treatment is needed.
content_body: |
  Pain is whatever the resident says it is. A resident's report should be taken seriously even when the CNA cannot see an injury, even when the resident has chronic pain, and even when the resident continues to smile or talk. Some residents understate pain because they do not want to bother staff. Others cannot describe pain clearly because of dementia, communication barriers, speech changes, or fatigue. The CNA's responsibility is to ask as assigned, listen, observe, support comfort within the care plan, and report.

  Many facilities use a pain scale, such as 0 to 10, faces, words, or another approved tool. Use the tool assigned by facility policy. A complete pain report may include location, rating, onset or timing, what the resident was doing, what makes it worse or better, related observations, and how pain affects function. For example: "Resident states right knee pain is 6 out of 10 when standing for transfer; grimaced and held bedrail; morning care paused; nurse notified." This report gives the nurse useful information without diagnosing arthritis, injury, or medication need.

  Nonverbal signs can matter. Guarding, grimacing, moaning, restlessness, rubbing an area, refusing movement, changes in sleep, poor appetite, tearfulness, sweating, increased tension, or sudden behavior change may suggest discomfort. In residents with cognitive impairment, pain may appear as agitation, withdrawal, resistance to care, calling out, or a change from usual behavior. CNAs should not label the resident as difficult. Report what was seen and heard, and include direct quotes when available.

  Comfort measures must remain within scope and the care plan. Examples may include repositioning, offering a blanket, adjusting pillows, reducing noise, allowing rest before continuing care, supporting privacy, helping the resident use prescribed assistive devices as assigned, and notifying the nurse. Heat, cold, massage, topical products, medication, or changes in activity restrictions require policy direction and licensed guidance. If a resident reports new severe pain, pain after a fall or transfer, chest pain, sudden headache, abdominal pain, or pain with shortness of breath, weakness, confusion, fever, or other concerning signs, report immediately according to facility procedure.

  Pain can affect vital signs and daily function. It may increase pulse or respirations, raise blood pressure, limit mobility, reduce appetite, interfere with sleep, and change mood. That does not mean the CNA interprets the whole clinical picture. It means the CNA collects and reports a clear picture: the resident's words, the pain scale result, related observations, activity at the time, actions taken within scope, and who was notified.

learner_visible_title: Lesson 4 — Factual Vital-Sign Recording and Timely Reporting
moodle_description: Connects temperature and pain observations to objective charting, urgent reporting, privacy, and completion evidence expectations.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 3 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and practice replacing vague or diagnostic wording with factual CNA documentation.
content_body: |
  Recording and reporting are related, but they are not the same action. Reporting means communicating information to the licensed nurse or approved chain according to facility policy. Documentation means entering the factual record in the approved charting system. If a resident has an unusual temperature, significant pain, sudden change, or signs of distress, report first or as policy directs; do not assume that a chart note will be seen in time.

  Factual vital-sign documentation usually includes the value, unit of measure, route or site, time, relevant resident position when required, the resident's statement, related observations, and notification. For temperature, include Fahrenheit or Celsius according to facility practice and record the route or method. For pain, include the assigned scale or words used by the resident, location when stated, activity at the time, nonverbal signs, and action taken. A strong entry is concise: "0730 oral temperature 100.8 F. Resident states, 'I feel cold,' wrapped in two blankets, skin warm to touch. Resident states right knee pain 6/10 when standing. Nurse notified."

  Avoid wording that labels, blames, guesses, or diagnoses. "Resident is infected," "resident is drug-seeking," "resident is dramatic," "resident has arthritis flare," or "family caused this" are not appropriate CNA documentation. If a resident statement matters, put it in quotation marks and keep it exact enough to preserve meaning. If you notify the nurse, document notification according to policy. If the nurse gives direction, follow your facility's rules for documenting actions performed within CNA scope.

  Privacy is part of documentation safety. Do not discuss vital signs in hallways where unauthorized people can hear. Do not leave paper notes exposed. Log out of electronic systems. In this Moodle unit, never enter real resident information. Use fictional examples only. A practice reflection that includes a real resident's initials, room number, medical condition, or facility name can become a privacy problem even if the learner meant well.

  Accuracy includes honesty about uncertainty. If equipment fails, a resident refuses, you used the wrong route, or you realize a documentation error occurred, follow policy and notify the correct person. Do not invent values, change times, copy another person's measurement as your own, or chart a task before it is done. The goal is not to make the chart look perfect. The goal is to communicate what happened so the resident receives safe, timely care.

  As you enter the scenario, remember the U01 reporting formula: measurement plus route or method, time, resident statement, objective observations, action within scope, and notification. This formula keeps your work useful, respectful, and within the CNA role.

This is a fictional practice scenario; do not enter any real resident information.

  **Decision Point 1 — First safe CNA response:** What should the CNA do first after obtaining the temperature and hearing the pain report?

  A. Record the assigned oral temperature with time, pause the task as safe, observe the pain cues, report the change and pain statement to the licensed nurse promptly, and follow direction.  
  B. Wait until the end of the shift because 100.8 F is only useful if it appears on the routine chart.  
  C. Tell the resident the pain is probably arthritis and continue the transfer without reporting.  
  D. Ask another learner in Moodle whether this real resident might need medicine.

  **Correct feedback for A:** This response connects measurement, route, time, resident statement, pain observation, prompt reporting, and scope-safe follow-through.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B, C, or D:** Delaying a report, diagnosing the cause, pushing through pain, or sharing real resident information is unsafe and outside this course's privacy and scope boundaries.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Rewrite the response using five words: measure, pause, observe, report, document.

  **Decision Point 2 — Sorting information:** Which sorting choice best matches CNA documentation and reporting categories?

  A. "Oral temperature 100.8 F at 0730" belongs in measure/record; "right knee pain 6/10" and "I feel cold" belong in reportable observations; "resident has an infection" belongs in do-not-chart-as-diagnosis.  
  B. "Resident has an infection" belongs in measure/record because fever always means infection.  
  C. "I feel cold" should be ignored because only numbers count as vital signs.  
  D. "Right knee pain 6/10" should be hidden until the resident asks for medication.

  **Correct feedback for A:** Good sorting separates factual measurement, resident statement, pain report, and diagnostic wording to avoid.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B, C, or D:** CNAs report symptoms and observations; they do not diagnose causes, ignore resident statements, or withhold pain information.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Place each item into one column: measure/record, report now, comfort action within scope, or do not chart as diagnosis.

  **Decision Point 3 — Best charting and report wording:** Which statement best fits CNA scope after the nurse has been notified?

  A. "Resident is infected and knee is probably injured; medication needed."  
  B. "Resident was dramatic at breakfast and refused to act normal."  
  C. "Nothing important happened because the resident was able to speak."  
  D. "0730 oral temperature 100.8 F; resident stated, 'I feel cold' and reported right knee pain 6/10 when standing; guarding and slower movement observed; nurse notified."

  **Correct feedback for D:** This wording is factual, includes route, time, direct statement, pain score, related observation, and notification without diagnosis.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A, B, or C:** Diagnostic claims, labels, and dismissive wording do not belong in CNA documentation and may delay care.
