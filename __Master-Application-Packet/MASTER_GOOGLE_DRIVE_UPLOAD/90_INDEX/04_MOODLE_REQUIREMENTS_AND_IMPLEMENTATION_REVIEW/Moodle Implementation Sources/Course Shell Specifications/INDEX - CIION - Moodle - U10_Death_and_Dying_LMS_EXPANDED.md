content_body: |
  Death and dying care is one of the most sensitive areas of CNA practice. A dying resident is still a person with preferences, rights, routines, relationships, culture, and spiritual or nonspiritual beliefs. Family members and friends may be grieving before death occurs, at the time of death, and after death. The CNA cannot remove grief, but the CNA can make the environment more respectful by speaking calmly, offering privacy, following the care plan, and noticing comfort needs.

  By the end of this unit, you should be able to describe common grief responses without judging them, identify emotional, spiritual, cultural, and family-support needs, recognize signs that may occur as death approaches, and choose comfort measures that are appropriate for the CNA role. You will also distinguish hospice and postmortem support from decisions that belong to licensed staff, hospice professionals, facility leadership, the family, or legal authorities.

  Scope matters in end-of-life care because families may ask urgent questions such as, “How long does my loved one have?” or “What does the breathing pattern mean?” A safe CNA response acknowledges the concern, reports observations, stays within the care plan, and connects the family with the nurse or hospice contact. Guessing, interpreting legal documents, changing oxygen or medications, or promising outcomes can harm trust and exceed role boundaries.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 1 — Grief, Loss, and Compassionate Communication

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 1

estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 1 — Grief, Loss, and Compassionate Communication
moodle_description: First lesson page on grief responses, anticipatory grief, and CNA communication choices that protect dignity.
completion_tracking: Automatic completion on view plus expected engagement time if configured.
restrict_access: Available after U10 overview completion.
gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions: Read the lesson segment and notice how the examples use fictional residents only. Focus on words that support feelings without making promises or judgments.
content_body: |
  Grief is a response to loss. A resident may grieve the approach of death, the loss of independence, separation from home, changes in body function, or the effect of illness on family members. Families may experience anticipatory grief before death occurs. Staff may also feel sadness, especially when they have cared for a resident for a long time. Grief can include denial, anger, bargaining, sadness, acceptance, relief, guilt, silence, crying, laughter, spiritual questioning, or practical problem solving. These responses do not occur in a required order. A person may move back and forth between feelings during the same conversation.

  The CNA’s communication goal is not to force a “right” emotional response. The goal is to be present, respectful, and truthful within role. Helpful statements include: “I can see this is a hard moment,” “I will let the nurse know what you are seeing,” “Would you like the room quieter?” or “I can stay for a moment while I finish this care.” These statements acknowledge distress without pretending to know the future. Less helpful statements include: “Do not cry,” “Everything happens for a reason,” “I know exactly how you feel,” or “They will pass tonight.” Even when intended kindly, those statements may dismiss grief or make a prognosis claim.

  Listening is an active skill. A resident may want to talk about fear, unfinished relationships, favorite memories, or simple comfort needs such as dry lips or a cold room. A family member may repeat the same question many times because stress makes information hard to absorb. The CNA should answer only within role, report observations promptly, and invite the nurse, social worker, chaplain, hospice team member, or supervisor when the need goes beyond CNA duties. Protect privacy by closing curtains or doors as appropriate, lowering your voice, and avoiding hallway discussions.

moodle_description: Second lesson page on resident rights, privacy, cultural humility, spiritual support, and family presence at end of life.
completion_tracking: Automatic completion on view plus expected engagement time if configured.
restrict_access: Available after U10 Book Page Lesson Segment 1 completion.
gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions: Read with attention to choice, privacy, and respectful support. The unit does not ask you to disclose your personal beliefs.
content_body: |
  A dying resident keeps the same basic rights that apply throughout care: dignity, privacy, respectful treatment, freedom from unnecessary exposure, choice when possible, communication with visitors according to policy, and care that follows the plan established by the team. End-of-life care may make these rights more visible. A resident may want a favorite blanket, music, prayer, silence, hand holding, a window shade adjusted, fewer interruptions, or a certain family member present. Another resident may not want visitors or may prefer a nonreligious environment. The CNA honors preferences that are known and safe, reports unmet needs, and follows facility policy.

  Spiritual care is not limited to a particular religion. It may include meaning, forgiveness, rituals, readings, sacred objects, silence, meditation, music, connection with nature, or time with a chaplain or community leader. Cultural needs may involve who is present, how the body is touched, modesty expectations, language needs, food or fluid practices, timing of rituals, or beliefs about what should happen after death. The CNA does not need to be an expert in every belief system. The safe approach is to ask respectfully when appropriate, avoid assumptions, protect items from being moved unnecessarily, and report requests to the nurse.

  Family dynamics can be difficult. One person may want constant updates while another is quiet. Some families disagree about visitors or rituals. A CNA should not take sides, interpret legal documents, explain consent, or decide who receives medical information. The CNA can say, “I want to make sure the nurse knows your question,” or “Let me check the care plan and policy before I move anything.” Protect confidentiality by speaking only with authorized people through approved channels. Do not share resident stories, photos, recordings, or identifying details in class activities, text messages, or social media.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Signs of Approaching Death and Comfort Measures Within CNA Scope

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3
lane: CNA renewal support online CE
unit_id: U10
activity_id: U10_BOOK_SEGMENT_3_SIGNS_COMFORT
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 3 — Signs of Approaching Death and CNA Comfort Measures
moodle_description: Third lesson page on signs of approaching death, reportable observations, and comfort measures the CNA may provide when assigned.
completion_tracking: Automatic completion on view plus expected engagement time if configured.
restrict_access: Available after U10 Book Page Lesson Segment 2 completion.
gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions: Study the signs and comfort actions as observation-and-reporting knowledge. This online CE does not validate hands-on performance.
content_body: |
  As death approaches, body systems may slow and patterns may change. A resident may sleep more, eat or drink less, withdraw from conversation, have difficulty swallowing, produce less urine, become incontinent, or seem restless. Hands and feet may become cool. Skin may appear pale, dusky, or mottled. Breathing may become irregular, with long pauses, shallow breaths, noisy secretions, or a pattern that changes over time. The resident may be less responsive or may appear to speak to people who are not present. These signs can be distressing to families. The CNA reports observations promptly and uses words that describe what was seen, heard, measured, or stated.

  Comfort measures are usually simple but important. Depending on the care plan and direction from licensed staff, the CNA may provide mouth care, apply lip moisturizer if allowed, reposition with pillows, smooth linens, reduce noise, adjust lighting, offer a cool cloth, provide perineal care, protect skin, keep the resident covered, and support family privacy. The CNA may notice pain cues such as grimacing, moaning, guarding, restlessness, clenched hands, or increased resistance to touch. Pain assessment and medication decisions belong to licensed staff, but CNA observations help the nurse respond.

  Safety and comfort go together. Do not force food or fluids on a resident who is not safe to swallow or who declines. Do not raise the head of the bed, turn the resident, or use devices contrary to the care plan. Do not change oxygen, suctioning, medication, or hospice equipment unless your role, training, and facility policy specifically allow an assigned task. When family members want to help, guide them only within approved boundaries, such as speaking softly, holding a hand if acceptable, or asking the nurse how they may participate.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Hospice, Biological Death, and Postmortem Boundaries

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 4
lane: CNA renewal support online CE
unit_id: U10
activity_id: U10_BOOK_SEGMENT_4_HOSPICE_POSTMORTEM
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 4 — Hospice and Postmortem Boundaries
moodle_description: Fourth lesson page on hospice goals, team communication, signs that require immediate reporting, and boundaries after death.
completion_tracking: Automatic completion on view plus expected engagement time if configured.
restrict_access: Available after U10 Book Page Lesson Segment 3 completion.
gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions: Read this segment for role clarity. The CNA supports comfort and dignity but does not pronounce death, interpret legal orders, or release the body.
content_body: |
  Hospice focuses on comfort, quality of life, symptom support, and family support for a person with terminal illness. A resident receiving hospice may still live in a long-term care setting and may receive daily care from facility staff while hospice professionals provide additional services. The CNA remains part of the care team by following the plan, noticing changes, reporting symptoms, and treating the resident as a whole person rather than as a diagnosis. Hospice does not mean “no care.” It means the care goals emphasize comfort and the resident’s values.

  Some findings require immediate reporting even when death is expected. Report new or worsening pain signs, difficulty breathing, choking, bleeding, falls, sudden agitation, new skin breakdown, family conflict affecting safety, equipment concerns, or requests that are outside the care plan. Also report possible signs of biological death according to policy, such as no visible breathing, no response, marked color change, or other facility-defined indicators. The CNA does not pronounce death or decide that care should stop. A licensed nurse or other authorized person follows policy and law.

  Postmortem care begins only after required confirmation and direction. The CNA’s assigned responsibilities may include privacy, respectful positioning, bathing or cleansing, clean gown or linens, removal or securing of soiled items, and room preparation, but only according to facility policy, cultural wishes, and licensed direction. Do not remove tubes, lines, jewelry, personal items, identification bands, or medical devices unless specifically directed by policy and authorized staff. Belongings are handled through inventory and witness procedures. Continue confidentiality after death; the resident’s story and family details are still private.

content_body: |
  Scenario setup: Resident J is receiving hospice services in a long-term care setting. The care plan emphasizes comfort, mouth care, repositioning, privacy, and prompt reporting of changes. During evening rounds, you notice that Resident J is sleeping most of the time, has a dry mouth, and has breathing with long pauses. Two family members are at the bedside. One says, “Is this it? Are they about to die?” Another asks whether you can give water because the resident’s mouth looks dry. Later, the nurse asks you to remain available while the nurse evaluates the resident.

  Decision Point 1 — Family question about prognosis:
  A. “Yes, I think death will happen tonight.”
  B. “I can see why you are concerned. I will tell the nurse about the breathing pauses so the nurse can answer your questions.”
  C. “Do not ask questions like that; it will upset everyone.”
  D. “Look up that breathing pattern online and decide what you think.”
  Correct feedback: Choice B is safest because it acknowledges emotion, reports the observation, and refers prognosis questions to the nurse or hospice contact.
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, C, and D are unsafe because they predict, dismiss grief, or direct the family away from the care team.
  Remediation prompt: Rewrite the response in one sentence that includes empathy, observation reporting, and referral to the nurse.

  Decision Point 2 — Dry mouth and comfort:
  A. Give a large cup of thin water even though the resident is minimally responsive.
  B. Change the medication schedule so the resident will wake up.
  C. Provide assigned mouth care or lip comfort measures as allowed, keep the resident positioned per care plan, and report the dry mouth.
  D. Tell the family that dry mouth means the staff did something wrong.
  Correct feedback: Choice C is safest because mouth care, positioning, observation, and reporting are CNA comfort actions when assigned.
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, B, and D may create aspiration risk, exceed scope, or blame without facts.
  Remediation prompt: Select two comfort measures that are within CNA scope when they are in the care plan.

  Decision Point 3 — Possible death or postmortem boundary:
  A. Begin postmortem care and remove personal jewelry before the nurse arrives.
  B. Tell the roommate and visitors exactly what happened.
  C. Leave the door wide open so staff can see into the room from the hall.
  D. Protect privacy, stay calm, notify or follow the nurse’s direction immediately, and wait for authorized confirmation and policy-guided assignment.
  Correct feedback: Choice D is safest because the CNA protects dignity and follows facility direction without pronouncing death or handling belongings independently.
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, B, and C violate role boundaries, confidentiality, or privacy.
  Remediation prompt: List one action the CNA should wait to perform until directed by policy and authorized staff.

  Completion condition: The learner must answer all three decision points, view any remediation attached to an unsafe choice, and reach the final summary screen showing: support dignity, provide assigned comfort, report changes, refer prognosis questions, and follow postmortem policy.
