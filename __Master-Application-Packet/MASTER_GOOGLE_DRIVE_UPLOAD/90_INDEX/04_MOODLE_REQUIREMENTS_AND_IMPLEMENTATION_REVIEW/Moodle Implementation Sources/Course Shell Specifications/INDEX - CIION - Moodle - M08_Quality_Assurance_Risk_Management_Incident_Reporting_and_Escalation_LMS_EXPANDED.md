## Module Purpose

This module prepares RN case managers to recognize quality and risk events during care coordination, choose escalation pathways based on urgency, document factually, participate in just-culture follow-up, and preserve evidence that supports safe patient care and quality review. The module remains Draft / Pending BRN CEP Approval and supports 3 contact hours through reading, applied decisions, documentation practice, active interaction, quiz completion, and reflection.

## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of M08, the learner will be able to:

- Explain how quality assurance, risk management, incident reporting, complaints, near misses, and escalation support RN case management and patient safety.
- Assess patient-care risk signals, including changes in condition, unsafe transitions, medication discrepancies, missed services, complaints, and coordination delays.
- Prioritize immediate, same-day, routine, and quality-review pathways based on urgency, potential harm, patient vulnerability, and organizational policy.
- Document incident-related case management facts without blame, unsupported conclusions, copied official forms, or real identifying information in practice activities.
- Coordinate communication with the patient, caregiver, provider, supervisor, interdisciplinary team, and quality/risk personnel while staying within RN scope.
- Escalate unsafe conditions, unresolved risk, and reportable concerns through appropriate channels and time frames.
- Evaluate follow-up evidence, prevention actions, and quality review findings to reduce recurrence and strengthen continuity of care.

content_body:
Quality assurance in RN case management is the organized review of whether care coordination processes are supporting safe, timely, patient-centered care. Risk management is the related practice of recognizing conditions that may lead to harm, loss of continuity, regulatory concern, complaint, or preventable deterioration. Incident reporting is one tool within that larger quality and risk system. Escalation is the RN case manager's timely movement of a concern to the person or process that can address it.

In home health, community-based care, transitions of care, payer-supported case management, and care management programs, the RN may be the person who first hears that a patient fell, missed medication, could not obtain equipment, experienced a delay in ordered services, has worsening symptoms, or feels unsafe with the current plan. The RN case manager may not be the treating provider and may not control every operational process, but the RN does have responsibility to recognize patient-care significance, communicate through the right channels, document facts, and follow up until the next safe step is clear.

M08 uses four practical timing categories. Immediate escalation is used when there is current or likely imminent harm, a significant change in condition, urgent safety risk, need for emergency services, or a policy-defined reportable concern requiring rapid notification. Same-day escalation is used when the patient is not in immediate danger but a delay until tomorrow could reasonably worsen the risk, interrupt ordered care, or leave an unsafe condition unresolved. Routine follow-up is used when the issue can be handled through normal care coordination time frames without current safety risk. Quality-review referral is used when the event, trend, complaint, or near miss should be examined for process improvement even if the immediate patient need has been stabilized.

These categories are not a substitute for employer policy, payer requirements, scope rules, or applicable reporting laws. They are a learning framework for RN case management judgment. When a case is unclear, the safer professional action is to seek supervisory guidance, preserve factual documentation, and continue patient-centered follow-up.

content_body:
RN case management quality work begins before an incident report is written. It begins when the RN notices that a process, handoff, communication gap, environmental concern, medication issue, service delay, complaint, or change in condition may affect patient safety or continuity of care. The RN's first task is to connect the observation to patient-care risk. A missed delivery of wound supplies is not only a logistics problem if it may delay ordered wound care. A caregiver complaint is not only dissatisfaction if it reveals that discharge teaching was misunderstood. A near fall during a transfer is not only an awkward moment if it shows that equipment, teaching, supervision, or functional status changed.

Quality assurance looks at whether the care coordination system is performing as intended. Risk management looks at what could go wrong, what already went wrong, and what actions may reduce future harm. Incident reporting captures an event or near miss so the organization can respond, trend, investigate, and improve. Complaints provide patient or caregiver information about a perceived or actual gap in care. A near miss is especially important because it identifies a weakness before harm occurs. In RN case management, each of these inputs can reveal a need for assessment, teaching, communication, provider notification, supervisor review, or quality referral.

The RN should avoid treating quality language as separate from nursing judgment. A quality concern becomes meaningful when the RN can describe what happened, what risk was present, what the patient needed, who was notified, what follow-up was arranged, and what remains unresolved. The RN should also avoid assigning blame in the case management note. Blame language may distract from patient safety, weaken the usefulness of the record, and exceed what is known. Factual language supports both patient care and quality review.

Consider a patient who reports dizziness after discharge and mentions taking a sedating medication at a different time than listed on the discharge instructions. If the patient also nearly fell while walking to the bathroom, the RN case manager should recognize more than a routine teaching opportunity. The situation involves change in condition, medication safety, fall risk, need for clarification, and possible escalation. Quality thinking helps the RN ask whether instructions were clear, whether medication reconciliation was completed, whether caregiver support is adequate, and whether the process could be improved for the next patient.

content_body:
Escalation timing is a patient-safety decision. The RN case manager considers current symptoms, likelihood of harm, patient vulnerability, available support, policy requirements, and whether a delay would reasonably increase risk. The same event type can fall into different pathways depending on context. A missed appointment may be routine if it is a nonurgent annual visit with no active symptoms. It may require same-day escalation if it delays evaluation of worsening shortness of breath. It may require immediate action if the patient reports chest pain, severe respiratory distress, or another emergency symptom.

Immediate escalation is used when the RN identifies current danger, a significant change in condition, an urgent symptom pattern, suspected abuse or neglect according to applicable policy, a serious medication error with potential harm, or a situation where emergency services or rapid clinical direction may be needed. The RN's role is not to diagnose beyond scope or independently order treatment. The RN's role is to recognize danger, instruct the patient or caregiver according to policy and clinical judgment, notify the appropriate provider or emergency resource, alert the supervisor or risk process when required, and document the facts and actions.

Same-day escalation is used for unresolved risk that should not wait for routine processing. Examples include a new wound concern without systemic emergency signs, a medication discrepancy involving high-risk medication where the patient is stable but needs clarification, equipment failure that affects safe mobility, a caregiver report of repeated confusion, or a service delay that may interrupt ordered treatment. Same-day escalation often involves provider contact, supervisor notification, pharmacy clarification, interdisciplinary coordination, or urgent scheduling support.

Routine follow-up is appropriate when there is no current safety concern and the issue fits normal coordination workflow. Routine does not mean unimportant. It means the time frame is reasonable for the risk. A request for community resources, clarification of a future appointment, nonurgent transportation planning, or update to a care plan may be routine if the patient's condition is stable and no time-sensitive care is at risk.

Quality-review referral can accompany any pathway. A near miss may be resolved immediately for the patient and still deserve quality review because the process weakness could recur. A complaint may be handled respectfully and still need trend review. A same-day escalation may reveal that discharge instructions, handoff documentation, or service authorization steps are not reliable. The RN contributes to quality review by providing factual event details, follow-up outcomes, and process observations rather than personal accusations.

Read the event-type guidance and note how each example changes the RN case management response.

content_body:
An incident is an event that affected or could affect patient safety, care quality, or service reliability. In RN case management, incidents may include falls, medication discrepancies, missed critical services, unsafe discharge conditions, equipment failures, documentation gaps that affect care, communication failures, or patient complaints that reveal potential harm. The RN should follow organizational definitions and reporting procedures, but the case management reasoning begins with the patient's condition and the risk created by the event.

A near miss is an event that almost reached the patient or almost caused harm. Near misses are valuable because they expose a weak step while there is still time to improve. For example, a home oxygen delivery problem discovered before the patient runs out of oxygen is a near miss if it could have created respiratory risk. A medication list discrepancy found during a call before the patient takes the wrong dose is a near miss with direct safety relevance. The RN should not ignore near misses because no injury occurred. The absence of harm may reflect luck, caregiver vigilance, or the RN's intervention, not a safe process.

Complaints deserve respectful listening and careful routing. Some complaints involve customer service concerns, but many contain clinical or safety information. A caregiver who says, "No one explained what symptoms matter," may be describing an education gap. A patient who says, "I called twice and nobody called back," may be describing a communication failure that delayed care. The RN should acknowledge the concern, gather facts, avoid promises outside authority, explain the next step, and route the complaint according to policy.

Change in condition is a central escalation trigger. New confusion, worsening pain, dyspnea, fever, uncontrolled blood sugar, dizziness, weakness, bleeding, wound deterioration, medication side effects, functional decline, or inability to perform essential self-care may require provider notification or emergency guidance depending on severity. The RN case manager should distinguish between stable background information and new or worsening findings. A patient with chronic shortness of breath who reports sudden severe worsening is different from a patient with stable baseline exertional symptoms and an upcoming routine appointment.

The RN case manager should also recognize cumulative risk. One minor barrier may be routine, but multiple small barriers can create a serious safety concern. A delayed equipment delivery, caregiver fatigue, uncertain medication instructions, and a new fall history together create a stronger case for escalation than any single item alone. Quality assurance depends on seeing these patterns early enough to intervene.

content_body:
Incident-related documentation should help the next qualified reader understand what was known, what risk was present, what action was taken, who was contacted, what instructions were given, what follow-up is pending, and how the patient was protected. The RN case manager should document observed or reported facts, the source of information, relevant times, patient response, communication attempts, and follow-up outcomes. When the RN does not know something, the record should not pretend certainty. "Patient reported taking the evening dose at 0800 for two days" is more useful than "patient mismanaged medications." "Caregiver stated delivery had not arrived by 1500" is more useful than "vendor failed."

Factual documentation avoids blame, speculation, sarcasm, and unsupported conclusions. It also avoids copying incident forms, official forms, or confidential quality committee content into the learning activity. In real practice, organizations may separate the clinical record, incident reporting system, grievance process, and quality review files. The RN should follow policy about what belongs in each system. In this course, learners practice the clinical reasoning and neutral wording without using official forms or real identifiers.

Just culture is a quality approach that looks at system design, human factors, at-risk behavior, and reckless behavior without automatically blaming an individual for every bad outcome. For the RN case manager, a just-culture mindset means asking what process allowed the risk to develop, what safeguards worked, what safeguards failed, and how recurrence can be reduced. It does not mean ignoring accountability. It means that quality review is more useful when facts are clear and improvement actions are tied to process gaps.

For example, if a patient did not understand discharge medication changes, the RN should address the immediate patient risk and also consider process contributors. Was the medication list reconciled? Were instructions written in a way the patient could understand? Was caregiver teaching needed? Was the follow-up call scheduled soon enough? Did the patient have access barriers? Did communication between settings leave ambiguity? These questions help identify prevention actions such as clearer teaching, earlier follow-up, closed-loop pharmacy communication, or updated discharge handoff steps.

Confidentiality applies during quality and incident review. Only the minimum necessary information should be shared through appropriate channels. Practice activities in this module must use fictional details. In real practice, the RN should use approved reporting systems and secure communication routes.

Read the segment and focus on how follow-up evidence shows that an identified risk was addressed.

content_body:
Risk recognition is incomplete without follow-up. Once a concern is identified, the RN case manager should track whether the next safe step actually occurred. Follow-up evidence may include successful provider notification, patient or caregiver instructions, updated care plan, medication clarification, appointment confirmation, service restart, equipment replacement, interdisciplinary referral, supervisor review, complaint routing, or quality referral. The record should show not only that the RN noticed a risk, but also how the risk was moved toward resolution.

The RN should distinguish closure from transfer of responsibility. A case management note may show that the RN notified the provider and gave patient instructions, but the RN may still need to verify whether orders were received, whether the patient understood the plan, or whether the service gap was corrected. If responsibility shifts to another team member, the handoff should be clear enough that the next person knows the risk, the deadline, and the expected follow-up. Closed-loop communication is especially important when patient safety depends on another person acting.

Quality review evidence looks beyond the single patient event. It asks whether a pattern exists, whether policy was followed, whether an education or process change is needed, and whether prevention actions can be measured. A single complaint about confusing discharge instructions may lead to a review of teaching materials. Repeated same-day escalations for equipment delays may point to vendor communication gaps. Several near misses involving medication list discrepancies may indicate a need for stronger reconciliation steps.

Prevention actions should be specific enough to review later. "Educate staff" may be too vague if the underlying issue is unclear. A stronger prevention action might be, "Add a discharge-call prompt asking whether the patient has started, stopped, or changed any medication since leaving the facility," or "Require closed-loop confirmation when ordered safety equipment has not arrived within the expected time frame." The RN case manager's documentation can support these actions by naming the process gap and linking it to patient-care risk.

content_body:
Scenario setup: You are the RN case manager completing a scheduled follow-up call for a fictional patient, "Patient A," three days after discharge from an acute-care hospitalization. Patient A reports dizziness, a near fall while walking to the bathroom, and uncertainty about whether a sedating medication should be taken in the morning or at bedtime. The caregiver adds that the walker was delivered late, the patient skipped one dose of a prescribed medication because the instructions were confusing, and no one has called back about a home safety question. The patient denies current chest pain or severe shortness of breath in this fictional scenario, but reports feeling "too unsteady to walk alone." No real names, dates, addresses, facility names, or record numbers may be used in learner responses.

Decision Point 1: What is the best first RN case management action?

A. Classify the call as routine because the fall did not occur and the patient denies emergency symptoms.

B. Recognize a current safety risk, assess the immediate situation further, give safety instructions within policy, and begin same-day escalation for medication clarification and fall-risk follow-up.

C. Tell the caregiver to decide whether the medication should be changed because the caregiver is present.

D. Wait until the next scheduled call so the RN has more complete information.

Correct-path feedback: Choice B is the safest response. The patient has dizziness, a near fall, unsafe mobility, medication uncertainty, and a skipped dose. Even without emergency symptoms in this fictional scenario, the RN should assess immediate safety, provide policy-aligned instructions, and escalate the medication and fall-risk concern the same day.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, C, and D minimize risk or shift responsibility inappropriately. A near miss can still reveal serious risk. Medication clarification should not be delegated to the caregiver as an independent decision. Waiting may allow preventable harm.

Remediation prompt: Identify two facts from the scenario that move the issue beyond routine follow-up. Then identify who should be contacted before the end of the day according to the fictional case pathway.

Decision Point 2: Which documentation approach best supports patient care and quality review?

A. "Caregiver failed to follow instructions and caused medication confusion."

B. "Patient is unsafe at home because discharge process was poor."

C. "Patient reported dizziness and near fall during bathroom ambulation. Caregiver reported walker delivery delay and uncertainty about sedating medication timing. RN reviewed immediate safety precautions within policy, initiated provider/pharmacy clarification pathway, notified supervisor per risk process, and scheduled follow-up confirmation."

D. "Incident report completed; see confidential quality file for details."

Correct-path feedback: Choice C uses factual, neutral, patient-care language. It identifies the source of information, relevant risk, RN actions, escalation pathway, and follow-up plan without blame or unsupported conclusions.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A and B assign blame or make broad conclusions without enough facts. Choice D may omit clinically relevant case management information from the care record. In real practice, incident systems and clinical records may serve different purposes, and the RN should follow policy about what belongs in each.

Remediation prompt: Rewrite one blame-based phrase into factual language. Include the source of the information and the follow-up action.

Decision Point 3: After the patient is stabilized and same-day contacts are made, what quality follow-up is most appropriate?

A. Close the issue because the patient was not injured.

B. Send a broad message to all staff naming the caregiver and describing the complaint.

C. Treat the event only as a payer authorization problem.

D. Refer the near miss and communication delay for quality review, identify possible process contributors, and track whether medication clarification, equipment reliability, and callback expectations were resolved.

Correct-path feedback: Choice D connects the near miss to process review and prevention. The patient need still matters, but quality review also asks whether similar medication-instruction, equipment-delivery, or callback gaps could affect other patients.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, B, and C miss the quality purpose. No injury does not erase risk. Confidentiality still applies. Payer or vendor issues may be part of the process review, but the RN case manager should keep the focus on patient safety, communication, and follow-up evidence.

Remediation prompt: Name one prevention action that could reduce recurrence and one piece of evidence that would show the action was completed.

You are not deciding medical treatment. You are deciding how quickly the RN case manager should move information to the right clinical, supervisory, operational, or quality channel.

Use these pathway definitions:

- Immediate: current or likely imminent harm, serious change in condition, emergency symptoms, suspected abuse or neglect according to policy, or another concern requiring rapid clinical or supervisory direction.
- Same-day: unresolved risk that is not an emergency in the fictional facts but should not wait for routine processing.
- Routine: stable issue that fits normal care coordination time frames without current safety risk.
- Quality-review: event, trend, complaint, or near miss that should be reviewed for process improvement after immediate patient needs are addressed.

Sample events to map:

1. Patient reports sudden severe shortness of breath during a follow-up call.
2. Caregiver reports the patient nearly fell twice after delayed walker delivery, but the patient is currently seated safely and denies emergency symptoms.
3. Patient requests a list of transportation resources for a routine appointment next month.
4. Three patients in one week report confusing medication instructions after discharge.
5. Patient states a wound dressing supply has not arrived and the next dressing change is due tonight.
6. Patient says a staff member was rude but reports no current clinical concern.
7. Pharmacy identifies a high-risk medication discrepancy before the patient takes the first dose.
8. A callback about new dizziness was not returned until the following day.

For each event, select a pathway and write one sentence explaining the patient-care risk or quality-review reason. If you choose quality-review only, explain why no immediate patient-care escalation is needed in the fictional facts. If you choose immediate or same-day, explain whether quality review may also be appropriate after the patient need is addressed.
