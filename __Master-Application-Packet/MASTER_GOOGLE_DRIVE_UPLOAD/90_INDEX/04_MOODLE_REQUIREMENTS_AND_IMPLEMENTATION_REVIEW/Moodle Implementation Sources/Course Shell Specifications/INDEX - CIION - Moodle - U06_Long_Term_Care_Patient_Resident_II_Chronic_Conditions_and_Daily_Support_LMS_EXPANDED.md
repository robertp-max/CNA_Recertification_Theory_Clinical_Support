content_body:
Long-term care residents often live with more than one ongoing condition. A resident may have dementia, arthritis, diabetes, stroke effects, chronic lung disease, heart disease, depression, anxiety, a developmental disability, a seizure disorder, or changes related to aging. These conditions can affect memory, sleep, appetite, mobility, communication, toileting, comfort, and willingness to participate in care. The CNA does not diagnose the condition or decide the treatment plan. The CNA gives daily support, notices changes from the resident's usual pattern, and reports objective information to the licensed nurse.

By the end of this unit, you should be able to describe dementia-sensitive communication; distinguish validation, redirection, reality orientation, and reminiscence; support residents with developmental and mental health needs without stigma; recognize chronic-illness observations that affect daily care; and report behavior or function changes in factual language. You will apply these skills in a fictional evening-care scenario where a resident becomes distressed and says they need to go home to feed children.

Daily support is not just a list of tasks. It is the way care is offered. Residents are more likely to feel safe when staff explain before touching, use the resident's preferred name, offer simple choices, allow time, reduce noise, protect privacy, and respect cultural and personal preferences. A calm approach can prevent distress from escalating. When distress does occur, the CNA looks for unmet needs such as pain, hunger, thirst, toileting, fatigue, fear, overstimulation, loneliness, or confusion.

completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; recommended minimum expected engagement time 15 minutes.
restrict_access: Available after overview completion.

learner_instructions:
Read this lesson page and identify at least two ways a CNA can respond when a resident's words do not match the current time or place.
content_body:
Dementia is a general term for changes in memory, judgment, language, attention, perception, and daily function. Alzheimer's disease is one cause of dementia, and other related conditions may also affect cognition. A resident with dementia may ask the same question many times, search for a past home or workplace, resist care, wander, call out, misplace items, or become frightened by routine care. These actions are not "bad behavior." They are often communication about a feeling, need, or environment that the resident cannot explain in the usual way.

A CNA begins by checking safety and looking for possible unmet needs. Is the resident in pain? Does the resident need the toilet? Is the room too loud, cold, dark, crowded, or rushed? Has the routine changed? Is the resident hungry, thirsty, tired, or worried about a familiar responsibility from earlier life? The CNA reports patterns so the nurse and care team can adjust approaches in the care plan.

Validation means acknowledging the resident's feelings or perceived reality to reduce distress. If a resident says, "I need to go home to feed my children," a validating response might be, "You sound worried about your children. You took good care of them." Validation is not the same as making fun of the resident or arguing facts. Redirection gently shifts attention to a safe next step, such as looking at a family photo, folding towels, listening to familiar music, walking with staff if assigned, or having a snack if allowed. Reminiscence invites meaningful memories: "Tell me about what evenings were like at your home." Reality orientation may be useful for some residents when it is care-plan guided and not distressing, but repeatedly correcting a distressed resident can make fear worse.

The CNA's tone matters. Slow movements, a calm voice, eye-level approach, simple words, and one instruction at a time can reduce fear. Explain before touch, ask permission when possible, and offer choices that are real and safe: "Would you like to wash your face first or brush your hair first?" When care cannot be completed safely, stop, keep the resident safe, report to the nurse, and document the objective facts.

moodle_description: Book page on respectful support for residents with developmental disabilities and mental health needs.
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; recommended minimum expected engagement time 15 minutes.
restrict_access: Available after segment 1 completion.
gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions:
Read the lesson and focus on person-first, ability-focused support. Notice which observations should be reported without labeling the resident.
content_body:
Long-term care residents may have intellectual or developmental disabilities, cerebral palsy, autism-related support needs, seizure disorders, Parkinson's disease, traumatic brain injury, anxiety, depression, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, post-traumatic stress, substance-use history, or other conditions that affect daily routines. A diagnosis does not define the person. The CNA should focus on the resident's abilities, preferences, communication style, usual routine, safety needs, and response to support.

Respectful communication begins with addressing the resident directly, not speaking over them as if they are absent. Use adult language with adults. Give one step at a time when a resident is overwhelmed. Allow extra time for speech, movement, or decision-making. Ask yes/no or either/or questions when open-ended choices are too difficult. Follow the care plan for communication boards, hearing aids, glasses, adaptive utensils, transfer devices, seizure precautions, or behavioral support strategies. If a resident uses gestures, pictures, or short phrases, treat that communication as meaningful.

Mental health needs require the same dignity as physical health needs. A resident who withdraws, cries, paces, startles, refuses care, sleeps more, eats less, or expresses fear may be showing distress, pain, grief, medication effects, environmental stress, or a change in condition. The CNA reports observations and resident statements without diagnosing. Avoid labels such as "attention seeking," "crazy," "lazy," or "noncompliant." Instead, record what was seen or heard: "Resident stayed in room after lunch, ate 25 percent, stated, 'I do not want to be around people today,' and declined group activity. Nurse notified."

Safety remains important. If the resident talks about wanting to harm self or others, reports hallucinations that create fear, has a seizure, falls, shows sudden confusion, or has a major change in function, follow facility policy and notify the nurse immediately. Keep privacy and dignity central while getting help. The CNA is not expected to counsel, diagnose, or decide medication needs.

learner_instructions:
Read this lesson and think about how small daily changes can signal a larger change in condition. Focus on what to report and how to support independence.
content_body:
Chronic illness often shows up during ordinary care. A resident with chronic lung disease may need extra rest during bathing. A resident with arthritis may dress slowly because of stiffness or pain. A resident with diabetes may show shakiness, sweating, unusual sleepiness, hunger, or confusion that must be reported. A resident with heart disease may become short of breath with activity or develop swelling. A resident after stroke may have weakness, swallowing concerns, communication changes, or frustration with tasks that used to be easy. The CNA supports the care plan and reports changes; the nurse evaluates the meaning.

Functional ability means what the resident can do in daily life. This includes bed mobility, transfers, walking, wheelchair use, bathing, dressing, grooming, eating, toileting, communication, and participation in activities. Function can change gradually or suddenly. A resident who usually walks to the dining room but now stops to catch breath, grips the rail, and asks to sit needs objective reporting. A resident who normally feeds self but begins dropping utensils, coughing, or leaving most food untouched also needs reporting.

Good daily support balances safety and independence. Do not take over every task just because it is faster. Set up supplies, place items within reach, cue one step at a time, encourage rest breaks, and praise effort without treating the adult like a child. Protect skin by reporting redness, moisture, pressure, pain, or device rubbing. Protect nutrition and hydration by reporting poor intake, swallowing concerns, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, or fluid restriction concerns. Protect comfort by reporting pain statements, grimacing, guarding, new agitation, or refusal that may be related to discomfort.

The care plan guides what the CNA may do. Follow assigned transfer methods, diet instructions, activity limits, equipment use, toileting schedules, and safety precautions. If the resident asks for something outside the plan, such as an unsafe food, unassigned transfer, or medication advice, pause and ask the nurse. Staying within scope protects the resident and the CNA.

completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; recommended minimum expected engagement time 15 minutes.

gradebook_setting: None; not graded.
learner_instructions:
Read this page and practice changing labels into facts. The goal is to help the nurse understand what happened before, during, and after a behavior or function change.
content_body:
Objective reporting is one of the most useful contributions a CNA makes. The licensed nurse and care team need details that can be evaluated, not labels that blame the resident. Instead of "resident was difficult," describe the facts: time, place, what was happening before the distress, the resident's exact words if relevant, what the resident did, safety concerns, support offered, response to support, and who was notified. This pattern is sometimes described as antecedent, behavior, and consequence: what came before, what was observed, and what happened after.

Behavior reports should include possible triggers without pretending to know the cause. A CNA might report that distress began when the room became noisy, when care was rushed, when the resident was awakened, when clothing was removed without explanation, or when the resident could not find a familiar item. The CNA may also report what helped: lower voice, family photo, warm blanket, toileting, snack, quiet area, favorite music, or a different caregiver approach.

Function reports should compare to the resident's usual pattern when known. Useful details include distance walked, level of assistance, balance, shortness of breath, pain statement, fatigue, appetite, swallowing signs, continence change, sleep change, skin change, or participation in ADLs. Avoid writing a diagnosis such as "depression is worse" or "dementia progressed." Instead, write, "Resident remained in bed until 1030 for three mornings, ate less than half of breakfast, and stated, 'I feel too tired to get dressed.' Nurse notified."

Privacy rules apply to every report and learning activity. In Moodle, use only fictional scenario information. In the workplace, follow facility policy for charting and reporting in approved systems. Do not post, text, or discuss resident information outside the care team. Objective reporting protects dignity because it describes what happened without shaming the resident.

content_body:
Scenario setup: Resident F has dementia and usually accepts evening care when staff approach slowly, explain each step, and offer a family photo during grooming. Tonight the hallway is noisy. Resident F becomes tearful, pulls the sweater close, and says, "I need to go home to feed my children." When an aide reaches quickly for the resident's sleeve, Resident F pulls away and says, "Do not make me late." The care plan says to use calm validation, offer simple choices, reduce noise when possible, and report behavior patterns or function changes to the nurse.

Decision point 1: What is the best opening response?

- Choice A: "Your children are adults now. You live here, so stop worrying."  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: This argues facts and may increase distress. Even if the statement is not current reality, the resident's worry is real.  
  Remediation prompt: Identify the feeling first, then choose words that acknowledge worry without shaming.
- Choice B: "You sound worried about your children. You took good care of them. Would you like to tell me about evening meals at home?"  
  Correct feedback: This validates the feeling and invites reminiscence. It gives the CNA a calm bridge toward safer care.
- Choice C: "If you do not cooperate, I will skip your care and tell everyone you refused."  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Threatening or shaming violates dignity and does not solve the unmet need or safety concern.  
  Remediation prompt: Replace threats with a calm choice and a report if care cannot be completed safely.

Decision point 2: Resident F pulls away again when care is rushed. What should the CNA do next?

- Choice A: Slow down, explain before touch, offer "wash face first or brush hair first," move away from hallway noise if safe, and keep the family photo visible.  
  Correct feedback: This follows the care-plan approach, reduces triggers, offers safe choices, and supports dignity.
- Choice B: Ask another aide to hold the resident's arm so care can be finished quickly.  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Forcing care can harm the resident and escalate fear. If safety is a concern or care cannot be completed, stop and notify the nurse.  
  Remediation prompt: Choose the least distressing safe approach and report barriers to care.
- Choice C: Leave the resident alone at the bedside with supplies scattered because the resident is upset.  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Walking away without safety checks can create fall or injury risk.  
  Remediation prompt: Keep the resident safe, remove immediate hazards as appropriate, and get help according to policy.
- Choice D: Tell the resident that the diagnosis is causing the behavior and that staff know best.  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Explaining a diagnosis in this way is not supportive and may feel dismissive.  
  Remediation prompt: Focus on the resident's words, feelings, safety, and response to the care-plan strategy.

Decision point 3: Which report to the nurse is most objective?

- Choice A: "Resident was manipulative during evening care and acted crazy."  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: This is labeling, not objective reporting. It does not describe what happened or what helped.
- Choice B: "The dementia is worse and the resident probably needs medication."  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: The CNA does not diagnose progression or decide medication needs.  
  Remediation prompt: Report observed behavior, resident statements, safety concerns, support offered, response, and notification.
- Choice C: "At 1900 Resident F was tearful, stated, 'I need to go home to feed my children,' and pulled the right arm away when reached quickly. Resident calmed when family photo was shown and choices were offered. Evening grooming completed. Nurse notified."  
  Correct feedback: This report includes time, exact words, observed action, support used, response, completion of care, and notification.
- Choice D: "No report is needed because this happened before."  
  Unsafe-choice feedback: Patterns still matter. Repeated or escalating distress should be reported so the care team can review triggers and the care plan.  
  Remediation prompt: Choose reporting language that helps the nurse see the pattern without blaming the resident.

Completion condition: The learner must complete all three decision points, view feedback, complete remediation after any unsafe choice, and reach the final "U06 scenario complete" page.
