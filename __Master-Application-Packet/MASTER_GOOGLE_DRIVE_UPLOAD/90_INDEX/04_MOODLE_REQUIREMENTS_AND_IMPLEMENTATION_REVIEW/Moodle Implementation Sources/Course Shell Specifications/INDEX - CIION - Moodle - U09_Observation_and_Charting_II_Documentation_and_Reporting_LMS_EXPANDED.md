learner_instructions: Read the overview and identify one documentation habit you want to strengthen during the unit.
content_body:
CNA documentation is more than a clerical task. It is how routine care becomes visible to the care team. A bath, meal, transfer, toileting episode, behavior change, skin observation, refusal, or safety event may affect the plan of care. When documentation is accurate, nurses and other team members can see patterns and respond. When documentation is missing, late, vague, or inaccurate, the resident can be placed at risk because the team may not have the facts needed to make safe decisions.

This unit builds on objective observation by focusing on what happens after you notice something. You will examine where CNAs may document, how ADL support can affect MDS-related assessments and care planning, why legal charting principles matter, and how to correct an entry without hiding or changing the truth. You will also practice separating immediate reporting from routine documentation. Reporting is communication to the proper person at the proper time. Documentation is the official record of facts, care provided, resident response, and notifications according to facility policy. Both are required, but one does not replace the other.

By the end of U09, you should be able to identify common charting documents, document ADL assistance according to facility definitions, select factual wording for unusual events, explain why falsification is unsafe and unethical, follow a correction process when an error is made, and protect confidentiality in electronic and paper records. All practice information in this unit is fictional and must stay fictional.

moodle_description: Explains common documentation locations and the CNA role in creating an accurate record trail.

learner_instructions: Read the segment, then think about which documents in your workplace require CNA entries and which require notification to licensed staff before charting.
content_body:
A resident record is not one single note. It is a collection of documents and electronic fields that together show what was planned, what care was provided, how the resident responded, and what changes were reported. Depending on the facility, CNA documentation may appear in an electronic health record, paper flow sheet, assignment sheet, ADL record, intake and output record, meal percentage screen, bowel and bladder log, bathing or skin observation sheet, turning and repositioning record, restorative nursing record, behavior observation log, shower sheet, weight record, safety round, or narrative note. A care plan or Kardex may tell you what to do, while a charting screen or flow sheet records what actually happened.

Each document has a purpose. ADL records show the assistance needed for bathing, dressing, grooming, eating, toileting, bed mobility, transfers, walking, and wheelchair mobility. Intake records show meal percentages and fluid amounts when assigned. Bowel and bladder documentation can show constipation, diarrhea, incontinence patterns, toileting response, and skin risk. Restorative records can show whether the resident participated, what cues or help were needed, and what stopped the activity. Behavior logs can show time, trigger, resident statement, staff response, and outcome without labeling or blaming.

Unusual events may require both routine charting and an internal incident or variance report according to facility policy. An incident report is not a place for opinions, blame, or cover-up wording. It is a facility process for recording facts about an unusual event and helping the facility review safety. CNAs should know who to notify, when the nurse must assess first, and where to document. Never copy an official form into this course and never invent a form for practice that resembles a government document.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — ADL and MDS-Related Documentation Accuracy

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 2
lane: CNA renewal online CE
unit_id: U09
activity_id: U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_02
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 2 — ADL Support, MDS-Related Records, and Care Planning
moodle_description: Connects CNA ADL charting to care planning, resident function, and MDS-related assessment processes without reproducing official assessment forms.
completion_tracking: Require view of this book page and expected engagement time before unlocking Segment 3.
restrict_access: Available after U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_01 completion.
gradebook_setting: No grade; completion-only evidence.
learner_instructions: Read the segment and focus on the difference between what the resident did and what staff did to assist.
content_body:
ADL documentation must describe real performance, not assumptions. If a resident usually dresses independently but today needed hands-on help because of weakness, the entry should reflect today's care. If a resident usually needs help but completed more of the task with encouragement, that should also be documented. The care team depends on these details to understand function, risk, and progress. In many long-term care settings, ADL documentation also supports MDS-related assessment and care planning processes. CNAs do not complete the full assessment independently, but the daily facts they record can influence how accurately the team understands the resident's needs.

The most useful ADL documentation separates resident self-performance from staff support. For example, the resident may wash the face after setup, need verbal cueing for sequencing, require weight-bearing assistance for transfer, refuse a shower but accept a partial bed bath, or be unable to complete toileting because of fatigue. Some systems use terms such as setup help, supervision, limited assistance, extensive assistance, total dependence, or activity did not occur. Do not guess what a term means. Use the facility definition, ask a nurse or supervisor when unsure, and document after the care is performed.

Accuracy protects the resident. Overstating independence may cause the team to miss fall risk, skin risk, pain, or need for adaptive equipment. Overstating dependence may reduce opportunities for independence and dignity. Charting every resident the same way because it is faster creates a false picture. Copy-forward entries can be dangerous if yesterday's condition is carried into today's record without checking whether it is still true. If the resident refuses care, document the refusal factually, report as required, and include the safe alternatives offered according to policy. Do not punish, argue, or write judgmental statements.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Legal Charting Principles and Correction Process

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3
lane: CNA renewal online CE
unit_id: U09
activity_id: U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_03
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 3 — Timely, Factual, Complete, and Corrected Properly
moodle_description: Teaches legal charting principles, common unsafe practices, and how to correct documentation errors according to policy.
completion_tracking: Require view of this book page and expected engagement time before unlocking Segment 4.
restrict_access: Available after U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_02 completion.
gradebook_setting: No grade; completion-only evidence.
learner_instructions: Read the segment and compare safe charting habits with unsafe shortcuts.
content_body:
A resident chart is a legal record. Good charting is timely, factual, complete, legible when written, entered under the correct user, and completed within the CNA role. Timely means charting as close to the care or observation as policy requires, not hours later from memory unless a late-entry process is used. Factual means recording what you saw, heard, measured, smelled, did, and reported. Complete means including key details such as time, location, care provided, resident response, refusal, pain statement, safety concern, and who was notified when notification was required.

Unsafe charting habits include documenting care before it is done, charting for another staff member, sharing passwords, copying the same entry across residents, using unapproved abbreviations, guessing meal intake, omitting a fall or skin change, erasing a paper entry, deleting an electronic entry to hide an error, backdating, blaming another person, or changing wording because someone is worried about discipline. Falsification harms residents and can harm the CNA's certification and employment. If a coworker suggests wording that is not true, the safe response is to refuse, report through the chain of command as required, and document only facts you know.

Errors happen, and the correction process is designed to preserve the record. Follow facility policy. In an electronic record, use the approved amendment, correction, or late-entry function and notify the appropriate supervisor if required. In a paper record, policy may require a single line through the mistake, the word error or a similar notation, initials, date, time, and the correct information; never use correction fluid or scribble out the entry. A late entry should identify the current date and time and the actual time of the care or observation. Corrections should make the record clearer, not hide what happened.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Reporting Versus Documentation

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 4
lane: CNA renewal online CE
unit_id: U09
activity_id: U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_04
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 4 — What to Report Now and What to Record
moodle_description: Clarifies immediate reporting, routine documentation, notification wording, and confidentiality practices.
completion_tracking: Require view of this book page and expected engagement time before unlocking the scenario/application.
restrict_access: Available after U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_03 completion.
gradebook_setting: No grade; completion-only evidence.
learner_instructions: Read the segment and notice how reporting and documentation work together without replacing each other.
content_body:
Reporting is timely communication to the correct person. Documentation is the official record after care, observation, or notification. A CNA may need to report before documenting when there is a change in condition, injury, fall or slide to the floor, new pain, difficulty breathing, choking risk, bleeding, skin tear, suspected abuse, elopement risk, refusal that affects safety, missing item, or any event facility policy identifies as urgent. Opening the chart is not enough if the nurse needs to know now. Waiting until the end of the shift to document an urgent change can delay assessment and treatment.

A strong report is brief but complete. It identifies the resident, the immediate concern, what was observed, what the resident stated, time and location, safety actions already taken, and what help is needed. A CNA does not diagnose. Instead of saying, “Resident had a stroke,” a scope-safe report might say, “Resident's speech became slurred at lunch, right side of face appears uneven, and resident dropped the cup; nurse notified immediately.” The nurse evaluates, directs, and documents within licensed scope. The CNA documents assigned facts and notifications according to policy.

Documentation should show that the report occurred when notification is required: who was notified, when, and about what. For example, “At 1430 resident stated left forearm hurts after slide from wheelchair; small open skin area observed; nurse Lee notified at 1432” is more useful than “resident fine.” If the nurse gives direction, follow the care plan and policy for whether the direction is documented by the CNA or nurse. Protect confidentiality during every step. Turn screens away from public view, log out, do not share passwords, keep papers secure, avoid hallway conversations, and never place real resident information in Moodle activities.

restrict_access: Available after U09_BOOK_SEGMENT_04 completion.

learner_instructions: Work through the fictional scenario. Choose the safest action at each decision point. If you choose an unsafe option, read the feedback and complete the remediation prompt before continuing.
content_body:
Scenario setup: Resident K is seated in a wheelchair near the closet after morning care. While reaching for a sweater, Resident K slides from the wheelchair to the floor. Resident K says, “I don't think I hit my head, but my arm stings.” You see a small open skin area on the left forearm. Another aide says, “Do not make a big deal. Just chart that you assisted with transfer.” This scenario is fictional. Do not add real names, locations, or identifying details.

Decision Point 1 — What should you do first?

A. Stay with the resident, call or send for the licensed nurse according to policy, observe and protect safety without moving the resident unless directed or immediate danger exists.  
Correct feedback: This is the safe path. A slide to the floor and possible injury require immediate reporting and nurse assessment according to policy. Documentation will follow; it does not replace the report.

B. Help the resident back into the wheelchair quickly and chart later if pain continues.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Moving the resident before appropriate assessment may worsen injury and hides important facts. Remediation prompt: Review Segment 4 and identify why reporting comes before routine charting.

C. Chart “assisted with transfer” because the coworker suggests it.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: That wording is false and omits the unusual event. Remediation prompt: Review Segment 3 and rewrite the fact pattern without blame or cover-up wording.

Decision Point 2 — Which documentation statement is most factual after the nurse has been notified and policy directions are followed?

A. “Resident was careless and fell because the wheelchair was too far from the closet.”  
Unsafe-choice feedback: This blames and assumes cause. Remediation prompt: Remove opinions and keep only observed facts, resident statements, and notification.

B. “Resident completed transfer with assistance and no concerns.”  
Unsafe-choice feedback: This is falsification because it contradicts the event. Remediation prompt: State what actually happened, not what would be easier to explain.

C. “At 0915, Resident K slid from wheelchair to floor while reaching toward closet; stated, ‘my arm stings’; small open area observed on left forearm; nurse notified at 0916.”  
Correct feedback: This entry is timely, factual, objective, includes the resident statement, identifies the observation, and documents notification.

Decision Point 3 — You accidentally entered the event under the wrong resident record. What is the safest correction process?

A. Delete the entry if possible and pretend it did not happen.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Hiding a mistaken entry can create another record problem. Remediation prompt: Review the correction process and explain why transparency protects the record.

B. Follow facility policy for electronic amendment or correction, notify the appropriate supervisor if required, document the correct information in the correct record, and do not backdate or erase.  
Correct feedback: This preserves the record and corrects the error through the approved process.

C. Ask the coworker to enter a replacement note under their login so your name is not involved.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Sharing responsibility by misusing another login is not ethical or accurate. Remediation prompt: Identify why each CNA must document only under the correct user account.

Completion condition: The learner must complete all three decision points. Unsafe choices require viewing feedback and completing the remediation prompt before the activity can be marked complete.
