estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 1 — Pulse Findings Are Numbers Plus Feel
moodle_description: Explains pulse rate, rhythm, strength, common terms, and CNA reporting responsibilities for abnormal pulse findings.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after U02 overview page is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify which pulse findings need prompt reporting when paired with symptoms or change from baseline.
content_body: |
  A pulse is the wave of blood felt in an artery as the heart pumps. CNAs commonly measure a radial pulse when assigned, but facility policy and the care plan determine the site, equipment, timing, and whether a licensed nurse must take or verify a finding. Pulse reporting should include more than the rate. The rate is the number of beats per minute. Rhythm describes whether the beats are regular or irregular. Strength describes whether the pulse feels strong, bounding, weak, or thready. A pulse may also be described by site when required, such as radial, pedal, or another approved site.

  Common terms help the care team communicate quickly. Tachycardia generally means a fast pulse. Bradycardia generally means a slow pulse. Arrhythmia means an irregular rhythm. A bounding pulse may feel very strong. A thready pulse may feel weak and difficult to feel. The CNA does not decide why the pulse is fast, slow, strong, weak, or irregular. Causes may involve activity, fever, pain, anxiety, fluid balance, medication effects, heart problems, respiratory distress, or other factors that licensed staff evaluate. The CNA reports what was measured and what was observed.

  Pulse findings become more urgent when they appear with distress or a change from the resident's usual pattern. Examples include a new irregular pulse with dizziness, weakness, faintness, chest discomfort, shortness of breath, pale or bluish color, confusion, sweating, or a resident statement such as "my heart is racing." If the resident has just walked, transferred, or used the bathroom, activity may help explain why the rate is different, but it does not erase the need to report concerning signs. A safe report includes the value, timing, activity, resident statement, related observations, and who was notified.

  Objective pulse charting avoids labels such as "heart trouble" or "panic attack." A scope-safe note might say: "Pulse 112 after return from bathroom; rhythm regular to palpation; resident stated, 'I feel weak'; nurse notified." If the pulse feels irregular, document only according to facility policy and report promptly. When in doubt, ask the nurse to reassess.

learner_visible_title: Lesson 2 — Breathing Observations That Cannot Wait
moodle_description: Reviews respiration rate, rhythm, depth, effort, breath sounds, color changes, and abnormal-breathing terms.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and look for the difference between routine respiration counting and urgent respiratory distress reporting.
content_body: |
  Respirations are counted by observing breathing for rate, rhythm, depth, and effort. Rate is breaths per minute. Rhythm may be regular, irregular, or patterned with pauses. Depth may be normal, shallow, or deep. Effort describes how hard the resident appears to work to breathe. A resident who is sitting calmly and breathing easily is different from a resident who is leaning forward, using neck or shoulder muscles, pausing between words, or saying, "I cannot catch my breath."

  CNAs often count respirations after measuring pulse because calling attention to breathing may change the resident's pattern. Follow facility policy for timing and documentation. Record the rate and report the associated picture when it matters: labored respirations, noisy breathing, wheezing or gurgling sounds, shallow breaths, long pauses, coughing, sputum concerns, restlessness, bluish lips or nail beds, sudden anxiety, or a resident statement about shortness of breath. Cyanosis means a bluish discoloration that can signal low oxygen and requires prompt attention. Dyspnea means difficult or labored breathing. Apnea means a pause or absence of breathing. Tachypnea means rapid breathing; bradypnea means slow breathing. Cheyne-Stokes respirations are a pattern of breaths that gradually increase and decrease with periods of apnea; report this pattern when observed.

  Respiratory distress is a priority because breathing problems can worsen quickly. The CNA should stay with the resident as safe, position according to the care plan or nurse direction, keep the call light available, reduce exertion, and notify the licensed nurse or emergency chain immediately according to facility policy. Do not delay urgent reporting to finish routine charting. Do not adjust oxygen flow, apply new oxygen, or remove equipment unless trained, authorized, and directed by policy or licensed staff. If oxygen tubing, a nasal cannula, or other equipment appears displaced, report and follow local instructions.

  Strong respiratory reporting uses measurable facts and resident words. Instead of "breathing bad," say, "Respirations 26 and labored after bathroom; resident states, 'I cannot catch my breath'; lips appear bluish; nurse notified." This wording helps licensed staff act quickly and protects the resident by avoiding assumptions.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Combined TPR, Blood Pressure, and Pain Observations

estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 3 — Connecting TPR, Blood Pressure, and Pain Without Diagnosing
moodle_description: Connects combined TPR reporting, blood-pressure concepts, measurement context, and pain as a vital observation.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify how context changes the meaning of a vital-sign reading.
content_body: |
  TPR means temperature, pulse, and respirations. These readings are often reported together because the pattern can be more helpful than one value alone. A resident with fever, fast pulse, and rapid respirations may need prompt nursing assessment. A resident with normal temperature but new shortness of breath and a fast pulse also needs attention. Combined reporting gives the nurse the measured values, time, and surrounding observations instead of separate pieces of information.

  Blood pressure is written as systolic over diastolic. Systolic pressure is the higher number, measured when the heart contracts. Diastolic pressure is the lower number, measured when the heart relaxes. CNAs may measure blood pressure when trained and assigned, or they may report machine readings according to policy. Accuracy depends on using the correct cuff size, approved site, resident position, arm support, rest period, timing after activity, and equipment function. A reading taken immediately after walking to the bathroom may differ from a reading taken after rest, so context belongs in the report.

  Blood-pressure concerns are not only about a number. Report unusual readings according to facility parameters, especially when paired with dizziness, faintness, headache, blurred vision, chest discomfort, shortness of breath, weakness, confusion, sweating, fall, pain, or a major change from baseline. Orthostatic changes, when blood pressure drops with position change, are evaluated by licensed staff, but the CNA may observe dizziness, unsteadiness, or pallor when a resident sits or stands. Report those facts immediately and protect the resident from falls within the care plan.

  Pain is also part of the vital-sign picture. Pain can raise pulse, respirations, and blood pressure; it can reduce appetite, sleep, mobility, mood, and participation in care. The resident's pain report matters. Use the assigned pain scale when directed, ask location and description only within policy, observe guarding, grimacing, restlessness, sweating, reluctance to move, or changes in usual behavior, and report. Do not promise medication, decide whether pain is real, or diagnose the cause. A scope-safe report might say, "Resident rates chest tightness 7/10, pulse 104, respirations 24, skin pale, nurse notified immediately."

learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and practice turning vague labels into factual vital-sign reports.
content_body: |
  Abnormal vital-sign findings require two linked actions: timely reporting and accurate documentation. Reporting comes first when the resident may be unstable. A chart note entered later does not help a resident who is currently short of breath, dizzy, pale, weak, in severe pain, or showing bluish color. Follow the facility chain of command. If the nurse is not immediately available and the resident appears to be in distress, follow emergency policy rather than waiting silently.

  A complete CNA report answers practical questions: Who is the resident in the real care setting? What value did you measure? What was the site, position, route, or context if required? When was it taken? What was the resident doing before the reading? What did the resident say? What did you see, hear, or feel? What care-plan safety action did you take? Who was notified? In Moodle practice, replace identifying information with fictional labels such as Resident B.

  Objective charting records facts, not conclusions. Avoid "resident is faking," "has pneumonia," "heart attack," "anxiety attack," or "noncompliant." Instead, write what happened: "Resident returned from bathroom at 0910, respirations 26 and labored, pulse 112, stated 'I cannot catch my breath,' lips appeared slightly bluish, assisted to sit safely, nurse notified." This note is useful because it includes timing, values, observations, the resident's words, safety support, and notification.

  Documentation should be timely, complete, and corrected only according to policy. Do not backdate, copy inaccurate values forward, leave abnormal findings without a report, or chart care before it occurs. If a device gives a reading that does not fit the resident's condition, follow policy for recheck or nurse notification; do not hide the concern. If another staff member suggests waiting until the end of the shift because "the resident just walked," remember that activity context is information to report, not a reason to ignore cyanosis, labored breathing, severe pain, dizziness, or a new irregular pulse.

You measure pulse 112 and respirations 26, with labored effort. The resident's lips appear slightly bluish. The resident also reports chest tightness as 7 out of 10. You are in an online CE scenario, so use only fictional information and follow the scope-safe decision path.

  **Decision Point 1 — Immediate priority:** What should the CNA do first?

  A. Stay with Resident B as safe, help the resident sit or remain positioned according to the care plan, and notify the licensed nurse or emergency chain immediately with the measured facts.  
  B. Finish routine charting first because the resident just walked back from the bathroom and activity can change vital signs.  
  C. Tell the resident the problem is probably anxiety and encourage slow breathing without notifying anyone.

  **Correct feedback for A:** This response recognizes respiratory distress, cyanosis, tachycardia, pain statement, and activity context. It keeps the resident safe, uses the reporting chain, and avoids diagnosis.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B or C:** Charting cannot replace urgent reporting. Activity context matters, but labored respirations, bluish color, shortness-of-breath statement, and chest tightness require prompt escalation. Diagnosing anxiety is outside CNA scope.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Rewrite the first response using four actions: stay, position safely, notify, report facts.

  **Decision Point 2 — Best urgent report wording:** Which report gives the nurse the most useful scope-safe information?

  A. "Resident is breathing bad and looks scary; I think it might be a heart attack."  
  B. "Resident B returned from bathroom, pulse 112, respirations 26 and labored, states 'I cannot catch my breath' and chest tightness 7/10, lips appear slightly bluish."  
  C. "Resident walked too fast and probably caused the problem, but I will check again later."

  **Correct feedback for B:** This report includes time-related context, measured values, breathing effort, resident statements, pain rating, color observation, and no diagnosis.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A or C:** Vague language and diagnosis do not help the nurse act. Delaying because the resident walked can miss a serious change.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Remove labels and guesses. Add pulse, respirations, effort, color, resident words, pain rating, activity context, and notification.

  **Decision Point 3 — Objective charting after reporting:** After the nurse has been notified according to policy, which chart note is most appropriate?

  A. "Resident had anxiety attack after bathroom and was difficult about breathing."  
  B. "No note needed because nurse was told verbally."  
  C. "Resident probably needs oxygen; CNA will adjust flow next time."  
  D. "0910: Resident returned from bathroom, pulse 112, respirations 26 and labored; resident stated 'I cannot catch my breath' and rated chest tightness 7/10; lips appeared slightly bluish; assisted to sit safely; nurse notified."

  **Correct feedback for D:** This note is factual, timely, and within CNA scope. It includes measured values, resident statements, visible observations, safety support, and notification.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A, B, or C:** Diagnosing anxiety, skipping documentation, or independently adjusting oxygen is unsafe and outside the purpose of this unit. Verbal reporting and charting both matter.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Build the note in order: time, activity context, values, resident quote, pain score, color/effort observation, safety support, notification.
