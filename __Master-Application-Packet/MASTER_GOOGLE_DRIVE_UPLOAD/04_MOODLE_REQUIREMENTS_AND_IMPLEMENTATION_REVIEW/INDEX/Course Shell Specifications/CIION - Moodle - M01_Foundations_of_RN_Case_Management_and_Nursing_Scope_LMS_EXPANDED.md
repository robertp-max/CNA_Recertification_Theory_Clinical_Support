## Behavioral Learning Objectives

- Explain how RN case management differs from general administrative case handling when patient risk, nursing judgment, and continuity of care are involved.
- Assess direct and indirect patient-care information using the nursing process as the organizing framework for case management.
- Prioritize patient-centered goals, safety risks, and coordination needs within registered nursing scope.
- Document case management findings, communications, referrals, education, follow-up, and unresolved needs in audit-ready nursing language.
- Coordinate care with providers, caregivers, interdisciplinary partners, payers, and community resources while preserving RN accountability.
- Escalate changes in condition, safety concerns, role-boundary issues, and unresolved barriers through appropriate channels.
- Evaluate whether case management actions are patient-centered, within RN scope, supported by evidence, and ready for Moodle completion tracking.

  Read the segment and identify three actions that are nursing case management because they require patient-care judgment. Notice where the RN role ends and where provider, supervisor, interdisciplinary, or administrative support roles begin.
content_body: |
  RN scope is the starting point for safe case management. The RN case manager uses nursing knowledge to interpret patient-care information, identify risks, coordinate services, support education, communicate with the team, and evaluate whether the plan is moving toward patient-centered goals. The role may include phone calls, record review, resource coordination, payer communication, and follow-up tasks, but those tasks become nursing case management only when they are connected to patient need and nursing judgment.

  A referral queue, authorization request, or appointment list can look administrative at first glance. The RN lens changes the question. The RN considers what patient condition, functional limitation, medication issue, caregiver gap, transition risk, or safety concern makes the task clinically important. If a patient newly discharged after heart failure reports increasing shortness of breath while asking about a follow-up appointment, the RN does not treat the call as simple scheduling. The RN recognizes a possible change in condition, assesses what information is available, determines whether urgent escalation is needed, and documents the communication and next steps.

  Scope boundaries protect the patient and the nurse. The RN may assess, teach within nursing scope, coordinate ordered services, identify barriers, advocate, and communicate concerns. The RN does not independently diagnose a new medical condition, prescribe a treatment, promise payer approval, alter provider orders without authorization, or represent a draft course or certificate as approved. When information points beyond RN authority, the RN identifies the boundary and uses the appropriate escalation route.

  Role clarity also prevents case management from becoming business-centered instead of patient-centered. Cost, coverage, access, and operational issues may matter because they affect whether the patient receives safe and timely care. They should not replace the patient's goals and safety needs as the center of the plan. A nurse can discuss coverage barriers factually, seek resources, and communicate medical-necessity information, but the documentation should stay clinical, neutral, and tied to care coordination.

  In practice, RN case management identity is shown by the trail of reasoning. The record should make clear what was known, why the issue mattered to patient care, what action was taken, who was contacted, what response occurred, and what follow-up remains. If another reviewer cannot see the patient-care connection, the note may look like task tracking rather than nursing coordination. M01 asks you to make that connection visible.

moodle_description: |

completion_tracking: View required.
restrict_access: Requires completion of M01_BOOK_SEGMENT_1_RN_SCOPE_FOUNDATION.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  As you read, map each phase of the nursing process to a case management action. Prepare to use the same sequence during the case scenario and documentation exercise.
content_body: |
  The nursing process gives RN case management a disciplined structure. It keeps the nurse from reacting only to the loudest request and instead supports organized clinical reasoning. The sequence is not always perfectly linear, but the RN should be able to show how each action relates to assessment, planning, implementation, coordination, evaluation, or documentation.

  Assessment in case management includes gathering and validating information. The RN may review a referral, discharge summary, medication list, therapy note, durable medical equipment request, patient interview, caregiver concern, or prior communication. Assessment also includes noticing what is missing. If the discharge instructions mention a new anticoagulant but the patient does not understand bleeding precautions, that gap becomes clinically meaningful. If the referral says home safety is adequate but the caregiver reports repeated falls, the RN needs to reconcile conflicting information and prioritize safety.

  Planning translates assessment into patient-centered goals and actions. A goal should be meaningful to the patient and concrete enough to guide follow-up. "Improve health" is too broad. "Patient will state two symptoms that require same-day clinical contact before the next scheduled follow-up" is more useful because it connects teaching, risk recognition, and evaluation. The plan should identify the RN action, the team member or provider to contact, the expected timing, and the follow-up method.

  Implementation and coordination are where many case management activities occur. The RN may arrange communication between the patient and provider, send clinically relevant information to an interdisciplinary partner, coordinate ordered services, confirm that a referral was received, reinforce discharge teaching, or identify a barrier such as transportation, caregiver availability, medication access, or equipment delay. These activities should be documented as nursing care coordination when they arise from assessed patient need.

  Evaluation closes the loop. The RN checks whether the patient understood teaching, whether a referral led to action, whether a barrier remains, whether symptoms changed, and whether escalation is still needed. Evaluation is often the difference between open-ended task completion and accountable case management. A note that says "left voicemail" is incomplete if no follow-up plan exists for a high-risk issue. A stronger note records why the call mattered, what was attempted, what alternative route will be used, and when the RN will reassess.

  Documentation runs through every phase. It should be factual, timely, concise, and complete enough to support continuity, audit review, and professional accountability. Avoid vague statements such as "handled issue" or "patient okay" when a specific risk, teaching point, response, or follow-up is needed. Audit-ready coordination evidence shows the clinical basis for the action, the parties involved, the outcome, and remaining responsibilities.

moodle_description: |

completion_tracking: View required.

gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Compare direct and indirect activities. For each example, decide what patient-care risk or goal makes the activity appropriate for RN case management documentation.
content_body: |
  Direct patient care occurs when the RN interacts with the patient or caregiver in a way that involves assessment, teaching, counseling within nursing scope, care planning, symptom review, or evaluation of understanding. In case management, direct contact may be a phone call, video visit, home-health coordination call, discharge follow-up, or structured patient interview. The key feature is not the setting; it is the use of nursing judgment in relation to patient care.

  Indirect patient care occurs when the RN works on behalf of the patient without the patient present. This can include reviewing records, communicating with a provider, clarifying an order, coordinating with therapy, following up on a referral, documenting a barrier, or sending relevant information to a payer according to policy. Indirect work is not automatically nursing case management. It becomes nursing case management when the RN can explain how the action supports patient safety, continuity, access, education, or goal progress.

  Consider a patient discharged with a new wound-care order and no supplies at home. Calling a supply vendor could be routine purchasing if it is disconnected from clinical need. In an RN case management context, the same call may be indirect patient care because delayed supplies increase infection risk, pain, caregiver burden, and potential readmission. The RN should document the assessed risk, the ordered service or supply need, the communication made, the expected delivery or follow-up, and any escalation if the delay threatens safety.

  Direct and indirect care often work together. A patient may state during a call that they skipped medication because the label was confusing. The RN assesses understanding, reinforces ordered instructions within scope, identifies the risk, contacts the provider or pharmacist if clarification is needed, coordinates follow-up, and documents the outcome. The direct interaction revealed the issue, and the indirect coordination helped resolve it.

  The RN case manager should avoid two extremes. One extreme is overstepping scope by making provider-only decisions or changing orders independently. The other is underusing nursing judgment by treating all coordination as clerical. Safe practice sits between those extremes. The RN recognizes patient-care meaning, acts within scope, uses the team appropriately, and creates a record that supports continuity.

completion_tracking: View required.
restrict_access: Requires completion of M01_BOOK_SEGMENT_3_DIRECT_INDIRECT_CARE.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read with attention to the difference between advocating for a patient and promising an outcome. Prepare to explain two borderline role examples in the active interaction.
content_body: |
  Patient-centered goals help the RN case manager decide what matters most and how to coordinate care without losing sight of the person receiving services. A goal should reflect patient risk, preferences, functional needs, health literacy, caregiver support, cultural considerations, and the setting in which the plan must actually work. A patient may value staying at home, understanding medications, controlling symptoms, avoiding preventable hospital use, or having a caregiver who knows when to seek help. The RN case manager turns those priorities into coordinated actions within scope.

  Good goals are specific enough to guide follow-up. "Patient wants services" does not tell the team what to do. A more useful goal might be: "Patient will receive ordered home health start-of-care visit within the provider-directed time frame, and caregiver will verbalize two symptoms that require clinical contact." This goal supports coordination, teaching, provider communication, and evaluation. It also allows the RN to document progress or unresolved barriers.

  Role boundaries matter because case managers often work at the intersection of clinical care, coverage rules, operations, and family expectations. The RN may explain the nursing concern, gather facts, communicate with the provider, request clarification, reinforce ordered teaching, and advocate for timely services. The RN may not independently authorize benefits, guarantee equipment delivery, diagnose a new disease, order a medication, or tell a patient to ignore provider instructions. When a patient or family asks for something outside RN authority, the nurse can still respond therapeutically by acknowledging the concern, explaining the appropriate pathway, and escalating or referring the request.

  Advocacy is active, factual, and patient-centered. It includes speaking up when a barrier threatens safety, when a patient does not understand instructions, when a caregiver cannot perform expected tasks, when a referral delay creates risk, or when a plan does not match the patient's circumstances. Advocacy does not mean promising that every requested service will be approved. It means making sure relevant patient-care information reaches the right decision-makers and that the patient is not lost between systems.

  Boundary language should be clear in documentation. Instead of writing, "Approved more visits," the RN might document, "Patient reports increased weakness and two recent near falls. RN notified provider and home health agency of safety concern and requested review of therapy needs according to policy. Follow-up planned by next business day." The second note shows assessment data, coordination, scope, and follow-up without claiming authority the RN does not hold.

moodle_description: |
  Learners identify escalation triggers and document coordination evidence that supports safety, continuity, and review readiness.
completion_tracking: View required.
restrict_access: Requires completion of M01_BOOK_SEGMENT_4_GOALS_BOUNDARIES_ADVOCACY.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  While reading, list signs that require immediate, same-day, routine, or supervisory escalation. Then identify the evidence a reviewer would need to understand your decision.
content_body: |
  Escalation is the process of moving a concern to the person or pathway with authority to address it. In RN case management, escalation may involve a provider, supervisor, emergency pathway, interdisciplinary partner, compliance lead, quality process, or payer contact according to policy. Escalation is not a failure of case management. It is often the safest nursing action when the risk exceeds the RN's independent authority or when a barrier remains unresolved.

  Immediate escalation is appropriate when information suggests urgent safety risk or possible change in condition. Examples include severe shortness of breath, chest pain, signs of stroke, uncontrolled bleeding, suicidal statements, sudden confusion, serious medication reaction, unsafe home situation, or inability to obtain essential medication after discharge. The RN follows policy, emergency procedures, and provider notification requirements rather than trying to solve the problem through routine messaging.

  Same-day escalation may be needed for concerning but not immediately life-threatening issues, such as worsening symptoms, missed high-risk follow-up, medication discrepancy, significant caregiver breakdown, new wound concern, repeated falls, or equipment delay that threatens safe care. Routine escalation may apply to nonurgent clarification, referral status checks, service gaps, or care-plan questions that still need closure. Supervisory escalation is appropriate when the RN encounters role ambiguity, conflicting instructions, unresolved safety barriers, documentation concerns, or pressure to act outside scope.

  Audit-ready coordination evidence should allow another qualified reviewer to understand the clinical story. It should identify the source of information, relevant assessment findings, patient-centered goal, nursing action, communication recipient, time frame, response, unresolved issue, and next follow-up. Evidence is stronger when it is specific and neutral. "Called doctor" is weak because it lacks reason, content, and outcome. "Notified cardiology triage line of patient-reported 4-pound weight gain in two days and increased ankle swelling after discharge; patient instructed according to existing discharge plan to seek urgent care for worsening shortness of breath; RN to confirm provider response by 1500" is much clearer.

  Documentation should also separate facts from interpretation. The RN may document what the patient said, what the caregiver reported, what the record showed, what teaching was provided, what referral was made, and what response was received. If the RN is concerned about nonadherence, unsafe conditions, or conflicting information, the note should describe the observable basis for concern rather than blame the patient or family. This supports advocacy and continuity while respecting patient dignity.

  Evaluation completes the evidence loop. After escalation or coordination, the RN should determine whether the risk has been addressed, whether the patient understands the plan, whether a team member accepted responsibility, and whether additional follow-up is scheduled. If the issue remains unresolved, the next action should be documented. A case is not audit-ready simply because a task was attempted; it is review-ready when the record shows accountable reasoning through closure or planned reassessment.

learner_instructions: |
  Work through the fictional case. Choose the safest RN case management action at each decision point. If you choose an unsafe or incomplete option, read the feedback and remediation prompt before trying again.
content_body: |
  Scenario setup: You are the RN case manager reviewing a fictional referral for "Patient A," an older adult recently discharged after a heart failure exacerbation. The discharge packet lists a new diuretic dose, a low-sodium diet instruction, a cardiology follow-up request, and a home health referral. During a follow-up call, the caregiver says Patient A is more short of breath when walking to the bathroom, has gained weight since discharge, and is confused about which medication changed. The caregiver also asks whether you can "approve whatever visits are needed" so the family does not have to keep calling.

  Decision point 1 - Identify the RN case management priority.

  Choice A: Treat the call primarily as a scheduling issue and send a routine message asking the clinic to call when available.

  Choice B: Assess the reported symptoms, clarify available information, reinforce existing urgent-care instructions within scope, and escalate the possible change in condition through the appropriate clinical pathway.

  Choice C: Tell the caregiver to double the diuretic because weight gain after discharge usually means fluid retention.

  Correct-path feedback: Choice B is the safest RN case management action. The report includes possible worsening heart failure symptoms and medication confusion. The RN should gather relevant information, use existing instructions within scope, escalate according to policy, and document the concern and follow-up.

  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A minimizes a potential change in condition and delays needed escalation. Choice C crosses into provider-only medication management and could harm the patient.

  Remediation prompt: Identify one symptom, one medication-safety concern, and one escalation route that should be documented before moving forward.

  Decision point 2 - Set a patient-centered coordination goal.

  Choice A: "The family will receive every service they request."

  Choice B: "The payer will approve additional visits today."

  Choice C: "Patient A and caregiver will understand the current discharge warning signs and medication follow-up plan, and the RN will confirm that the provider or triage pathway receives the symptom report."

  Correct-path feedback: Choice C is patient-centered, measurable, and within RN case management scope. It focuses on understanding, escalation, and closed-loop communication rather than promising an outcome outside the RN's authority.

  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A and Choice B promise results the RN cannot independently guarantee. They also shift the focus away from assessed risk, teaching, and safe follow-up.

  Remediation prompt: Rewrite the goal so it names the patient-care risk, the learner-visible action, and how follow-up will be evaluated.

  Decision point 3 - Document and escalate role boundaries.

  Choice A: "Family wants more services. RN approved extra visits and changed medication instructions."

  Choice B: "Caregiver reports increased shortness of breath with bathroom ambulation, weight gain since discharge, and confusion about new diuretic dose. RN reviewed existing discharge warning signs, advised caregiver to follow urgent instructions for worsening symptoms, notified provider triage pathway according to policy, requested medication clarification, and will confirm response by documented follow-up time. RN explained that visit authorization decisions follow provider and payer processes."

  Choice C: "Caregiver anxious. Told family not to worry and to wait until the next scheduled appointment."

  Correct-path feedback: Choice B documents assessed information, nursing action, escalation, patient teaching within scope, role boundaries, and follow-up. It is audit-ready because it explains why the RN acted and what remains pending.

  Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A claims authority the RN does not have and documents an unsafe medication change. Choice C dismisses risk, lacks escalation, and does not support continuity of care.

  Remediation prompt: Add the missing elements: patient-care finding, scope-safe RN action, escalation recipient, pending response, and follow-up time frame.

  Completion condition: The learner must select the safe path for all three decision points or complete remediation and retry until the safe path is reached. The final page should require the learner to enter a brief statement identifying the highest-priority risk, one patient-centered goal, one RN case management action, one escalation step, and one documentation element.
