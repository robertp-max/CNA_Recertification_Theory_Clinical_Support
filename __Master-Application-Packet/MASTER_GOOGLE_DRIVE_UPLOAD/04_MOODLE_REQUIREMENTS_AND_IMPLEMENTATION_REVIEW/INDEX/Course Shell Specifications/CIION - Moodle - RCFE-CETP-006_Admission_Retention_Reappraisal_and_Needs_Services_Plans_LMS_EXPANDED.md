Admission and retention decisions are among the most consequential responsibilities in RCFE administration because they connect resident dignity, resident safety, family expectations, staff capability, facility program design, and regulatory accountability. A resident may be appropriate for admission only when the facility can understand the person's needs, explain the services it will provide, maintain required records, and identify when outside coordination or a different level of care may be needed. The administrator's work is to manage the process before, during, and after admission so the record tells a clear and truthful story.

In this course, you will practice three linked workflows. First, you will examine admission screening and required records. You will consider how pre-admission information, appraisal information, physician documentation, resident characteristics, admission-agreement linkage, and facility capability come together before a person moves in. Second, you will connect assessed needs to needs and services plan instructions. A useful plan is specific enough for staff to follow, current enough to reflect the resident's condition, and organized enough for review when the resident changes. Third, you will review retention, relocation awareness, escalation, and record audit. The course does not ask you to make final legal or clinical determinations. It asks you to recognize when a situation needs review, documentation, communication, and leadership action.

By the end of this course, you should be able to identify records and decision points used in RCFE admission and retention review; explain how appraisal information, physician reports, and service planning guide resident support; recognize changes that may require reappraisal or outside coordination; draft objective documentation for sample admission and retention scenarios; and describe how Moodle evidence supports completion decisions. You will apply these objectives through three scenario interactions, one participation check, one administrator documentation assignment, and a 30-question post-test.

Use the fictional course scenarios carefully. They are written to help you practice judgment without exposing private information or implying that any real facility action is approved. When you answer, write in objective terms. Instead of saying a person is "difficult," describe the observable issue, the known record, the action taken, who was notified, what remains pending, and the follow-up date. Administrator documentation should be calm, specific, and useful to the next person who must understand the decision.

content_body:
Admission begins before a resident enters the facility. The administrator should treat admission screening as a structured review of needs, services, risk, preferences, and facility capability rather than as a sales conversation or a paperwork event. A prospective resident may bring family expectations, recent medical information, a history of support needs, medication routines, behavior patterns, mobility needs, dietary needs, social preferences, financial questions, and concerns about independence. The facility may have staff, program, environmental, and policy limits. The admission workflow must bring those facts together in a way that is understandable, documented, and ready for review.

A strong admission screening process starts with source control. Learners should distinguish between what is reported by a prospective resident, what is reported by a responsible person, what appears in a physician report, what is learned during appraisal, what is observed during a tour or interview, and what is still unknown. Each source has a different level of reliability and may require follow-up. For example, a family member may say the prospective resident "just needs reminders," while the physician documentation or appraisal interview may reveal transfer needs, wandering risk, dietary restrictions, or supervision needs that require careful planning. The administrator should not collapse these details into a vague admission note. The record should show what is known, what is pending, and how unresolved items will be addressed before admission.

Required records function as the facility's admission memory. They help the administrator and staff understand who the resident is, what services are expected, what information supports the admission, and what follow-up is needed. In this course, think of the admission record as a connected set of evidence rather than separate forms. The pre-admission appraisal should connect to the needs and services plan. The physician report should connect to care needs, restrictions, medication awareness, and any required coordination. The admission agreement should align with the services the facility can actually provide. Resident rights information and house policies should be communicated in a way the resident or responsible person can understand. If there is a gap between assessed need and facility capability, the administrator must not paper over the gap with optimistic wording.

Facility capability is a key admission decision point. Capability includes staffing patterns, staff training, physical environment, program design, policy, supervision, communication systems, and the ability to coordinate with outside providers. An administrator may need to ask: Can staff reliably carry out the service instructions? Are there environmental hazards that make the resident's needs difficult to meet? Is the resident's condition stable enough for the setting? Are there needs that require outside provider coordination before admission? Does the admission record include enough information for night shift, weekend staff, and substitute staff to understand the resident's needs? A responsible admission decision considers the full operating day, not only the moment of move-in.

Objective documentation protects the resident and the facility because it separates facts from impressions. Useful notes describe the date, source of information, observed or reported need, record reviewed, action taken, person notified, pending item, and follow-up timeline. Avoid labels that judge personality or intent. If the prospective resident declined to provide information, write what was requested, what was declined, who was informed, and how the facility will proceed. If a physician report is missing or unclear, document the missing item and the request for clarification. If an admission decision is deferred, explain the reason in neutral terms and identify the next review step.

The administrator also needs to manage communication. Residents and responsible persons should receive clear information about what the facility can provide, what records are needed, and what conditions may require further review. Staff should receive admission information in a format that supports consistent care. Leadership should be informed when the admission raises capability questions. Communication should not promise outcomes, minimize known risks, or imply approval beyond the course status or facility's authority. In Moodle practice activities, your documentation should show how you would keep the admission workflow accurate, respectful, and auditable.

Read the hour page and identify how a resident need becomes a measurable service-plan instruction. Pay attention to triggers that require reappraisal or leadership review.

content_body:
A needs and services plan is useful only when it turns assessment information into clear, current, and workable instructions. The plan should help staff understand what support the resident needs, when the support is provided, who is responsible for the action, what must be observed, and when the administrator or designee should be notified. A plan that says "assist as needed" may sound flexible, but it often fails to guide staff because it does not explain the resident's routine, cueing needs, risk pattern, or escalation threshold. A stronger instruction describes the support in observable terms and connects it to the resident's assessed condition.

The plan should be individualized. Two residents may both need reminders, but one may need a morning orientation cue before breakfast while another may need repeated redirection during evening restlessness. One resident may need help arranging transportation to appointments, while another may need monitoring for a pattern of missed meals. Individualization does not require private detail in a Moodle assignment. It requires careful thinking about the fictional facts provided in the scenario. When you write plan language for this course, use sample descriptors such as "Sample Resident A" and focus on the service instruction, observation point, communication step, and documentation follow-up.

Reappraisal is the administrator's structured response to meaningful change. A change may be gradual or sudden. It may be reported by staff, observed by the administrator, communicated by family, documented by an outside provider, or revealed through incidents. Triggers can include a change in mobility, increased falls, confusion, new exit-seeking behavior, change in medication routine, refusal of meals, weight change concerns, repeated missed appointments, new assistance needs, conflict affecting care, hospitalization, return from acute care, or any pattern suggesting that the current plan no longer matches the resident's needs. The administrator should not wait for the record to become obviously outdated. When the facts change, the plan should be reviewed.

A practical reappraisal workflow starts with observation and source review. What changed? Who reported it? When did it begin? Is the change isolated or repeated? What record supports the concern? What immediate support is already in place? What information is missing? Next, the administrator considers whether the current needs and services plan still gives staff enough guidance. If staff are improvising because the plan does not address the new condition, the plan needs attention. If the change may exceed facility capability, leadership review and external coordination may be required. If the resident's dignity, choice, or rights are affected, communication should be especially careful and respectful.

Service-plan updates should be written so they can be carried out. A useful update might describe when staff should check on the resident, what cueing method is preferred, what environmental support should be used, what refusal pattern should be documented, who should be contacted after repeated concerns, and when the administrator will review the record again. The plan should not overstate what staff can do, substitute for clinical orders, or promise a result. It should connect facility services with assessed need and identify the communication path when conditions change.

Documentation is also part of reappraisal. The record should show the trigger, the review, the decision, the plan update, the people notified, and the next follow-up. If information remains pending, mark it as pending rather than pretending the record is complete. If an outside provider or responsible person must be contacted, document the contact attempt and outcome. If staff need retraining on a changed instruction, record the direction given. If the situation requires escalation, the note should explain why routine action is not enough. Good reappraisal documentation lets a reviewer understand the timeline without guessing.

The administrator's judgment is measured by how well the process protects residents while staying within the facility's role. Some changes can be managed through updated service planning, staff communication, and monitoring. Other changes require higher-level review, outside coordination, or consideration of whether the facility can continue to meet the resident's needs. In this course, you will practice recognizing the difference. You are not being asked to make clinical conclusions. You are being asked to notice when the record, the plan, the resident's condition, and the facility's capability no longer line up.

Read the hour page and consider how an administrator should respond when a resident's needs may exceed current facility capability. Prepare to audit a fictional record in the scenario interaction.

content_body:
Retention review asks whether the facility can continue to meet a resident's needs safely, respectfully, and within the facility's program and regulatory responsibilities. It should not be treated as an automatic reaction to a difficult day or a single complaint. It should also not be delayed when repeated facts show that the current plan may no longer be adequate. The administrator's task is to manage the review process with dignity, fairness, documentation, communication, and escalation when needed.

Retention awareness begins with the current record. The administrator should compare the resident's assessed needs, service-plan instructions, incident history, staff observations, family or responsible-person communication, outside-provider information, and facility capability. The question is not only "What happened?" It is also "What does the pattern show?" A resident who fell once may need a focused review of circumstances and supports. A resident with repeated falls, increased confusion, missed meals, and staff reports of unsupervised wandering may require a broader reappraisal and leadership discussion. A record audit helps the administrator see whether the facility has a current, consistent, and supportable picture of the resident's needs.

Relocation awareness must be handled with care. This course does not provide official notice language, legal advice, or authorization to act outside approved procedures. Instead, it teaches administrators to recognize when a resident's needs may require review beyond routine service-plan updates. When relocation or a different setting may become part of the discussion, the administrator should slow down the process enough to verify facts, involve appropriate leadership, protect resident rights, communicate respectfully, and document the basis for each step. The resident is a person with preferences, relationships, routines, and dignity, not a paperwork problem.

Escalation is a professional control. Escalation may involve the licensee, administrator designee, nurse consultant where applicable, outside provider, responsible person, ombudsman information pathway, licensing consultation, or other approved facility channels depending on the issue. The reason to escalate is not to avoid responsibility. The reason is to bring the right expertise and authority to a decision that exceeds routine staff action. Learners should be able to identify an escalation threshold: unclear physician information, repeated incidents, potential inability to meet needs, disputed information, resident-rights concern, missing record required for decision-making, or conflict between resident preference and safety planning.

A retention review memo should be objective and organized. A useful memo may include the resident identifier used for the fictional exercise, date of review, trigger for review, records examined, current service-plan instructions, new or changed needs, actions already taken, people notified, unresolved questions, facility capability concern, immediate supports, next review date, and escalation path. It should not contain blaming language, assumptions about motives, or unsupported conclusions. It should not state that the facility will guarantee a result. It should show that the administrator recognized a change, reviewed the record, communicated through proper channels, and planned follow-up.

Record audit is the final discipline in this course. Before a resident is retained under an updated plan, before a relocation discussion is escalated, or before a decision is deferred, the administrator should ask whether the record supports the action. Are the admission records complete enough to understand the original fit decision? Does the needs and services plan match current facts? Are reappraisal triggers documented? Are staff instructions current? Are communications dated? Are pending items labeled? Are follow-up dates visible? Can a reviewer understand the decision without calling the administrator to explain it?

In your Moodle assignment and scenario responses, practice writing records that would help the next administrator continue the work. Use concise, factual language. Separate what is known from what is pending. Explain why a plan update, reappraisal, escalation, or record correction is needed. Preserve dignity in the way you write about the resident and family. This approach supports better care and a stronger compliance record because it keeps decisions tied to evidence.

content_body:
Scenario setup: Sample Resident A is seeking admission. The responsible person reports that the resident only needs reminders and would like to move in within two days. The preliminary conversation suggests recent falls, a new walker, occasional missed meals, and a physician report that has been requested but not yet received. Staff are eager to fill the room, but the administrator notices that the pre-admission appraisal is incomplete and the admission agreement cannot yet be connected to a clear service plan.

Decision point: What should the administrator do before accepting the admission date?

Choice A: Defer the final admission decision until required information is reviewed, document what is pending, request the physician report, complete the appraisal review, and compare identified needs with facility capability.

Choice B: Accept the move-in date because the family appears confident and the resident can bring missing documents after admission.

Choice C: Ask direct-care staff to create an informal support plan after move-in without documenting the unresolved pre-admission questions.

Choice D: Tell the responsible person that the facility can manage all needs so the admission does not get delayed.

Correct-path feedback: Choice A is the best path. The administrator is not rejecting the resident; the administrator is controlling the admission process. The record should show what is known, what is missing, who was contacted, what follow-up is required, and how facility capability will be reviewed before admission.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices B, C, and D create avoidable risk. They allow admission to move ahead without a complete record, shift decision-making to staff without clear instructions, or promise capability before facts are verified.

Remediation prompt: Rewrite the administrator note in three sentences: one sentence for known facts, one sentence for pending records, and one sentence for the next review action.

Completion condition: Learner selects the correct path or completes remediation after an unsafe choice, then submits the three-sentence administrator note.
