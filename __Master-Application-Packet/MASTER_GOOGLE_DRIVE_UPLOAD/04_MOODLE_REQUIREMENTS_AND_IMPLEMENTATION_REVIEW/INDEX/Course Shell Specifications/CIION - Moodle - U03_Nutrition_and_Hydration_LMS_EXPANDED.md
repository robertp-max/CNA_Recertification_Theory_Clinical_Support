learner_visible_title: Lesson 1 — Why Food and Fluids Matter in Daily CNA Care
moodle_description: Explains basic nutrient and fluid functions and connects them to practical CNA observations during meals, hydration rounds, and routine care.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after U03 overview page is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and look for resident observations that should be reported rather than explained away.
content_body: |
  Nutrition gives the body materials to function. Carbohydrates provide energy for movement, thinking, breathing, and healing. Protein helps maintain muscle, skin, immune response, and tissue repair. Fats provide concentrated energy and help absorb certain vitamins. Vitamins and minerals support many body processes, including blood formation, bone strength, fluid balance, nerve function, and skin integrity. Fiber supports bowel regularity when it is allowed by the diet and the resident can tolerate it. Water and other permitted fluids help circulation, body temperature, digestion, urinary elimination, skin condition, and medication effects.

  The CNA does not prescribe a diet, decide calorie needs, diagnose malnutrition, or tell a resident which medical diet should replace the current order. The CNA's nutrition role is practical and close to the resident: prepare the environment, verify the tray as assigned, assist with eating or drinking according to the care plan, record intake accurately, and report concerns. A resident who refuses one item because it is disliked may simply need an allowed alternative. A resident who repeatedly eats poorly, coughs with liquids, becomes sleepy during meals, vomits, has diarrhea, complains that food is stuck, or appears weaker than usual may need prompt nursing follow-up.

  Hydration requires special attention in older adults. Thirst may be reduced. Residents may avoid fluids because they fear incontinence, dislike asking for toileting help, have pain with urination, dislike available drinks, or are on a fluid restriction that must be followed carefully. Some medications and illnesses can increase risk for fluid imbalance. Reportable observations may include very dry mouth or cracked lips, new confusion, dizziness, concentrated urine, decreased urination when output is tracked, constipation, poor skin turgor when identified by licensed staff, sudden weight concerns reported by policy, or repeated fluid refusal. CNAs should not diagnose dehydration, but they should recognize patterns and report them.

  Intake documentation must match facility policy. Some settings record meal percentages such as 0, 25, 50, 75, or 100 percent. Others record milliliters of fluid intake, supplements consumed, tube feeding intake observed on assigned records, or output amounts. Guessing is unsafe. If a resident drinks half of a 240 mL carton, the recorded intake should reflect the amount actually consumed according to the facility method. If the resident spills a drink, refuses a supplement, or receives a replacement tray, record and report according to policy. Accurate intake information helps the nurse and dietary team evaluate whether the plan is working.

  Nutrition support is also emotional. Food may connect a resident to childhood, language, celebration, family, religion, comfort, independence, and identity. A CNA can honor that meaning while still following safety rules. Respectful phrases include, "Let me check whether that item matches your current diet," "I can ask the nurse or dietary staff about an allowed option," and "Your preference matters; I need to keep you safe while I check the plan."

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — Therapeutic Diets, Tray Checks, and Ordered Limits

unit_id: U03
activity_id: U03_BOOK_SEGMENT_02
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 2 — Follow the Ordered Diet Before Offering Food or Fluid
moodle_description: Reviews common therapeutic diet categories, texture and liquid modifications, food restrictions, supplements, allergies, and CNA tray-check responsibilities.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 1 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and focus on when to stop, check the order, and report a mismatch.
content_body: |
  A therapeutic diet is ordered to support a resident's health condition, swallowing ability, treatment plan, or safety. Names and details vary by facility, so CNAs must use the current care plan, tray card, diet list, nurse direction, and approved local terms. Common examples include consistent carbohydrate or diabetic diet, low sodium diet, low fat diet, renal-related restrictions, high protein supplements, calorie-controlled plans, clear liquid diet, full liquid diet, mechanical soft diet, minced or ground texture, pureed texture, thickened liquids, fluid restriction, and nothing by mouth when ordered. A diet may also include allergy alerts, food dislikes, adaptive equipment, supervision level, or instructions such as "no straws" or "small sips only."

  Tray checking is a safety step. Before assisting, confirm the right resident, the right tray, the diet label or tray card, allergies or restrictions visible in the approved system, required utensils or adaptive items, and the resident's position and readiness. If the tray contains a food or liquid that does not match the order, do not decide that it is "close enough." Stop and notify the nurse or dietary process according to facility policy. If a visitor leaves food, the same principle applies. A favorite snack may be culturally meaningful and emotionally important, but it still needs to match the current plan.

  Texture-modified diets and thickened liquids require special attention. A resident with dysphagia may need food chopped, minced, mechanical soft, pureed, or otherwise modified so it is easier and safer to chew and swallow. Thickened liquids may be described by facility-approved terms such as mildly, moderately, or extremely thick, or by older terms used locally. A CNA should not thin a beverage because the resident dislikes the texture, add ice that melts into a thickened drink unless policy allows, mix textures without direction, or offer crunchy items to a resident whose diet excludes them. The nurse or dietary staff must be involved when there is a question.

  Fluid restriction is another area where kindness must stay aligned with the order. A resident may be thirsty, may ask for extra water, or may have family members who encourage drinking. The CNA can offer mouth care, allowed fluids within the assigned amount, ice chips only if permitted, and supportive explanation, but should not exceed the ordered limit. Record intake carefully so the care team can track the total. Report thirst, dry mouth, repeated requests, dizziness, edema, shortness of breath, or other concerns.

  Dietary preferences and religious practices are not optional background information. Some residents avoid pork, beef, shellfish, alcohol, caffeine, or foods prepared in certain ways. Some fast during specific times, avoid mixing certain foods, need vegetarian or vegan options, or prefer familiar seasonings and meal patterns. The CNA should not make assumptions based on appearance, last name, language, or religion. Ask respectfully, use interpreter resources when needed, and report preferences through the approved dietary or nursing channel so safe options can be considered.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 3 — Feeding Assistance and Aspiration-Risk Observations

activity_type: Book Page Lesson Segment 3
lane: CNA/CDPH online CE lane
unit_id: U03
activity_id: U03_BOOK_SEGMENT_03
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 3 — Assist with Meals Without Rushing, Forcing, or Ignoring Safety Cues
moodle_description: Teaches safe feeding-assistance sequence, dignity during meals, aspiration-risk cues, and scope-safe reporting when coughing or wet voice appears.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 2 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and identify which signs mean feeding should pause and the nurse should be notified.
content_body: |
  Safe feeding assistance begins before the first bite. Perform hand hygiene. Check identity and the tray according to policy. Make sure dentures, glasses, hearing aids, adaptive utensils, clothing protectors, and call light access are addressed according to the care plan. Position the resident upright as directed, usually with hips and head aligned and the chin not tipped far backward. If the resident is in bed, raise the head of the bed as allowed and support posture. Good positioning helps comfort, breathing, chewing, swallowing, and dignity.

  The resident should be awake enough to participate. If the resident is very sleepy, short of breath, agitated, nauseated, in pain, or saying they cannot swallow, the CNA should not simply continue. Notify the nurse and follow facility direction. Feeding a resident who is not ready can increase risk. The CNA should also create a calm meal environment when possible: reduce distractions, explain what food is offered, ask about preference within the tray, alternate solids and fluids if directed, offer small bites and sips, and allow enough time to chew and swallow. Rushing can lead to pocketing food in the cheek, coughing, fatigue, refusal, or embarrassment.

  Never force food or fluid. A resident has the right to refuse, and refusal may also signal a problem. Ask respectfully, offer allowed alternatives if available, and report repeated refusal or sudden change. Do not scold, trick, threaten, or hide medication in food unless specifically directed through approved procedures. Do not scrape a spoon against teeth, push large bites, or feed while standing over the resident in a rushed way. Sit at eye level if possible and keep conversation respectful. The goal is nourishment with dignity, not just an empty plate.

  Aspiration means material such as food, fluid, saliva, or stomach contents enters the airway. The CNA does not diagnose aspiration, but must recognize warning signs. Pause feeding and report promptly if the resident coughs repeatedly during or after eating, has a wet or gurgly voice, drools more than usual, pockets food, clears the throat frequently, has watery eyes during swallowing, becomes short of breath, shows color change, reports food is stuck, or has a sudden change in alertness during a meal. If the resident appears to be choking or cannot breathe, activate help according to emergency policy. If signs are less dramatic but concerning, keep the resident safe, stop offering more food or fluid until instructed, and notify the nurse.

  Documentation should be factual. Examples include: "Resident coughed twice after nectar-thick liquid at lunch; wet voice noted; feeding paused; nurse notified," or "Resident ate 25 percent of mechanical soft meal and declined further bites, stating, 'I am too tired'; nurse notified." Avoid conclusions such as "Resident aspirated" unless that wording is directed by licensed staff or policy. CNA documentation should show what happened, what the resident stated, what you did within scope, and who was notified.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Intake, Culture, Dehydration, and Alternative Nutrition Boundaries

activity_id: U03_BOOK_SEGMENT_04
estimated_time_minutes: 15
learner_visible_title: Lesson 4 — Report Patterns, Respect Food Practices, and Stay Within Scope
moodle_description: Connects intake/output records, dehydration observations, cultural/religious food influences, and CNA boundaries for tube feeding or IV nutrition.
completion_tracking: Require learner to view this book page; include expected engagement time in course analytics if available.
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 3 is complete.
gradebook_setting: None.
learner_instructions: |
  Read the lesson segment and notice the difference between helpful CNA support and decisions that belong to licensed or dietary staff.
content_body: |
  Meal and hydration observations become more useful when they are reported as patterns. One skipped dessert may not be urgent. Three meals in a row with only a few bites, new coughing with liquids, repeated refusal of thickened drinks, vomiting after meals, new diarrhea, or fluid intake far below the plan may need timely reporting. If output is assigned, report a sudden decrease in urine, very dark urine, repeated incontinence changes from baseline, or complaints of burning or pain with urination. If daily weights are part of local policy, follow the assigned process and report changes according to nurse direction.

  Dehydration observations often appear during routine care. A resident may be more tired, dizzy when standing, confused, constipated, dry-mouthed, or less interested in meals. The CNA should keep permitted fluids within reach if allowed, offer fluids on the schedule, assist with opening containers, provide oral care, help with toileting so the resident is not afraid to drink, and record intake accurately. These supportive actions do not replace reporting. If a resident is on a fluid restriction, the CNA must balance encouragement with the ordered limit and ask the nurse when requests exceed the plan.

  Culture, religion, and personal history influence food choices. A resident may avoid certain meats, need meals after a fasting period, prefer chopsticks or finger foods, avoid foods prepared with alcohol, choose vegetarian meals, have prayers before eating, or find unfamiliar textures distressing. A resident who refuses a tray may be refusing the unfamiliar food, not refusing nourishment. Respectful support includes asking what the resident prefers, offering allowed choices, using interpreter services when needed, protecting privacy around religious practices, and reporting patterns to the nurse or dietary team. The CNA should not argue about beliefs or promise a food that conflicts with the current order.

  Alternative nutrition includes tube feeding and parenteral or IV nutrition managed by licensed staff under facility policy. Residents with feeding tubes may still need oral care, positioning, comfort, observation, and dignity. CNAs may be assigned to report coughing, nausea, vomiting, abdominal discomfort statements, diarrhea, leakage around a tube site, redness, swelling, drainage, a tube pulled out or appearing displaced, pump alarms according to policy, or the resident trying to pull at tubing. CNAs should not start or stop a tube feeding, change a rate, flush a tube, reconnect tubing, silence alarms in a way that hides a problem, adjust IV nutrition, or decide whether oral intake is safe unless a specific task is permitted and directed under approved policy. When in doubt, stop and ask the nurse.

  The central U03 judgment is simple to say and important to practice: food support must be safe, ordered, respectful, and observed. The CNA protects the resident by noticing details early, not by independently changing the plan. Follow the diet, assist patiently, pause when safety cues appear, report objective facts, and document within scope.

learner_instructions: |
  Work through the fictional scenario. Choose the safest CNA response at each decision point. If you choose an unsafe option, read the feedback and remediation prompt before continuing.
content_body: |
  **Scenario setup:** Resident C is on nectar-thick liquids and a mechanical soft diet. The care plan says the resident should sit upright for meals, take small bites and sips, and have supervision during eating. A visitor, trying to be kind, leaves a cup of regular water and a bag of chips at the bedside. At lunch, the tray includes the correct meal texture and thickened beverage. After a sip, Resident C coughs twice and the voice sounds wet.

  **Decision Point 1 — Before the meal begins, what should the CNA do first?**

  A. Confirm Resident C's identity and tray, check the diet label, position the resident upright, remove or hold the regular water and chips until the nurse can address them, and explain respectfully that the current diet must be followed.  
  B. Leave the water and chips within reach because visitors are allowed to bring comfort foods.  
  C. Pour regular water into the thickened beverage to make it more acceptable to the resident.  
  D. Skip the tray check because the kitchen is usually correct.

  **Correct feedback for A:** This choice combines tray verification, positioning, unsafe item recognition, respectful communication, and ordered-diet safety.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for B, C, or D:** Visitor foods and thin liquids may conflict with the diet order. Thinning a beverage or skipping the tray check can increase swallowing risk and is outside the safe CNA role.  
  **Remediation prompt:** List the first four safety checks you should complete before offering a bite or sip.

  **Decision Point 2 — Resident C coughs twice and has a wet-sounding voice after a sip. What is the safest CNA response?**

  A. Encourage several more sips to see whether the resident gets used to the liquid.  
  B. Tell the resident to stop being difficult and finish the meal quickly.  
  C. Pause feeding, keep the resident upright and safe, observe for breathing distress, notify the nurse promptly, and document the cough/wet voice and notification according to policy.  
  D. Diagnose aspiration in the chart and call the family to explain what happened.

  **Correct feedback for C:** Coughing with a wet voice during a meal is a reportable swallowing concern. The CNA pauses, protects safety, reports facts, and documents within scope.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A, B, or D:** Continuing to feed can increase risk. Shaming violates dignity. Diagnosing aspiration or calling family independently is outside CNA scope and may violate privacy.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Rewrite the response using the words pause, upright, observe, report, and document.

  **Decision Point 3 — The visitor says, "But regular water is harmless, and the chips are the only snack Resident C likes." Which response is best?**

  A. "You're right. I will leave them here and not tell anyone."  
  B. "If Resident C promises to chew carefully, the diet order does not matter."  
  C. "You should not bring anything, and this is your fault."  
  D. "I can see you are trying to help. Resident C has a current diet and liquid plan for safety, so I need to check with the nurse or dietary team about safe options. I will keep Resident C's preferences in the report."

  **Correct feedback for D:** This response protects dignity, recognizes the visitor's intent, follows the ordered diet, and routes preferences through the care team.  
  **Unsafe-choice feedback for A, B, or C:** Ignoring the diet order is unsafe. Blaming the visitor is disrespectful and does not solve the safety concern. The CNA should communicate respectfully and report through approved channels.  
  **Remediation prompt:** Write one sentence that both respects the visitor and makes clear that the ordered diet must be followed.
