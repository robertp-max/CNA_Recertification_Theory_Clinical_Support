## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of this 3 contact-hour module, the learner will be able to:

- Explain how structured intake converts referral information, patient interview findings, caregiver input, and available records into a usable RN case management baseline.
- Assess referral validity, medication risk, functional risk, psychosocial risk, social determinants, health literacy, and missing information during intake.
- Prioritize intake findings by patient safety urgency, change in condition, medication risk, functional vulnerability, access barriers, and patient-stated goals.
- Document objective intake findings, patient preferences, risk flags, planned follow-up, and unresolved gaps in audit-ready care plan language.
- Coordinate next steps with the patient, caregiver, provider, pharmacy, interdisciplinary team, payer contact, or community resource when the assessment supports that action.
- Escalate urgent or unresolved findings through appropriate clinical and organizational pathways without making provider-only decisions.
- Evaluate whether the initial care plan remains patient-centered, measurable, within RN scope, and supported by completion evidence.

content_body:
Structured intake is the point where RN case management begins to turn scattered information into a care coordination plan. A referral may arrive with diagnoses, orders, insurance details, discharge notes, medication changes, social concerns, and urgent requests. Some information will be current, some will be incomplete, and some may conflict with what the patient or caregiver reports. The RN case manager's value is not simply collecting these items. The value is recognizing which findings affect safety, access, adherence, continuity, patient preference, and the need for timely follow-up.

Patient-centered care planning begins during the first review. The patient is not only a diagnosis or referral category. The patient has goals, routines, supports, fears, literacy needs, cultural or language preferences, financial limits, transportation barriers, and varying ability to follow instructions. Care planning is stronger when the RN integrates these realities early instead of treating them as secondary details. A wound-care order, for example, may be clinically appropriate, but the plan may fail if the patient cannot obtain supplies, understand return precautions, afford food consistent with diabetes teaching, or identify who can assist when the caregiver works nights.

This module teaches a disciplined intake sequence. First, validate the referral source and identify what is known. Second, complete a patient and caregiver interview using respectful, plain-language questions. Third, screen medication, functional, psychosocial, environmental, and social risks. Fourth, prioritize findings by urgency and consequence. Fifth, document a measurable care plan that links each priority to an action, owner, time frame, escalation pathway, and follow-up evidence. The goal is a record that supports safe care now and audit review later.

You will complete five lesson segments, a branching case scenario, an intake priority builder, a documentation/application assignment, a 15-question knowledge check, and a learner reflection. Module completion requires evidence for all required activities.

content_body:
Structured intake starts with source awareness. Referral information may come from a hospital discharge planner, clinic, provider office, payer, home health agency, family member, community program, or prior record. Each source may have useful information, but no single source should be treated as complete by default. The RN case manager reviews the referral for the patient's current reason for service, recent change in condition, diagnoses relevant to coordination, new orders, medication changes, discharge instructions, pending appointments, equipment needs, payer constraints, caregiver availability, and barriers already identified.

Validation means comparing the referral against other available information and the patient's current report. If the referral says the patient can ambulate independently but the caregiver reports two recent falls, the care plan should not rely on the referral alone. If the discharge medication list includes a new anticoagulant but the patient does not know why it was prescribed, medication education and provider or pharmacy follow-up become priorities. If a payer authorization lists a service but the patient lacks transportation to the service location, access planning is part of the case management assessment.

Effective intake documentation separates confirmed facts from pending questions. The record should show where the information came from, what was confirmed with the patient or caregiver, what remains uncertain, and who is responsible for follow-up. Phrases such as "patient unsafe" or "family noncompliant" are weak because they mix interpretation with judgment and do not explain what was observed or reported. A stronger note would state that the caregiver reported the patient missed two wound-care visits due to transportation problems, the patient stated supplies were not delivered, and the RN requested same-day follow-up with the supply vendor and notifying provider according to policy.

Referral validation also protects scope. The RN case manager can assess, educate, coordinate, document, and escalate concerns, but does not independently diagnose a new medical condition, change a medication order, or guarantee payer approval. When intake findings point to a clinical decision outside RN authority, the plan should identify the correct provider or team contact and the urgency of the communication.

content_body:
The patient and caregiver interview gives meaning to the referral. A useful interview is respectful, organized, and flexible. It starts by confirming identity in the actual care setting, but learner submissions for this course must never include real identifiers. The RN then clarifies the patient's understanding of why case management is involved, what changed recently, what the patient wants to accomplish, and what worries the patient or caregiver most. Open-ended questions help reveal priorities that may not appear in a referral, such as fear of falling, inability to buy food, confusion about insulin dosing, or uncertainty about who to call after hours.

Medication risk screening is essential during intake because medication discrepancies often create immediate safety concerns. The RN should compare the medication list from the referral or discharge record with what the patient reports taking, what containers are present, what the pharmacy can confirm when appropriate, and what the caregiver understands. Red flags include duplicate therapies, high-alert medications, missing medications after discharge, unclear dose changes, inability to afford prescriptions, side effects, and low confidence in self-administration. The RN case manager documents the discrepancy, patient response, education provided within scope, and escalation or coordination step.

Functional risk screening looks at how the patient manages daily life. Intake should consider mobility, transfers, bathing, dressing, toileting, meal preparation, cognition, vision, hearing, continence, skin integrity, fall history, equipment use, home access, and caregiver capacity. Functional findings often change the priority order of the care plan. A stable lab result may be less urgent than a patient who cannot safely reach the bathroom, has no food in the home, and does not understand wound precautions. Functional screening should produce actions such as therapy referral follow-up, equipment coordination, caregiver teaching, safety education, or provider notification when findings suggest a change in condition.

Caregiver input is valuable but should be documented as a source, not as a replacement for the patient's voice when the patient can participate. The RN should note what the caregiver reports, where it agrees or conflicts with the patient report, and what follow-up is needed. When caregiver strain, work schedule, language barriers, transportation limits, or health literacy issues affect the plan, those findings belong in the intake summary because they influence whether the patient-centered plan is realistic.

content_body:
Social determinants of health are not side notes when they affect care. During intake, the RN case manager considers transportation, food access, housing stability, utilities, medication affordability, insurance barriers, caregiving support, digital access, community safety, language access, and the patient's ability to schedule or attend follow-up. These factors can determine whether a clinically sound plan succeeds. A patient may understand the importance of wound care but be unable to obtain supplies. Another patient may want to keep a cardiology appointment but lack transportation. A third may have medication instructions written in language the patient cannot read.

Health literacy assessment is part of safe communication. The RN should not assume understanding because a patient nods, repeats a word, or has completed forms in the past. Plain-language teaching, teach-back, interpreter services when needed, and written instructions matched to the patient's needs help convert intake findings into usable action. A patient-centered plan should describe how teaching was provided and how understanding was assessed. Documentation might state that the RN reviewed signs of wound infection using plain language, the patient correctly described when to call the provider, and the caregiver requested large-print instructions for supply ordering.

Respectful inquiry matters. Asking about transportation, food, housing, cost, or family support can feel sensitive. The RN can normalize the questions by explaining that these issues affect whether the care plan will work. The tone should invite partnership, not blame. Documentation should avoid labels such as "nonadherent" unless the record explains the factual barrier. If the patient missed appointments because the bus route changed and the caregiver was unavailable, the care plan should address transportation and follow-up rather than treating the missed visits as a character problem.

Access barriers should lead to concrete next steps. Depending on policy and available resources, the RN may coordinate with social work, community programs, pharmacy, durable medical equipment vendors, transportation resources, provider offices, or payer contacts. The plan should identify the barrier, the action, the owner, the expected time frame, and how completion will be verified. This makes the plan measurable and audit-ready.

content_body:
Prioritization is the bridge between assessment and plan. Not every finding has the same urgency. The RN case manager considers immediate safety, change in condition, medication risk, functional vulnerability, inability to obtain ordered care, caregiver breakdown, health literacy barriers, and patient-stated concerns. High priority findings are those that could lead to harm, missed treatment, avoidable hospitalization, or failed transition if not addressed promptly. Lower priority findings still matter, but they can be scheduled after immediate risks are controlled or escalated.

A practical prioritization sequence starts with safety. Is there a current symptom, medication discrepancy, wound concern, fall risk, confusion, or inability to perform essential self-care? Next, consider time sensitivity. Is a follow-up appointment due soon, a medication running out, a supply missing, or a provider order unclear? Then consider feasibility. Does the patient have the knowledge, support, transportation, equipment, and resources to carry out the plan? Finally, align the plan with what matters to the patient. A care plan that ignores patient goals is less likely to be followed and less defensible as patient-centered case management.

Goals should be measurable and linked to findings. "Improve diabetes management" is too broad for an intake care plan. A stronger goal might state that within seven days the patient will describe the current insulin schedule using teach-back and identify who to contact for blood glucose readings outside the provider-defined range. Another goal might state that before the first wound-care visit the patient will have confirmed supplies in the home and transportation arranged, with vendor and appointment status documented. These goals do not promise outcomes outside RN control; they describe measurable coordination and patient education targets.

Patient-centered goals should preserve the patient's voice. If the patient says the main goal is to stay at home safely, the plan can connect fall prevention, medication clarity, caregiver scheduling, and follow-up appointments to that goal. If the caregiver is the only person available for dressing changes but works nights, the plan should address timing, teaching, backup support, or escalation when the care schedule is unrealistic. Measurable goals make the plan easier to evaluate because they define what completion looks like and what evidence should be documented.

content_body:
Audit-ready documentation allows another qualified reviewer to understand what information was available, what the RN assessed, why certain findings were prioritized, what actions were taken, and what remains pending. Intake documentation should include referral source, patient or caregiver interview findings, medication concerns, functional risks, social barriers, health literacy needs, patient-stated goals, coordination actions, escalation steps, and follow-up time frames. It should also make uncertainty visible. Missing information is not a documentation failure when it is clearly tracked with an owner, due date, and next step.

Objective language strengthens the record. Instead of writing that a patient is "unreliable," document that the patient gave two different reports about insulin dose, could not locate the discharge instruction sheet, and agreed to pharmacy follow-up. Instead of writing that the home is "unsafe," document that the patient reported two falls in the last week, uses furniture for support, and does not have the ordered walker. The RN may then document education, referral follow-up, provider notification, or escalation according to policy.

Care plan documentation should link each priority to a planned action. A complete entry might identify a medication discrepancy, state that the RN reviewed the current list with the patient, contacted the pharmacy for fill history according to policy, notified the provider office of the discrepancy, taught the patient not to change dosing without provider direction, and scheduled follow-up to confirm clarification. This type of note shows assessment, action, communication, education, and pending outcome. It also avoids implying that the RN independently changed a medication order.

Documentation should support continuity. If another case manager opens the record tomorrow, the next step should be clear. The record should identify who needs to be contacted, what response is pending, what the patient understands, what barriers remain, and when follow-up should occur. Patient-centered care planning is not complete because a template is filled out. It is complete when the plan is specific enough to guide safe coordination and flexible enough to be updated as new information emerges.

content_body:
Scenario setup: You are the RN case manager assigned to a fictional adult patient newly referred after hospitalization for a diabetes-related wound infection. The referral includes a wound-care order, a discharge medication list with insulin changes, a follow-up appointment in five days, and a note that transportation may be difficult. During the intake call, the patient says the wound supplies have not arrived, the insulin instructions are confusing, and the caregiver works nights. The caregiver reports the patient has been weaker than usual and almost fell while walking to the bathroom. The patient says the most important goal is to remain at home safely and avoid another hospitalization.

Decision point 1: What is the best first RN case management priority?

Choice A: Begin by asking the patient which brand of wound supplies is preferred and document that preference as the primary plan.

Choice B: Compare the referral, patient report, caregiver report, medication list, wound supply status, and fall concern to identify immediate safety and care-continuity risks.

Choice C: Tell the patient to wait until the scheduled follow-up appointment because the provider will address all issues then.

Correct-path feedback for Decision 1: Choice B is the safest path because intake begins by validating sources and recognizing immediate risks. Medication confusion, missing wound supplies, near fall, and caregiver availability all affect whether the care plan can be carried out. The RN case manager should organize these facts before selecting coordination actions.

Unsafe-choice feedback for Decision 1: Choice A narrows the plan to preference before safety is assessed. Choice C delays action despite medication and functional concerns. Either path could miss a preventable risk.

Remediation prompt for Decision 1: Reopen the intake findings and list three facts that could cause harm or failed care continuity if not addressed before the appointment.

Decision point 2: Which medication-related action best fits RN case management scope?

Choice A: Tell the patient to return to the pre-hospital insulin schedule until the provider visit.

Choice B: Remove the medication concern from the plan because medication reconciliation belongs only to the provider.

Choice C: Document the discrepancy, assess the patient's understanding, provide education within scope, and coordinate prompt clarification with the provider or pharmacy according to policy.

Correct-path feedback for Decision 2: Choice C keeps the RN within scope while responding to medication risk. The RN does not change the order, but the RN can identify the discrepancy, teach safe follow-up steps, coordinate clarification, and document pending outcomes.

Unsafe-choice feedback for Decision 2: Choice A makes a provider-only medication decision. Choice B ignores a safety concern that belongs in intake prioritization and coordination.

Remediation prompt for Decision 2: Write one objective documentation sentence that identifies the discrepancy without changing the medication order.

Decision point 3: What should the initial patient-centered care plan include?

Choice A: A general note that the patient has multiple needs and will be followed as time allows.

Choice B: A plan focused only on payer authorization because authorization is the most important intake task.

Choice C: A statement that the caregiver is responsible for resolving transportation and supply barriers.

Choice D: Measurable goals and actions for medication clarification, wound supply delivery, fall-risk follow-up, transportation planning, patient teach-back, and provider/team communication.

Correct-path feedback for Decision 3: Choice D creates a measurable, patient-centered plan tied to assessment findings. It includes safety, access, teaching, coordination, and follow-up evidence without blaming the patient or caregiver.

Unsafe-choice feedback for Decision 3: Choice A is too vague for safe follow-up. Choice B ignores clinical and functional risks. Choice C shifts responsibility without assessing support or coordinating resources.

Remediation prompt for Decision 3: Convert one vague plan statement into a measurable goal with an owner, time frame, and evidence of completion.
