learner_instructions: Read the overview and compare the objectives with your own daily CNA practice. Notice where restorative care depends on patience, observation, and follow-through.  
content_body:  
Rehabilitative nursing is built on a simple but powerful idea: residents should be supported to do as much for themselves as they safely can. For a CNA, that idea shows up during ordinary care. A resident may need extra time to button a shirt, verbal cues to begin grooming, a wheelchair positioned correctly before transfer, setup for a meal, or encouragement to complete an assigned walking program. These moments can either support function or unintentionally take it away. If staff always rush in and do every task, the resident may lose confidence, strength, balance, endurance, and choice.

By the end of this unit, you should be able to explain restorative care as support for the resident's highest practical level of function, describe how the CNA contributes to the rehabilitation or restorative team, promote participation in activities of daily living, identify concerns with adaptive devices, recognize complications of inactivity, support assigned range-of-motion and mobility activities within scope, protect self-esteem, and document restorative observations as assigned. You will also practice reporting when a resident has pain, fatigue, new weakness, refusal, unsafe device fit, skin redness, or a change in ability.

The CNA does not create a therapy plan, change a mobility order, decide that a device is no longer needed, or force a resident to perform. The CNA follows the current care plan and the instructions of licensed nurses and therapy staff. Safe restorative care respects the resident's rights: choice, privacy, dignity, reasonable pace, and freedom from humiliation. Encouragement should sound like partnership, not pressure. Documentation should state what happened, what assistance was provided, how the resident responded, and who was notified when concerns appeared.

restrict_access: Available after the U07 overview page is complete.

learner_instructions: Read this segment and identify one routine care task where you might be tempted to do too much for the resident.  
content_body:  
Restorative care begins with believing that function matters. Function is the ability to move, communicate, eat, dress, toilet, participate, make choices, and use remaining strengths. Some residents are recovering after illness or injury; others are maintaining ability during a long-term condition. Improvement may be dramatic, such as walking farther after a hospitalization, or very small, such as grasping a washcloth with less cueing. Maintenance also matters. Preventing decline can protect comfort, independence, safety, and emotional well-being.

The restorative team may include the resident, family or representative, licensed nurses, CNAs, physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, activities, social services, dietary services, medical providers, and others identified by the care plan. Each discipline has a different role. Therapy staff may evaluate, treat, and recommend specific exercises or devices. Nursing staff coordinate the plan and monitor condition. CNAs provide repeated daily support, which makes their observations especially valuable. You may notice that a resident transfers more slowly today, avoids one arm, refuses a splint, becomes short of breath after fewer steps, or smiles when allowed to finish grooming independently.

The CNA responsibility is to follow the care plan, use training already validated by the facility, protect safety, and report changes. A CNA may set up supplies, give reminders, encourage rest breaks, assist with positioning, support assigned ambulation or range-of-motion activities, and record participation as directed. A CNA should not invent exercises, increase distance beyond the plan, remove a device permanently, promise a therapy outcome, or shame a resident for needing help. If the care plan says the resident should wash the face and upper body with setup, doing the entire bath because it is faster may work against the goal. The restorative question is: what support allows this resident to participate safely today?

Restorative care also requires listening. A resident who says, "I am tired," "This hurts," or "I am afraid I will fall" is giving information the team needs. The CNA can encourage, but encouragement must be balanced with respect and reporting. Pushing through pain or fear without notifying the nurse can cause harm and decrease trust.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 2 — ADLs, Adaptive Devices, and Dignified Setup

moodle_description: Book page covering ADL support, adaptive equipment, positioning, device safety observations, and dignity-preserving cueing.

learner_instructions: As you read, focus on the difference between helping a resident start a task and taking over the task.  
content_body:  
Activities of daily living, often called ADLs, include bathing, grooming, dressing, eating, toileting, transferring, walking or wheelchair mobility, and related self-care. In restorative nursing, ADL support is not only about finishing the task. It is also about preserving ability. A resident may be able to wash the face, brush the front teeth, comb part of the hair, pull up a sleeve, hold a cup with an adaptive handle, or stand for a short part of care. The CNA supports success by gathering supplies, placing items within reach, explaining the next step, offering choices, allowing enough time, and giving only the amount of help needed.

Adaptive and comfort devices can make participation possible. Examples include walkers, canes, wheelchairs, transfer belts used according to policy, splints, braces, prostheses, raised toilet seats, grab bars, long-handled sponges, reachers, built-up utensils, plate guards, nonskid footwear, heel protectors, cushions, footboards, bed cradles, and special mattresses. The CNA does not prescribe these devices, but the CNA often sees whether they are being used correctly and tolerated. Report broken brakes, missing wheelchair footrests, a cushion that is not in place, a splint that leaves redness, a prosthesis that seems loose, a walker with worn tips, a device that causes pain, or a resident who refuses an ordered device.

Dignified setup matters. Place the call light, water if allowed, glasses, hearing aids, clothing, grooming supplies, and adaptive devices where the resident can reach them. Protect privacy by covering the resident and closing doors or curtains as appropriate. Offer realistic choices: "Would you like to wash your face before or after brushing your teeth?" Avoid baby talk, teasing, or comments about slowness. If the resident becomes frustrated, acknowledge the effort: "You did the first part; I can help with the area you cannot reach." This preserves self-esteem while still completing care.

A common mistake is confusing independence with abandonment. Restorative care does not mean leaving a resident alone with unsafe equipment or refusing help. It means matching support to the resident's ability and the care plan. If a resident needs supervision, cueing, setup, or hands-on assistance, provide it as directed and document the level of help accurately.

moodle_description: Book page on complications of inactivity, assigned range-of-motion support, mobility assistance, safety cues, and reportable concerns.

learner_instructions: Read for reportable warning signs. Pay special attention to pain, fatigue, skin changes, shortness of breath, and changes from usual function.  
content_body:  
Inactivity affects the whole person. When a resident spends too much time in bed or in one position, muscles weaken, joints stiffen, and endurance decreases. Skin may break down from pressure or shearing. Lungs may not expand as fully, increasing risk for congestion. Circulation may slow, contributing to edema or clot risk. Digestion and elimination may slow, contributing to constipation. The resident may also lose confidence, become fearful of falling, feel bored or isolated, and stop trying tasks that once felt possible.

Range of motion, or ROM, helps maintain joint movement and comfort when it is included in the plan. Active ROM means the resident moves the joint independently. Passive ROM means the caregiver moves the joint for the resident. Active-assistive ROM means both the resident and caregiver participate. CNAs perform only assigned ROM activities for which they have been trained and validated by the facility. Movements should be gentle, controlled, and within the instructed range. Report pain, resistance, swelling, redness, warmth, a new limit in motion, unusual fatigue, dizziness, or the resident asking to stop. Never force a joint, bounce a limb, or continue a movement that causes sharp pain.

Mobility support may include turning, repositioning, dangling, transferring, walking, wheelchair mobility, or participation in a restorative walking program. Preparation reduces risk: check footwear, remove clutter, position equipment, lock wheelchair brakes when appropriate, use the assigned assistive device, and ask for help when the care plan requires two staff or when the resident seems different from baseline. Support should match the plan. If the resident usually walks 50 feet with a walker and one assist but today becomes pale and short of breath after standing, the safe response is not to push the distance. The CNA should protect the resident from falling, notify the nurse, and document factual observations.

Repositioning is also restorative. Encouraging a resident to shift weight, sit upright for meals, use pillows or supports as directed, and participate in turning can reduce complications and help the resident remain aware of body position. Small actions repeated consistently can protect function.

### Moodle Activity: Book Page Lesson Segment 4 — Self-Esteem, Family Involvement, Reporting, and Documentation

moodle_description: Book page connecting self-esteem, family involvement, care-plan meetings, objective reporting, and CNA documentation.  
completion_tracking: Mark complete when viewed; include expected engagement time or book-page completion requirement.  
restrict_access: Available after Lesson Segment 3 is complete.  
gradebook_setting: None; completion only.  
learner_instructions: Read this segment with documentation in mind. Think about how a short factual note can help the team adjust support.  
content_body:  
Rehabilitative nursing is physical and emotional. A resident who needs help after illness, injury, or decline may feel embarrassed, impatient, angry, discouraged, or dependent. Self-esteem can improve when staff notice effort and provide privacy. Instead of saying, "You are too slow," say, "Take your time; I am here for safety." Instead of announcing a resident's limitations in a hallway, speak quietly and protect confidentiality. Acknowledge progress without exaggerating: "Today you buttoned two buttons before I helped with the rest." This kind of communication respects the adult resident and reinforces participation.

Family or representatives can support restorative goals, but they may also need explanation from the care team. A family member might say, "Just do it for her," because watching effort feels uncomfortable. The CNA can respond respectfully within scope: "The care plan encourages her to do the parts she can do safely, and I will help with the rest." If the family has questions about therapy goals, prognosis, changes in orders, or device use, refer those questions to the nurse or therapist. Do not argue, disclose private information in public, or promise that the resident will recover a certain ability.

CNAs contribute to care-plan meetings and team decisions through daily observations. Even if you do not attend a formal meeting, your documentation and reports may inform the plan. Important restorative observations include what the resident did independently, what setup or cueing was needed, what device was used, distance walked if assigned for you to record, tolerance, pain statement, fatigue, refusal, unsafe condition, skin concern, and change from usual function. Documentation should be factual: "Resident washed face and upper chest after setup; required verbal cues to use long-handled sponge for back; stated, 'I am tired now'; nurse notified of fatigue." Avoid labels such as lazy, stubborn, manipulative, or noncompliant. If the resident refuses, document the refusal and what was reported according to policy, not a judgment about character.

Good reporting closes the loop. A restorative concern is not only charted later; urgent or meaningful changes are reported promptly. Device problems, pain, dizziness, new weakness, shortness of breath, skin redness, repeated refusal, or a sudden decline in ADLs should reach licensed staff. Documentation then records facts and notification.

content_body:  
Scenario setup: Resident G can wash the face and upper body after supplies are set up. The care plan encourages self-care and use of a long-handled sponge. Resident G says, "Please just do everything. It is faster when staff do it." You notice the resident looks tired but is alert, agrees to try after you explain the plan, and wants privacy. Later, while preparing for transfer to the wheelchair, you notice the wheelchair cushion is not in place and one footrest is loose.

Decision point 1 — ADL support: What is the best first response during bathing and grooming?

A. Close the curtain, place supplies within reach, offer a calm cue such as, "You wash the parts you can reach, and I will help with the rest," and allow extra time.  
B. Complete the whole bath without involving the resident because the schedule is busy.  
C. Leave the resident alone with supplies and return later, even though the care plan calls for setup and assistance.  
D. Tell the resident that refusing to try will make the team disappointed.

Correct feedback: Choice A supports the care plan, privacy, participation, dignity, and realistic help.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices B, C, and D either take over function, abandon needed support, or shame the resident. Restorative care uses encouragement, not pressure or neglect.  
Remediation prompt: Review Lesson Segment 2. Select the response that provides setup, respects pace, and assists only with the parts the resident cannot safely complete.

Decision point 2 — device and mobility concern: Before transfer, the wheelchair cushion is missing and one footrest is loose. What should you do?

A. Transfer anyway and fix the equipment after the resident is seated.  
B. Remove the footrest permanently and tell the resident not to worry about it.  
C. Stop the transfer setup, keep the resident safe, follow facility process to obtain safe equipment or assistance, and report the device concern.  
D. Ask the resident to stand longer while you search the room for the cushion.

Correct feedback: Choice C protects safety and keeps the CNA within role. Device problems should be corrected through facility procedure and reported.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, B, and D increase fall, positioning, or skin risk and may exceed CNA authority. A missing cushion or loose footrest is not a minor inconvenience when it affects safe mobility.  
Remediation prompt: Review Lesson Segments 2 and 3. Choose the action that prevents a rushed transfer and reports unsafe equipment.

Decision point 3 — documentation and team communication: After care, Resident G washed the face and upper body with setup, used the long-handled sponge with two verbal cues, reported fatigue, and the wheelchair issue was reported to the nurse. Which note is best?

A. "Resident was lazy and did not want to help; wheelchair was bad."  
B. "Resident probably needs more therapy because bathing is too hard."  
C. "Resident refused all care, but staff completed it."  
D. "Resident washed face and upper body after setup; used long-handled sponge with two verbal cues; stated, 'I am tired'; wheelchair cushion missing and loose footrest reported to nurse."

Correct feedback: Choice D is factual, includes assistance level, resident statement, device concern, and notification.  
Unsafe-choice feedback: Choices A, B, and C use judgmental language, diagnose or interpret, or contradict the facts. Restorative documentation should help the team without labeling the resident.  
Remediation prompt: Review Lesson Segment 4. Select the note that describes what was done, how the resident responded, and who was notified.
