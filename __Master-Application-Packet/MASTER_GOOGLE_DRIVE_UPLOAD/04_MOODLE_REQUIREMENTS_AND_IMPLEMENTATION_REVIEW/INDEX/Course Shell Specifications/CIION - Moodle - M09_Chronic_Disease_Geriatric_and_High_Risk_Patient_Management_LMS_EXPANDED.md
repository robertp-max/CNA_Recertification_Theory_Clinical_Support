## Behavioral Learning Objectives

By the end of this 3 contact-hour module, the learner will be able to:

- Explain chronic disease coordination priorities for patients with multiple conditions, unstable symptoms, functional limitations, and social barriers.
- Assess geriatric syndromes, including falls, frailty, cognition changes, nutrition risk, polypharmacy, and caregiver strain, as case-management factors.
- Prioritize high-risk markers that require more frequent monitoring, faster follow-up, team communication, or escalation.
- Document assessment findings, patient education, teach-back response, team contacts, unresolved barriers, and next steps in audit-ready nursing language.
- Coordinate individualized care planning with the patient, caregiver, provider, pharmacist, rehabilitation team, social work, and community resources.
- Escalate changes in condition, unsafe medication patterns, worsening cognition, repeated falls, or caregiver breakdown through appropriate clinical and supervisory pathways.
- Evaluate whether the care plan remains realistic, measurable, patient-centered, and responsive to new risk information.

content_body: High-risk case management is not defined by age alone or by the number of diagnoses listed in a record. A patient becomes high risk when clinical instability, functional limits, medication burden, cognition changes, caregiver strain, social barriers, or recent utilization create a realistic chance that the current plan will fail without coordinated follow-up. The RN case manager helps make those risks visible, organizes communication across the team, and keeps the plan connected to what the patient can actually understand and do.

In chronic disease coordination, the RN case manager watches for patterns: repeated shortness of breath in a person with heart failure, inconsistent blood glucose monitoring in a person with diabetes, missed inhaler use in a person with chronic lung disease, uncontrolled pain limiting mobility, or delayed follow-up after a medication change. The RN does not replace the prescriber, pharmacist, therapist, social worker, or caregiver. Instead, the RN uses assessment findings to identify risk, educate using clear language, confirm understanding through teach-back, coordinate the appropriate partner, and document the follow-up plan.

Geriatric case management adds another layer. Older adults may have falls, frailty, dehydration, weight loss, delirium risk, memory changes, sensory impairment, transportation barriers, and limited reserve after even a minor illness. Polypharmacy can increase dizziness, confusion, bleeding risk, hypoglycemia, constipation, urinary symptoms, or sedation. A medication list that looks routine on paper may become unsafe when the patient cannot read labels, takes duplicate medications, uses several pharmacies, or relies on an exhausted caregiver.

This module asks you to think like an RN case manager responsible for coordination intensity. You will decide which risks are highest priority, which team members should be involved, what education must be checked with teach-back, what monitoring schedule is reasonable, and when escalation is required. Your written work should show a clear connection between finding, risk, action, responsible person, time frame, and evidence of patient or caregiver response.

moodle_description: Teaches how RN case managers connect chronic disease findings to monitoring, education, team communication, and individualized follow-up.

completion_tracking: View required

restrict_access: Available after M09_PAGE_OVERVIEW is viewed

gradebook_setting: Not graded

learner_instructions: Read the lesson segment and identify two chronic disease findings that would change follow-up timing. Notice how each finding connects to patient education, monitoring, documentation, and team communication.

content_body: Chronic disease coordination begins with a practical question: what could get worse before the next scheduled contact, and what can the care team do now to reduce that risk? A patient with heart failure who reports increased swelling, decreased activity tolerance, and confusion about diuretic timing needs a different follow-up plan than a stable patient who understands daily weights and has a scheduled cardiology visit. A patient with diabetes who skips meals, has inconsistent glucose testing, and takes a sulfonylurea may need medication-safety communication and nutrition support before the next routine call. A patient with chronic lung disease who cannot demonstrate inhaler technique may need teaching, respiratory therapy input, and a symptom action plan that is understandable at home.

The RN case manager should organize chronic disease information into usable categories: current symptoms, recent changes, medication use, monitoring ability, self-management knowledge, access barriers, caregiver support, and escalation triggers. A symptom without a time frame is difficult to act on. A medication discrepancy without a next step remains a risk. A patient goal without a barrier assessment may be unrealistic. Strong coordination turns scattered facts into a plan that says who will do what, by when, and how the result will be checked.

Teaching should be specific to the patient's condition and readiness. "Call if you feel worse" is less useful than reviewing two or three warning signs and asking the patient or caregiver to repeat what they would do. Teach-back is not a test of the patient; it is a test of whether the explanation worked. If the patient cannot explain when to call the provider, how to use a monitoring tool, or what medication change occurred, the RN adapts the explanation, uses plain language, involves the caregiver if appropriate, and documents the response.

The RN case manager also protects scope boundaries. Coordination may include notifying a provider about symptoms, requesting order clarification, referring to pharmacy for medication review, asking social work to address transportation or food access, and arranging therapy input for functional decline. It does not include independently changing prescriptions, diagnosing a new condition, promising payer coverage, or substituting a checklist for nursing judgment.

moodle_description: Explains how geriatric syndromes affect case-management risk, care planning, communication, and escalation.

completion_tracking: View required

restrict_access: Available after M09_BOOK_SEGMENT_1_CHRONIC_DISEASE_COORDINATION is viewed

gradebook_setting: Not graded

learner_instructions: Read the segment and list the geriatric syndromes that can make a chronic disease plan fail even when the diagnosis-specific instructions appear correct.

content_body: Geriatric syndromes often overlap and can quickly undermine a care plan. Falls may reflect unsafe footwear, orthostatic symptoms, sedating medication, poor lighting, urinary urgency, neuropathy, pain, muscle weakness, or cognitive impairment. Frailty can make a routine infection or missed meal more serious. Cognitive change may be gradual dementia, acute delirium, medication effect, depression, sensory impairment, or an unrecognized medical problem. The RN case manager does not diagnose these conditions independently, but does assess observable findings, reports, safety concerns, and the need for timely provider or team follow-up.

Falls require more than a reminder to be careful. The RN case manager should ask what happened before the fall, whether injury occurred, whether the patient felt dizzy or weak, what medications were taken, whether assistive devices were used, who was present, and what changed afterward. The plan may include provider notification, physical therapy or occupational therapy referral according to policy, medication review, equipment follow-up, environmental safety teaching, caregiver instruction, and a specific reassessment time frame.

Cognition affects every part of self-management. A patient may nod during teaching and still be unable to organize medications, recognize worsening symptoms, or keep appointments. Teach-back is especially important when memory, attention, hearing, vision, or language barriers are present. If a caregiver manages medications or appointments, the RN should verify caregiver availability and capacity rather than assuming support exists. Caregiver strain can show up as missed calls, frustration, medication errors, inability to attend visits, or statements that the workload is no longer manageable.

Functional status should be tied to patient goals. A patient who wants to remain at home may need mobility support, meal access, medication organization, and urgent clarification of who can respond if symptoms worsen. The RN case manager should document both the risk and the plan: what was assessed, which team member was contacted, what education was provided, how the patient or caregiver responded, and when follow-up will occur.

moodle_description: Teaches medication-risk recognition, reconciliation questions, pharmacy/provider communication, and documentation expectations for high-risk patients.

completion_tracking: View required

restrict_access: Available after M09_BOOK_SEGMENT_2_GERIATRIC_SYNDROMES is viewed

gradebook_setting: Not graded

learner_instructions: Read the segment and identify medication findings that should trigger same-day communication rather than routine monitoring.

content_body: Polypharmacy is more than a medication count. Risk increases when a patient has duplicate therapies, recent medication changes, high-alert medications, side effects that resemble disease progression, multiple prescribers, multiple pharmacies, low health literacy, affordability barriers, or difficulty opening containers and reading labels. In case management, the RN's role is to compare available medication information, identify discrepancies or safety concerns, teach within scope, coordinate review, and escalate when findings suggest harm or urgent risk.

Medication reconciliation questions should be concrete. What medications are actually in the home? Which ones were stopped after the last visit or hospitalization? Does the patient take over-the-counter products, supplements, insulin, inhalers, patches, eye drops, or as-needed medications? Can the patient explain when and why each medication is used? Are there symptoms such as dizziness, falls, confusion, bleeding, low blood sugar, constipation, dehydration, or excessive sleepiness? Is cost causing skipped doses? Does the caregiver understand the schedule?

The RN case manager should not quietly "clean up" an unclear medication list without an authorized process. If the discharge list conflicts with what the patient is taking, if a high-risk medication appears duplicated, if the patient has symptoms that may be medication-related, or if the patient cannot safely administer the regimen, the RN coordinates provider or pharmacist review according to policy. The documentation should show the discrepancy, the potential safety concern, who was contacted, the response if received, interim safety teaching within scope, and the follow-up plan.

Medication education works best when it is connected to daily routines and warning signs. For example, a patient with heart failure may need to explain when they weigh themselves, what weight change they should report if ordered in the care plan, and whom they will call. A patient taking insulin may need to describe meal timing, glucose monitoring, and what symptoms require help. A caregiver managing pill boxes may need confirmation that new orders have been incorporated and old instructions removed.

learner_visible_title: Lesson Segment 4: High-Risk Markers, Monitoring Frequency, and Escalation

moodle_description: Explains how to identify high-risk markers, match follow-up timing to acuity, and escalate unresolved safety concerns.

completion_tracking: View required

restrict_access: Available after M09_BOOK_SEGMENT_3_POLYPHARMACY_AND_MEDICATION_SAFETY is viewed

gradebook_setting: Not graded

learner_instructions: Read the segment and separate same-day escalation markers from findings that can be managed through routine planned follow-up.

content_body: High-risk markers tell the RN case manager that the current plan may not be strong enough. Examples include recent hospitalization, emergency department use, multiple chronic diseases with worsening symptoms, medication discrepancies, repeated falls, new or worsening confusion, missed appointments, caregiver exhaustion, food or transportation insecurity, inability to obtain equipment, unstable housing, poor understanding of warning signs, and lack of timely provider follow-up. No single marker automatically defines the whole plan, but clusters of markers should increase coordination intensity.

Follow-up timing should be tied to acuity and uncertainty. Same-day escalation may be needed for a concerning change in condition, unsafe medication use, repeated falls with possible injury, severe caregiver breakdown, inability to obtain a critical medication, or symptoms that the care plan identifies as urgent. Next-day or short-interval follow-up may fit a patient who is stable at the moment but has unresolved barriers, poor teach-back, or a newly changed regimen. Routine follow-up may be appropriate when the patient is stable, understands the plan, has support, and has no unresolved safety concern.

Escalation is not failure; it is a nursing safety action. The RN case manager escalates when the risk exceeds what can be managed through routine education or coordination. Escalation may go to the provider, supervisor, pharmacist, home health clinician, therapist, social worker, emergency pathway, or another approved channel depending on the finding and organizational policy. The communication should be concise: what changed, why it matters, what action is requested, how urgent the issue is, what the patient or caregiver has been told, and what follow-up remains pending.

Documentation closes the loop. A high-risk note should not simply say "will monitor." It should describe the finding, nursing interpretation of risk within scope, patient or caregiver response, education provided, contacts made, pending replies, time frame for reassessment, and criteria for further escalation. If the RN cannot reach the patient or provider, the failed contact and next action should be visible in the record.

moodle_description: Teaches how to build and evaluate a patient-centered high-risk care plan with measurable goals, team roles, teach-back, and monitoring.

completion_tracking: View required

restrict_access: Available after M09_BOOK_SEGMENT_4_HIGH_RISK_MARKERS_AND_ESCALATION is viewed

gradebook_setting: Not graded

learner_instructions: Read the segment and draft one example of a measurable care coordination goal for a fictional high-risk patient.

content_body: An individualized high-risk care plan translates assessment findings into coordinated actions. The plan should identify the patient's priorities, the top safety risks, the teaching focus, the monitoring method, the follow-up interval, the team partners, and the criteria for escalation. A plan that ignores the patient's daily life may look complete but fail quickly. For example, a low-sodium diet plan may not work if the patient depends on delivered meals, cannot shop, or does not understand food labels. A medication plan may fail if the patient cannot afford refills or has two conflicting discharge lists.

Measurable goals make evaluation possible. Instead of writing that the patient will "manage chronic disease," the RN case manager can document that the patient or caregiver will explain three symptom triggers for calling the provider, demonstrate the monitoring routine, identify who fills the medication organizer, and confirm the next appointment date. The goal should be realistic for the patient's cognition, language, culture, support system, and access to resources.

Team communication keeps the plan from resting on one person. The provider may need symptom or medication clarification. The pharmacist may need to review duplications or adverse-effect concerns. Physical or occupational therapy may address mobility, falls, energy conservation, or equipment needs. Social work may address transportation, food insecurity, caregiver support, benefits, or community services. The caregiver may need written instructions, respite resources, or a clearer role. The patient remains central; coordination should support the patient's informed participation whenever possible.

Evaluation asks whether the plan is working. Did the patient obtain the medication? Did teach-back improve after re-education? Did the caregiver confirm the follow-up appointment? Did the fall-risk referral occur? Did symptoms improve, worsen, or remain uncertain? Did a provider respond to the escalation? The RN case manager updates the plan when new information changes the risk level. Clear documentation lets another qualified reviewer understand the clinical reason for the updated priority and the next planned action.

learner_visible_title: Case Scenario: High-Risk Chronic Disease and Geriatric Coordination

moodle_description: Branching case in which the learner prioritizes risks, selects coordination actions, and responds to unsafe choices through remediation.

completion_tracking: Require completion of all decision points and review of feedback

restrict_access: Available after all five M09 book pages are viewed

gradebook_setting: Completion only or practice grade according to provider policy

learner_instructions: Work through each decision point. Select the safest RN case-management action for the fictional scenario. If you choose an unsafe or incomplete action, read the feedback and remediation prompt before continuing.

content_body: Scenario setup: A fictional 79-year-old patient has heart failure, type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease, neuropathy, mild memory difficulty, and two falls in the past month. The patient was recently discharged after shortness of breath and medication changes. The caregiver is an adult child who works full time and reports feeling overwhelmed. The patient has missed one follow-up appointment because transportation fell through. The medication list includes recent dose changes, duplicate bottles of an older diuretic, a sulfonylurea, a blood pressure medication, and several over-the-counter products. During a phone check, the patient says, "I get dizzy when I stand, and I do not know which water pill is the new one." The caregiver says the patient has been eating poorly and forgot to check glucose twice this week.

Decision point 1: What is the best initial RN case-management priority?

Choice A: Treat the missed appointment as the only issue and schedule a routine reminder call for next month.

Choice B: Identify immediate safety risks, clarify symptoms and medication discrepancies, assess fall/injury concerns, and plan same-day provider or pharmacist communication according to policy.

Choice C: Tell the caregiver to throw away any medication bottles that look old and restart the discharge list without contacting the provider or pharmacist.

Correct-path feedback: Choice B is the safest path. The patient has dizziness, falls, medication confusion, poor intake, missed monitoring, and caregiver strain. These combined markers call for immediate risk assessment, medication-safety coordination, and timely communication with the appropriate clinical partner.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A delays action despite multiple high-risk markers. Choice C creates a provider-only medication decision and could cause harm. The RN case manager should not independently direct medication changes when the list is unclear.

Remediation prompt: Review which findings make this case higher risk than a routine missed appointment. Write one sentence naming the safety concern and one sentence naming the team partner you would contact.

Decision point 2: Which teaching approach best supports safe self-management?

Choice A: Provide a long printed packet covering every diagnosis and ask the patient to read it before the next call.

Choice B: Tell the caregiver that teaching is unnecessary because the caregiver is responsible for the plan.

Choice C: Focus on two or three priority warning signs, use plain language, ask the patient and caregiver to explain the plan back, and document what they understood or could not explain.

Correct-path feedback: Choice C is correct. Teach-back checks whether the explanation worked and helps the RN adapt teaching for memory, literacy, caregiver availability, and condition-specific warning signs.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A may overwhelm the learner and does not verify understanding. Choice B removes the patient from the plan and ignores the need to assess caregiver capacity and consent-aware involvement.

Remediation prompt: Rewrite one warning-sign instruction in plain language and add a teach-back question that would show whether the patient or caregiver can use it.

Decision point 3: Which care-plan update is most complete?

Choice A: Document "high risk" and continue to monitor as needed.

Choice B: Close the case because the patient has a caregiver and the medication issue can wait for the next appointment.

Choice C: Focus only on transportation because that was the reason for the missed appointment.

Choice D: Document the risk cluster, patient/caregiver responses, medication discrepancy, fall history, education and teach-back result, team contacts, escalation time frame, and next follow-up date.

Correct-path feedback: Choice D is correct. It links findings to coordination actions and makes the next steps visible for review. It also captures evidence of teaching, communication, unresolved barriers, and follow-up timing.

Unsafe-choice feedback: Choice A is too vague to support continuity. Choice B ignores active safety risks. Choice C addresses one barrier but misses medication safety, falls, cognition, nutrition, monitoring, and caregiver strain.

Remediation prompt: List three elements that must appear in the note so another RN can understand what was known, what was done, and what remains pending.

Completion condition: Learner completes all three decision points, opens all feedback screens, and responds to remediation prompts when presented.
